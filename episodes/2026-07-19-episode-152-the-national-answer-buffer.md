# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 152
**Title:** The National Answer Buffer
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the Prime Minister announced that the government would introduce a National Answer Buffer.

It was designed to sit between questions and answers.

Communications said this would restore confidence in the journey from uncertainty to response.

Policy said the journey had not yet been scoped.

Legal asked whether a buffer could be subpoenaed.

Treasury asked whether unanswered questions could be amortised.

The Cabinet Secretary convened an emergency meeting to determine whether the buffer was a process, a platform, or a holding position with branding.

Digital said it could be all three if nobody asked for the login.

The junior adviser asked whether the public might prefer the answers to the buffer.

Communications said the public preferred reassurance, and reassurance worked best when it had somewhere to stand.

Policy said several answers were being prepared.

Legal said prepared implied proximity.

Treasury asked whether proximity had a unit cost.

The Prime Minister said the country deserved timely answers, delivered at the responsible moment after the appropriate amount of timeliness had been verified.

The Cabinet Secretary wrote "RESPONSIVE DELAY" on the whiteboard.

Everyone agreed it was the closest government had come to movement all week.

## ACT ONE

By 08:30, departments had been instructed to classify all incoming questions by answer readiness.

Transport reported that several transport questions were moving in the right direction, although the right direction was subject to consultation.

Health said some answers were clinically adjacent.

Education said it had an answer pilot, a pilot answer, and a stakeholder pack explaining the difference.

The Home Office said its answers were robust but not currently portable.

Digital unveiled a dashboard showing questions entering the buffer and emerging as "response candidates."

Communications asked whether candidate sounded too electoral.

Legal asked whether response sounded too final.

Treasury asked whether emerging required lighting.

The Cabinet Secretary proposed a neutral scale:

QUESTION RECEIVED.

ANSWER AWARE.

RESPONSE FORMING.

PUBLIC WORDS PENDING.

The junior adviser asked what happened if a question reached the end of the scale.

Policy said it would be routed into lessons learned.

Communications said that sounded admirably circular.

## ACT TWO

At 10:00, the Prime Minister rehearsed the line for the morning broadcasters.

"We are answering the questions people are asking."

Legal said answering was risky.

Policy said people was broad.

Communications said asking created pressure.

The revised line became: "We are engaging with the issues being raised."

Treasury said engaging might imply staff time.

Digital suggested: "The system is aware of active public interest."

The Cabinet Secretary said it had dignity.

The junior adviser said it sounded like an out-of-office reply with a flag on it.

Communications asked whether the flag could be made more hopeful.

Meanwhile, a reporter had emailed a simple question: when would the first buffered answer be released?

Policy classified it as operational.

Legal classified it as chronological.

Treasury classified it as potentially expensive.

Digital classified it as test data.

The Cabinet Secretary ruled it should be placed in the buffer immediately to demonstrate confidence in the buffer.

The dashboard changed to:

FIRST QUESTION: RECEIVED.

FIRST ANSWER: IN PRINCIPLE.

BUFFER HEALTH: REASSURING.

Communications said this proved the system was working.

The junior adviser said it proved the system could hold one question hostage.

## ACT THREE

At midday, a briefing pack accidentally included a tab named "Actual Answers."

The room froze.

Communications asked who had authorised the plural.

Policy said the tab was illustrative.

Legal said illustrative answers were still capable of becoming discoverable.

Treasury asked whether deleting the tab would save money retrospectively.

The Prime Minister asked whether any answer was safe enough to release.

Policy offered: "We recognise the importance of the issue."

Communications said that was not an answer, which made it promising.

Legal said recognise was acceptable if nobody inferred eyesight.

Digital said it could be pushed to the dashboard as an "answer-shaped update."

The Cabinet Secretary approved the phrase.

The junior adviser asked whether answer-shaped updates were different from not answering.

Communications said yes, because one had shape.

Policy added that shape was a key component of public confidence.

Treasury asked whether confidence could be provided in smaller portions.

The Prime Minister said the nation needed to see that questions were being treated with seriousness, discipline, and a carefully managed absence of improvisation.

The Cabinet Secretary underlined absence.

## CLIMAX

The press conference began at 15:00.

Behind the Prime Minister, the screen displayed:

THE NATIONAL ANSWER BUFFER

STATUS: ACTIVE

QUESTIONS: BEING HELD RESPONSIBLY

ANSWERS: APPROACHING READINESS

The Prime Minister said: "Today we are making sure that the public's questions receive the structured attention they deserve."

A reporter asked whether that meant an answer today.

Communications signalled "structured."

Policy signalled "attention."

Legal signalled "not today."

The Prime Minister said: "It means the question has entered a strengthened response environment."

Another reporter asked how long the buffer would hold it.

Digital refreshed the dashboard.

It displayed:

QUESTION CONFIDENCE: HIGH.

ANSWER CONFIDENCE: DEVELOPING.

RESPONSE TEMPERATURE: STABLE.

PUBLIC RELEASE: UNDER ACTIVE CARE.

The reporter asked whether under active care meant delayed.

Treasury whispered that delayed was the most affordable form of delivery.

The Cabinet Secretary stepped forward and said the buffer represented a mature democracy's commitment to ensuring that answers did not rush ahead of the processes designed to make them answerable.

The junior adviser said the government had invented a queue for sentences.

Communications said queues were popular when described as fairness.

The Prime Minister concluded that the National Answer Buffer would provide the country with clarity about where clarity would eventually be located.

## TAG

By evening, every department had submitted its most difficult questions to the buffer.

Digital added a "gentle pulsing" animation to show democratic responsiveness.

Legal asked whether pulsing implied life.

Treasury asked whether life implied maintenance.
