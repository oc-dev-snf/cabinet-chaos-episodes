# Cabinet Chaos — Episode 090: The National Alignment Exercise

*Date: 2026-05-12*

## COLD OPEN
At 7:00 a.m., the government announces a “minor scheduling update” that requires every public service to hold a one-hour meeting before opening, to discuss how to reduce meetings.

By 7:12, the Department of Administrative Efficiency has created a forty-three page “Conversation Readiness Deck” with twelve mandatory icebreakers and a glossary defining “briefly” as “under nine minutes.”

A spokesperson insists this is not bureaucracy. “This is agility with stationery.”

## ACT ONE — THE ALIGNMENT BRIEFING
The Prime Minister unveils the National Alignment Exercise from a podium flanked by three identical banners reading *DELIVERABLES*.

“People say government is too complicated,” they begin. “So we are simplifying everything into a clear four-step framework.”

A reporter asks for the four steps.

“Step one,” says the PM, “is drafting a consultation paper on steps.”

“Step two?”

“Reviewing feedback on whether four is the right number.”

“Step three?”

“Piloting a temporary three-step model in two regions.”

“Step four?”

“Publishing lessons learned from the pilot to inform step finalization.”

The room nods as if this is wildly pragmatic.

## ACT TWO — MINISTRY PILOT DAY
Across government, ministries race to show they are “aligned.”

- The Transport Ministry repaints every bus lane with the word *SYNERGY*.
- The Health Ministry launches a portal where patients can upload symptoms directly into a strategy map.
- The Education Ministry renames homework “Independent Alignment Practice.”

Civil servants discover the quickest path to compliance: if a project has no clear purpose, call it “cross-cutting.”

An internal memo leaks:

> **Subject:** Clarification on alignment metrics  
> “All teams must report at least one measurable outcome by Friday. If no outcome exists, report momentum.”

Momentum, by 4 p.m., becomes the strongest-performing metric in government history.

## ACT THREE — THE PUBLIC CONSULTATION
A town hall is staged for citizen feedback.

Moderator: “What would you like to see improved?”

Citizen #1: “Shorter wait times.”

Moderator: “Excellent. We’ll log that under *aspirational throughput*.”

Citizen #2: “Can you fix the online forms?”

Moderator: “Powerful insight. That’s *digital dignity*.”

Citizen #3: “Can I just talk to a human?”

Moderator (checking script): “Great question. We have a chatbot that can explain why that’s not currently possible.”

The chatbot opens with: “Thank you for your patience during this exciting transition period.”

## ACT FOUR — CRISIS COMMS
At dusk, ministers hold a joint press conference to reassure the nation that services remain fully functional.

A journalist asks why the passport office briefly replaced all status updates with “in progress (strategically).”

A minister replies, “Transparency is important. We wanted people to know progress was not accidental.”

Another reporter asks whether the exercise produced tangible improvements.

“Yes,” says the Cabinet Office lead, “we’ve reduced duplicated effort by creating one central form to request permission to stop duplicating effort.”

The form is eighteen pages.

## CLIMAX — THE AUDIT
An independent auditor presents findings live on television:

1. Meeting hours increased 61%.
2. Slide production increased 214%.
3. Use of the phrase “delivery architecture” increased beyond measurable range.
4. One actual policy change succeeded because a junior clerk skipped three approvals by mistake.

The PM calls the results “encouraging proof of concept.”

When asked what happens next, they announce Phase Two: the National Alignment Exercise Review Taskforce.

Its first meeting is scheduled for three weeks, pending agreement on the agenda format.

## TAG
Late night in the Cabinet building: a cleaner finds a forgotten whiteboard reading:

**“WHAT DO PEOPLE NEED?”**

Underneath, in small handwriting:

**“Probably less of this.”**

The cleaner circles it, adds three stars, and leaves.
