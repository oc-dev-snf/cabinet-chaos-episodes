# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 154
**Title:** The National Timetable of Intentions
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the Prime Minister announced the National Timetable of Intentions.

It would not say when things would happen.

It would say when the government intended to consider saying when things might happen.

Communications said this represented a major step forward in public-facing chronology.

Policy said chronology had been underused as a delivery-adjacent asset.

Legal asked whether an intention with a timetable was more legally alive than an intention without one.

Treasury asked whether time could be reallocated from departments that were not using it efficiently.

The Cabinet Secretary convened an emergency meeting to decide whether the timetable should contain dates, windows, phases, or atmospheres.

Digital said it could build a portal where citizens selected a policy and received a calming indication of temporal seriousness.

The junior adviser asked whether the portal would include actual dates.

Communications said actual dates could panic the timetable.

Policy added that an undated timetable was more flexible and therefore more responsible.

The Prime Minister said the country deserved certainty about the government's commitment to becoming more specific at the right time.

The Cabinet Secretary wrote "SCHEDULED INTENTION" on the whiteboard.

Everyone agreed it sounded nearly accountable.

## ACT ONE

By 08:30, departments had been instructed to submit their most urgent intentions for timetabling.

Transport said several projects were now in the early stages of future sequencing.

Health said its priorities had a strong sense of eventuality.

Education said reforms would be placed in a learning-aware order.

The Home Office said its intentions were operationally firm but chronologically sensitive.

Digital unveiled a dashboard with four columns:

INTENTION RECEIVED.

TIMETABLE AWARE.

DATE-SENSITIVE.

SPECIFICITY PENDING.

Communications liked specificity pending because it sounded careful rather than evasive.

Legal asked whether pending could be interpreted as a promise to stop pending.

Treasury asked whether pending required storage.

The Cabinet Secretary proposed that every date should be replaced with a "responsible timing indicator" until departments had completed a confidence check.

The junior adviser asked what the indicator would indicate.

Policy said it would indicate that indication was underway.

Communications said that was exactly the kind of progress people could understand, provided nobody explained it.

## ACT TWO

At 10:00, the Prime Minister rehearsed the line for the morning broadcasters.

"We are publishing a timetable."

Legal said publishing was exposure.

Policy said timetable implied tables.

Treasury asked whether tables were capital expenditure.

Communications said the line needed firmness without furniture.

The revised version became: "We are setting out a clear framework for future timing clarity."

Digital suggested: "The public will be able to track the system's intention maturity."

The Cabinet Secretary said it had the reassuring feel of a password reset email.

The junior adviser said people might ask when something would happen.

Communications said when was an aggressive opening.

Meanwhile, a department tried to submit a real date by mistake.

The room fell silent.

Policy asked whether the date had been exposed to stakeholder weather.

Legal asked whether it had escape clauses.

Treasury asked whether it fell in the current spending period and, if so, could it be moved somewhere less vivid.

Communications asked whether the date could be blurred into "early-ish".

The Cabinet Secretary approved "early-ish" as long as the hyphen did not imply coordination.

## ACT THREE

At midday, the first draft of the timetable arrived.

It contained no dates, three quarters, two horizons, one pathway, and a footnote defining soon as "subject to responsible proximity".

Communications said it was bold.

Policy said it gave the public a sense of movement without the burden of location.

Legal said the footnote was doing valuable constitutional work.

Treasury asked whether horizons could be shared across departments to reduce duplication.

Digital said the dashboard was already assigning colours to intentions based on readiness.

Green meant "confidently not yet".

Amber meant "actively becoming".

Blue meant "publicly calm".

The junior adviser asked what red meant.

Digital said red had been removed after Legal said it looked too discoverable.

The Prime Minister asked whether one small policy could be placed on a date to show ambition.

Policy suggested the review of ministerial envelope sizes.

Communications said envelopes were low risk unless opened.

Legal said opened envelopes created contents.

Treasury asked whether contents were funded.

The Cabinet Secretary moved the envelope review to "later, with purpose".

Everyone agreed purpose did a lot of work for a word with no calendar invitation.

## CLIMAX

The afternoon press conference opened with a large screen showing:

THE NATIONAL TIMETABLE OF INTENTIONS

STATUS: LAUNCHED

DATES: BEING HANDLED RESPONSIBLY

INTENTIONS: IN ORDER

SPECIFICITY: APPROACHING

The Prime Minister said: "Today we are giving people a clearer sense of when they can expect clarity about timing."

A reporter asked whether any policy had a deadline.

Communications signalled "clarity".

Policy signalled "sense".

Legal signalled "do not discover time".

The Prime Minister said: "Several priorities are now moving through a disciplined process toward an appropriate timing position."

Another reporter asked whether that meant delayed.

Treasury whispered that delayed was just punctual in a cheaper year.

Digital refreshed the dashboard.

It displayed:

PUBLIC EXPECTATION: MANAGED.

TIME CONFIDENCE: STABLE.

DELIVERY DATE: UNDER CONSIDERATION.

FORWARD LOOK: FORWARD.

The reporter asked when the next update would be.

The Cabinet Secretary stepped forward and explained that updates would be issued according to the timetable once the timetable had completed its own timing review.

The junior adviser said the government had scheduled a diary reminder to buy a diary.

Communications said diaries demonstrated preparedness.

The Prime Minister concluded that the National Timetable of Intentions would ensure the country never again had to wonder whether the government intended to intend something on purpose.

## TAG

By evening, every department had requested an extension to submit its timing indicator.

The dashboard added a new column: EVENTUALLY VISIBLE.
