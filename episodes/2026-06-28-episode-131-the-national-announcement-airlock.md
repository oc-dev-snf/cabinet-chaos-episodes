# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 131
**Title:** The National Announcement Airlock
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the government discovered that announcements were escaping before they had been fully announced.

The problem began with a morning line about a responsible package of measures. Nobody knew which package, what measures, or whether responsibility had signed off on being included. By breakfast, three departments had briefed four newspapers that the package was bold, careful, targeted, universal, temporary, permanent, and subject to a review that had not yet been commissioned.

The Prime Minister asked why the announcement was already everywhere.

Communications said it had not been announced. It had merely become atmospherically available.

The Cabinet Secretary proposed the National Announcement Airlock: a controlled holding environment where policies could be pressurised, de-risked, and checked for loose adjectives before entering public circulation.

Legal asked whether the airlock would prevent leaks.

"No," said Communications, "but it gives leaks somewhere official to happen."

## ACT ONE

The first airlock protocol required every announcement to pass through three chambers: intention, wording, and survivability.

Policy liked intention. It sounded early enough to be flexible. Treasury liked survivability, because it implied some announcements might not survive, saving money. Delivery asked whether wording came before intention in practice. The room agreed not to minute that.

A whiteboard was divided into categories: ready to announce, nearly ready to announce, announced by accident, and announced in spirit. Strategy suggested "pre-public clarity zone" for anything that had already been denied on television. Legal added a red box marked "Do Not Enthuse."

The Minister for Public Reassurance wanted the airlock to have a dashboard. Digital produced one immediately. It showed a sealed door, a progress bar, and a warning light labelled "Narrative Pressure."

Within minutes, Narrative Pressure reached severe.

Communications blamed the weekend papers. The weekend papers blamed a senior source. The senior source blamed a background conversation. The background conversation could not be reached for comment.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments began sending announcements to the airlock in bulk.

Transport submitted a rail update with no trains in it. Health submitted a plan to reduce waiting by improving the experience of waiting. Education submitted a curriculum clarification that clarified it would be clarified after consultation. The Environment department asked whether fresh air entering the airlock counted toward targets.

The airlock filled with statements beginning "We are clear that" and ending with "in due course." A junior official tried to remove duplicate phrases and accidentally reduced a twelve-page briefing to the sentence "We are listening." Communications said this was punchy. Policy said it was legally weightless. Treasury asked whether listening had a unit cost.

Meanwhile, the dashboard introduced a new status: "Awaiting Responsible Release." Nobody remembered approving it, which made it feel authentic.

The Prime Minister wanted one announcement ready by lunchtime. The Cabinet Secretary asked which one.

"The one about the airlock," said the Prime Minister.

This caused concern, because announcing the airlock required the airlock to clear its own announcement, creating what Legal described as a procedural echo.

Digital said an echo could be handled with a loading screen.

## ACT THREE

At 12:15, the Announcement Airlock held its first formal release meeting.

The draft line read: "The government is today launching a new process to ensure the public receives announcements only when they are ready, accurate, and properly tested."

Legal crossed out "ready." Treasury crossed out "launching." Delivery crossed out "process" because the process had no delivery date. Communications replaced "accurate" with "clear" after deciding accuracy implied a standard that could be checked.

The revised line read: "The government is today setting out clear movement toward a more disciplined public information environment."

Everyone disliked it less than the alternatives.

A press officer asked whether the airlock would slow down urgent announcements. Strategy said it would accelerate certainty. Delivery asked whether certainty was faster than information. Communications said it depended on the outlet.

The dashboard flashed "Release Candidate Stable." Then it flashed "Release Candidate Briefed." Then it flashed "Release Candidate Denied."

Somewhere in the building, a phone began ringing with the confidence of a problem that already had a quote.

## CLIMAX

The press conference began with the spokesperson standing beside a graphic of a sealed door.

A reporter asked whether the new airlock existed because previous announcements had been chaotic. The spokesperson said previous announcements had demonstrated the need for a more structured journey from intention to public understanding. Another reporter asked whether the airlock announcement had itself leaked. The spokesperson said early awareness should not be confused with premature communication.

Then the dashboard, accidentally visible on the media feed, updated to "Announcement Escaped."

Communications lunged for the laptop. Digital said the feed was on a seven-second delay, which briefly reassured everyone until Legal pointed out that the seven seconds had already happened.

The spokesperson continued: "This reform ensures that every announcement will be carefully held until the appropriate moment."

At that exact moment, every department received an automated email: "Your announcement has left the airlock."

Phones lit up around the room. The sealed-door graphic changed to an open-door graphic. The warning light turned green, then amber, then a small apologetic grey.

The Prime Minister watched the feed and asked whether grey was good.

Strategy said grey meant mature.

## TAG

By evening, the National Announcement Airlock had been upgraded to include a pre-airlock lobby, a post-airlock interpretation suite, and a holding area for phrases that sounded decisive but were not yet funded.

Departments were instructed to submit all future announcements with a pressure rating, a denial plan, and a plain-English summary of why plain English would be finalised separately.

The Cabinet Secretary circulated new guidance: nothing was to be announced until it had been announced internally that it was safe to announce that an announcement was under consideration.

The final shot showed the airlock dashboard at midnight.

Inside the chamber sat one sentence, perfectly sealed:

"Further details will follow."

The pressure gauge climbed.

**END**
