# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 133
**Title:** The National Readiness Lane
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the government announced internally that the country was ready for a new phase, pending confirmation of what phase it was and whether readiness had been cleared by Treasury.

The phrase had appeared overnight in a briefing note titled "National Readiness Lane." Nobody knew who had written it. Policy suspected Strategy. Strategy suspected Communications. Communications said suspicion was unhelpful unless it could be turned into a line.

The Prime Minister asked what a readiness lane did.

The Cabinet Secretary said it moved priority work from general preparation into active pre-delivery.

Delivery asked whether active pre-delivery was delivery.

"No," said Treasury, relieved. "It is cheaper."

Legal asked whether calling it a lane implied direction. Digital asked whether the lane needed a dashboard. Communications asked whether the public would understand it as movement, urgency, or a new road scheme.

By 07:11, the room agreed the National Readiness Lane was not a road, not a queue, not a pledge, and not yet ready.

This was considered promising.

## ACT ONE

The first task was to decide which policies qualified for the readiness lane.

Health submitted a waiting list plan that was ready to be ready. Transport submitted a timetable review that was nearly ready to consider movement. Education submitted a curriculum update that had been ready last year but had since entered reflective readiness.

Treasury proposed a strict eligibility test: any policy entering the lane had to be affordable, reversible, and capable of being described without numbers.

Communications preferred a looser test: could the policy be photographed next to a hard hat, a clipboard, or a person looking at a tablet?

Digital produced a prototype dashboard with three columns: "Preparing," "Readying," and "Ready-ish." A fourth column, "Actually Happening," was hidden behind an admin permission nobody had.

The Minister for Public Reassurance asked for a public-friendly version.

The dashboard changed "Ready-ish" to "On Track."

Everyone felt better, except Delivery, who had read the tooltip.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments began competing to enter the lane.

The Environment department wanted readiness credit for considering a consultation on faster consideration. Housing wanted to enter the lane before announcing which lane housing was in. Culture submitted a theatre funding note with the phrase "audience-facing momentum," which Communications admired and nobody else understood.

The readiness lane became crowded. A junior official suggested a passing lane for policies that were especially ready. Treasury warned that passing implied speed. Legal warned that speed implied risk. Strategy proposed "measured acceleration," which was accepted because it sounded like doing two things at once.

The Prime Minister wanted one clear example for lunchtime.

Policy offered a community renewal pilot. Delivery said it had no locations. Treasury said it had no budget. Communications said it had a name, which was a form of location in the public mind.

The Cabinet Secretary wrote "location in the public mind?" in the minutes, then crossed out the question mark to make it look decided.

Digital updated the dashboard. The pilot moved into "On Track," triggering an automated notification: "Congratulations, your initiative has achieved visible readiness."

Nobody knew what visible readiness meant.

Communications said it meant the graphic could be shared.

## ACT THREE

At 12:30, the room prepared the National Readiness Lane announcement.

The draft said: "The government is accelerating delivery by moving priority reforms into a new National Readiness Lane."

Legal crossed out "accelerating." Treasury crossed out "delivery." Delivery crossed out "moving" because nothing had moved. Communications replaced "new" with "clear" because new invited questions and clear invited gratitude.

The revised line said: "The government is setting out a clear readiness lane for priority reforms."

The Prime Minister said it sounded circular.

Strategy said circularity showed coherence.

A press officer asked what would happen to policies outside the lane. Policy said they would remain in the wider readiness environment. Treasury liked the wider readiness environment because it sounded spacious and unfunded.

Digital warned that the dashboard had begun automatically ranking departments by readiness. Health was first. Transport was second. The Cabinet Office was marked "calibrating." Treasury was not on the list, having opted out of metrics on value-for-money grounds.

The Minister for Public Reassurance asked whether the public could see the ranking.

"Not unless we publish it," said Digital.

Communications looked at the ranking, then asked whether "publish" included accidental screenshots.

## CLIMAX

The afternoon briefing began with the spokesperson saying government was entering a new period of focused readiness.

A reporter asked whether that meant anything would happen sooner. The spokesperson said the readiness lane would ensure reforms were prepared at pace. Another reporter asked which reforms. The spokesperson said priority reforms. A third asked who had prioritised them.

The spokesperson looked down.

"The readiness process," they said.

Back in the meeting room, Policy called this structurally true. Legal called it non-actionable. Treasury called it reassuringly inexpensive.

Then the dashboard went live on the briefing room screen by accident.

Every policy appeared as a small icon in a traffic lane. Several were reversing. One had stopped at "Awaiting Naming." The community renewal pilot was circling a roundabout labelled "Stakeholder Confidence." Treasury appeared as a toll booth.

The room froze.

The spokesperson continued, now standing beside the image of a stationery procurement review overtaking a hospital maintenance plan.

A reporter asked whether the readiness lane had traffic.

Communications shouted for someone to unplug the laptop. Digital said unplugging it would create a blank screen. Strategy said a blank screen could suggest calm. Legal asked whether calm had been approved.

The Prime Minister watched the feed and asked why the potholes policy had hazard lights.

Delivery said it was the only honest icon.

## TAG

By evening, the National Readiness Lane had been revised into a National Readiness Network, with lanes, filters, gates, buffers, and a central advisory kerb.

Departments were instructed to submit all future proposals with a readiness level, a visibility score, and a short statement explaining why not being ready yet demonstrated responsible preparation.

The Cabinet Secretary issued guidance clarifying that readiness was not delivery, delay, announcement, review, or retreat, but might contain elements of all five when viewed in context.

The final shot showed the dashboard after midnight.

All policies had moved into a new column:

"Ready For Further Readiness."

Underneath, a smaller alert flashed:

"Lane capacity review required."

Treasury approved the review, provided it remained stationary.
