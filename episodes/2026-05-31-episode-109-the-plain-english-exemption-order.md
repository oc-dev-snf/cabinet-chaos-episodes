# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 109
**Title:** The Plain English Exemption Order
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

A wall poster in the policy unit reads:
- **SAY IT CLEARLY**
- **SAY IT BRIEFLY**
- **SAY IT ONCE**

Below it, someone has taped a fourth line:
- **UNLESS SITUATIONALLY INADVISABLE**

An aide asks what that means.

A senior adviser says, “It means we support plain English with strategic exceptions for institutional weather.”

Cut to opening titles.

## ACT ONE

A new directive arrives: all public guidance must be simpler, shorter, and understandable on first read.

Everyone agrees immediately and nervously.

A taskforce is formed to implement simplification at pace.

It is named the **Clarity Delivery and Interpretive Safeguards Board**.

Its first decision: define plain English.

Three competing definitions appear:
- words people understand
- words lawyers can tolerate
- words that survive morning radio

A compromise is drafted:

“Plain English is language that is clear in ordinary contexts, except where precision requires calibrated ambiguity.”

Comms says the sentence needs simplifying.

## ACT TWO

Departments submit rewritten guidance.

Original line:

“Applications will be processed in sequence.”

Revised line:

“Applications will be handled in a fair, orderly, and context-responsive sequence architecture.”

A reviewer asks why “architecture” is there.

The author says it “signals seriousness.”

Another team proposes a tiered clarity model:
- green: plain language
- amber: plain-ish language
- red: language requiring a companion explainer, glossary, and tea

By lunch, most documents are amber.

By mid-afternoon, amber is renamed “professional plain.”

## ACT THREE

A minister asks for a one-page summary suitable for the public.

The team delivers six pages, with a one-page executive summary explaining why one page is no longer feasible.

The summary contains a key sentence:

“We remain committed to plain expression while preserving essential nuance protections for responsible interpretation.”

The minister asks, “Can we say that in normal words?”

An adviser replies, “Yes: we’ll try to be clear unless clarity causes issues.”

The minister says, “Use that.”

Officials request a risk review before committing to “issues.”

## CLIMAX

At the evening briefing, the spokesperson announces:

“We are launching a major plain-English drive across government guidance.”

A reporter asks for an example.

The spokesperson reads from today’s revised leaflet:

“Queue here.”

A second reporter asks why the leaflet is twelve pages.

The spokesperson says, “Accessibility annexes, interpretive notes, and a definition of ‘here.’”

A third reporter asks when truly plain versions will appear.

The spokesperson replies, “Immediately after completion of the exemptions schedule.”

## TAG

At 8:47 p.m., staff receive an internal memo:

**Subject:** Plain English Exemption Order (Provisional Final Draft)

- Use short sentences where practical
- Longer sentences permitted where short sentences may appear too short
- Avoid jargon unless jargon prevents misunderstanding of anti-jargon policy
- Replace “in due course” with a specific timeframe, unless due course remains most accurate

Footer line:

“Tomorrow’s workshop will simplify this memo.”

**END**
