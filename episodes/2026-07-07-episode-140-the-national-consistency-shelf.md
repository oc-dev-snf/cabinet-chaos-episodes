# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 140
**Title:** The National Consistency Shelf
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 06:52, the government discovered that three departments had announced the same initiative using three different verbs.

Health was accelerating it.

Transport was unlocking it.

Education was supporting it, subject to regional delivery confidence.

The Prime Minister asked whether the policy itself had changed.

Policy said no.

Communications said that depended on whether verbs counted as policy.

Legal said verbs counted as risk.

Treasury said verbs counted as spend if they implied movement.

By 07:04, an emergency meeting had been called to restore consistency across government language before the breakfast programmes noticed that the same funding pot had been described as new, renewed, protected, targeted, and "already making a difference" within a single half-hour.

The Cabinet Secretary opened with a calming statement.

"We need somewhere to put all approved language so it stays still."

Someone from Operations suggested a shared folder.

Digital said shared folders were where certainty went to become version history.

Communications suggested a grid.

Legal said grids created discoverable edges.

Treasury suggested everyone simply say less.

The room treated this as ideology.

At 07:19, the Prime Minister approved the creation of the National Consistency Shelf: a central place where all official lines would be stored, labelled, and retrieved before anyone went near a microphone.

It sounded reassuringly domestic.

It also sounded like something nobody would admit had collapsed.

## ACT ONE

The first argument was whether the Shelf was literal.

Facilities said there was a spare cabinet outside Conference Room B, although it currently contained old lanyards, emergency biscuits, and a laminated sign reading DO NOT USE FOR POLICY.

Digital said the Shelf would be cloud-based.

Legal asked which cloud.

Digital said a secure cloud.

Legal asked whether secure meant secure from the public, ministers, or the previous version of the line.

Nobody answered fast enough.

Communications wanted a simple rule: if a phrase was on the Shelf, everyone used it.

Policy wanted a caveat: unless the phrase no longer reflected the operational position.

Treasury wanted a footnote: unless the phrase created a financial expectation.

Legal wanted a warning label: unless the phrase had been said before in a materially different context by someone still in office.

The Cabinet Secretary asked how a normal person was supposed to know which line to use.

The room looked at the junior adviser.

The junior adviser looked at the door.

The Prime Minister said the Shelf should have three categories: Approved, Strongly Approved, and Do Not Improve.

Communications liked Do Not Improve. It sounded disciplined.

Policy said some lines might need improving.

Communications said that was exactly the sort of thinking the Shelf was being built to prevent.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments had begun uploading approved phrases.

The system immediately rejected "record investment" because eleven departments claimed ownership of the same record.

It rejected "long-term plan" because five of the plans expired within the spending review period.

It rejected "working at pace" after Digital pointed out that no measurable pace had been selected.

Transport uploaded: "We are getting Britain moving."

Health uploaded: "We are getting services moving."

Education uploaded: "We are helping young people move confidently into the future."

The Shelf flagged a movement duplication event.

Communications said duplication was fine if the public liked movement.

Treasury said movement usually required capital.

Legal said confidence was safer than movement because it could not be photographed.

The Cabinet Secretary created a rule: each department was allowed one active verb, one reserve verb, and one emergency verb held behind glass.

Housing chose build.

Energy chose secure.

Business chose unlock.

Transport objected, because unlock implied transport and Business had taken it without timetable approval.

The Prime Minister asked whether "deliver" was still available.

The room went quiet.

Deliver was in quarantine.

It had been overused, under-defined, and had recently appeared in a sentence with "shortly."

## ACT THREE

At 12:10, the National Consistency Shelf went live with a dashboard showing approved wording by department, status, confidence level, and likelihood of surviving a radio interview.

For nine minutes, government sounded coordinated.

Then a minister described a policy as "practical, principled, and properly funded."

The Shelf had approved "practical."

Legal had conditional clearance on "principled."

Treasury had not approved "properly funded," preferring "funded within an appropriate envelope."

The dashboard flashed amber.

Communications said amber looked like disagreement.

Digital changed amber to warm compliance.

Legal said warm compliance was not a status.

Digital said it was now.

Meanwhile, a second minister had gone on television and called the same policy "a national reset."

The Shelf had never approved reset. Reset implied a previous malfunction.

Policy suggested "refresh."

Communications said refresh sounded like a towel.

Treasury suggested "reprioritisation."

The Prime Minister said that sounded like cancelling something with better lighting.

The junior adviser tried to add "national reset" to the Shelf retrospectively.

The system asked whether the phrase was already public.

The adviser selected yes.

The system asked whether the phrase was intended.

The adviser looked around the room.

The Cabinet Secretary said: "Select pending intention."

## CLIMAX

The afternoon press briefing was meant to demonstrate the Shelf working smoothly.

The spokesperson began: "The government has been consistent throughout."

The Shelf immediately sent an internal alert:

"PHRASE NOT FOUND. NEAREST MATCH: THE GOVERNMENT HAS BEEN CONSISTENTLY WORKING THROUGH."

Communications told the spokesperson to continue.

A reporter asked whether the new Shelf proved the government had been inconsistent.

The spokesperson said it proved the government was serious about consistency.

Another reporter asked why there needed to be a national system for saying the same thing.

Legal advised: "Because accuracy matters."

Communications advised: "Because delivery matters."

Treasury advised: "Because uncontrolled synonyms are expensive."

The spokesperson said: "Because the public deserves clear, reliable language."

The Shelf flagged clear and reliable as partially overlapping commitments.

On the internal screen, every department began editing its preferred line at once.

Health changed "practical support" to "practical help."

Education changed "help" to "support" to avoid duplication.

Transport changed "support" to "backing" because support was now contested.

Business changed "backing" to "momentum," which caused a movement duplication event and triggered Transport again.

The dashboard refreshed so quickly it looked like a slot machine for accountability.

A reporter asked whether the government could give one simple answer.

The spokesperson smiled, looked down at the printed Shelf extract, and said:

"We are united behind the agreed words currently available."

For three seconds, nobody spoke.

Then the Shelf marked the sentence Approved, because it could find nothing in it that meant anything testable.

## TAG

By evening, the National Consistency Shelf had been declared a landmark improvement in cross-government message discipline.

Departments were instructed to submit all future language with a shelf position, synonym impact assessment, and expiry date for confidence.

Digital added a search function called Find Approved Meaning.

Legal added a filter called Words Already Regretted.

Treasury added a column showing the cost of adjectives.

The junior adviser printed the final approved line and placed it in the cabinet outside Conference Room B.

By morning, someone had moved it to make room for biscuits.
