# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 031  
**Title:** The Briefing Clarity Pilot  
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## Premise
A cross-government initiative promises to make official announcements easier to understand. The pilot succeeds so quickly that departments panic, worried that clear language could expose indecision, contradictions, and the true cost of “flexible implementation.”

## COLD OPEN – INT. CABINET OFFICE CORRIDOR – 7:02 AM

A laminated poster appears overnight:

**"CLARITY WEEK: SAY WHAT YOU MEAN"**

Staff stop and read it like it might be a prank.

**JULIA:**
Who approved this?

**DAN:**
The Communications Improvement Board.

**NINA:**
That sounds like the board that usually approves longer sentences.

**DAN:**
New chair. Big push for plain English.

A second poster is half-peeled underneath:

**"MESSAGE OPTIMISATION FORTNIGHT"**

**JULIA:**
We’re rebranding honesty as a seasonal campaign.

---

## ACT ONE – INT. DELIVERY UNIT WAR ROOM – 8:15 AM

A giant screen shows two versions of the same announcement.

**VERSION A (OLD):**
> "Delivery timelines remain under active review in line with adaptive operational dependencies."

**VERSION B (CLARITY PILOT):**
> "The project is late by three months."

Silence.

**ANALYST:**
Engagement is up 240 percent.

**NINA:**
Because people understood it?

**ANALYST:**
Yes. Also because they are furious.

**DAN:**
Furious is still engaged.

### SCENE 2 – INT. MINISTERIAL OUTER OFFICE – 8:53 AM

A briefing pack is marked **"PLAIN LANGUAGE – HIGH RISK"**.

**MINISTER HARROW:**
I can’t say, "We changed our mind twice and now we’re trying Plan C." 

**PRIVATE SECRETARY:**
It tested as "authentic."

**MINISTER HARROW:**
It will test as "headline."

**PRIVATE SECRETARY:**
The pilot guidance says short sentences, active verbs, no evasive framing.

**MINISTER HARROW:**
No evasive framing is itself an evasive phrase.

---

## ACT TWO – INT. CROSS-GOVERNMENT CALL – 9:37 AM

Department leads report on clarity rollout.

**HEALTH REP:**
We replaced "patient pathway friction" with "forms are confusing." Complaint volume dipped, then rose, then dipped again.

**TRANSPORT REP:**
We replaced "service variance" with "trains were cancelled." Public response: "thank you for saying it plainly." Also: "please fix it."

**TREASURY REP:**
We are trialing "cost increase" instead of "fiscal re-profiling."

**JULIA:**
And?

**TREASURY REP:**
Ministers have requested "a less increase-sounding option."

### SCENE 2 – INT. PRESS GRID DESK – 10:21 AM

Draft lines are being simplified against visible resistance.

- "We are considering options" → "We have not decided yet"
- "Initial phase mobilisation" → "It starts next month"
- "Subject to constraints" → "If we can afford it"

**PRESS OFFICER:**
Journalists are calling this "refreshingly direct." That’s great. Terrifying, but great.

**DAN:**
Any downside?

**PRESS OFFICER:**
Yes. Follow-up questions are now specific.

**JULIA:**
So the downside of clarity is accountability.

---

## ACT THREE – INT. LIVE BRIEFING ROOM – 11:14 AM

The spokesperson steps up to the podium with the new one-page format.

**SPOKESPERSON:**
Good morning. Three updates. One: the rollout is delayed by eight weeks. Two: this will cost an extra twelve million. Three: we made a planning error and are correcting it.

Reporters look up, startled, then immediately start writing.

**REPORTER 1:**
Thank you. When did you know about the planning error?

**SPOKESPERSON:**
Last Thursday.

**REPORTER 2:**
Why announce now?

**SPOKESPERSON:**
Because today’s pilot says we should answer directly.

In the back row, Dan exhales slowly like a man watching a bridge hold in high wind.

### SCENE 2 – INT. PRIME MINISTER’S OFFICE – 11:49 AM

A briefing note summary:

> **CLARITY PILOT – DAY ONE:**  
> Public trust signal: up modestly  
> Media scrutiny: up significantly  
> Internal comfort level: down dramatically

**PM:**
So plain language helps trust but increases pressure.

**ADVISER:**
Yes. People appreciate being told the truth, then ask what you’re doing about it.

**PM:**
That seems fair.

**DAN:**
It is also operationally intense.

**PM:**
Good. Keep it.

**JULIA:**
All of it?

**PM:**
Maybe not all at once. We phase clarity.

**NINA:**
Can clarity be phased?

**PM:**
In government, everything can be phased.

---

## CLIMAX – INT. CABINET ROOM – 12:31 PM

A decision meeting on whether to keep the pilot.

Two columns on a whiteboard:

**KEEP CLARITY**
- Higher trust
- Fewer misunderstandings
- Faster corrections

**PAUSE CLARITY**
- Tougher headlines
- Less room for tactical ambiguity
- More immediate accountability

**COMMITTEE CHAIR:**
Recommendation?

**ANALYST:**
Keep it. We spend less time clarifying after publication.

**TREASURY REP:**
Can we at least preserve one strategic phrase?

**JULIA:**
Which one?

**TREASURY REP:**
"Subject to usual constraints."

**NINA:**
No. If there are constraints, name them.

A beat.

**PM:**
Approved. Clarity pilot becomes policy.

Everyone nods like they’ve just agreed to personal fitness targets.

---

## TAG – INT. CABINET OFFICE PRINT ROOM – 6:06 PM

A new style guide slides from the printer:

**"WRITE FOR HUMANS – VERSION 1.0"**

Rule one is underlined twice:

**"If a sentence hides the decision, rewrite it."**

Dan arrives with a pen and adds a tiny footnote:

**"Except legal text, where survival instincts apply."**

Nina reads it, grabs the pen, and adds:

**"Legal text pending separate reform."**

Julia pins the page to the notice board.

By morning, someone has added a sticky note:

**"We can work with this."**

**END**
