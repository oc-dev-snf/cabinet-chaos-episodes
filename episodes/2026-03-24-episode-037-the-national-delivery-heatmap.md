# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 037
**Title:** The National Delivery Heatmap
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

A minister stands in front of a giant screen glowing with a color-coded map titled: **"DELIVERY CONFIDENCE BY POSTCODE."**

MINISTER: Today we bring radical transparency. Every neighbourhood can track government delivery in real time.

MALCOLM: Nothing reassures the nation like turning public services into weather.

GLENN squints at the legend.

GLENN: Why is half the country "light aubergine"?

MALCOLM: Because "we haven't the faintest" tested badly in focus groups.

---

## ACT ONE

### 1) The innovation pitch

The PM's office launches the **National Delivery Heatmap**, promising that citizens can finally see what is "on track," "off track," or "narratively on track."

Officials insist the map is not political, merely data-driven.

The categories are:
- Green: delivered
- Amber: in progress
- Red: delayed
- Blue: being reframed
- Grey: awaiting a definition of "delivered"

NICOLA: How can a hospital upgrade be "blue"?

MALCOLM: Same way a pothole can be "strategic". You rename the failure and invoice it as reform.

### 2) The regional briefing

Civil servants brief local teams on how to improve map colors before the evening news cycle.

Suggested actions include:
- Split one project into seven milestones
- Mark milestone one as complete
- Publish a "trajectory statement"
- Reclassify remaining milestones as "phase-adjacent"

GLENN: So we haven't done the thing, but we've colour-corrected it.

MALCOLM: Exactly. Governance by highlighter pen.

---

## ACT TWO

### 1) The leaderboard problem

A friendly competition appears on the dashboard: **"Top Performing Regions This Week."**

Within hours, regions begin disputing each other's metrics.

One mayor says their area is unfairly scored because their projects are "harder, older, and attached to Victorian plumbing."

Another says they should get bonus points for "optimism under pressure."

NICOLA: Have we made local government collaborate less by introducing a podium?

MALCOLM: We took public service and added fantasy football.

### 2) The interpretation wars

Morning radio asks what "amber-plus" means.

By lunchtime, five departments issue five explanations:
- "Amber-plus means accelerated readiness"
- "Amber-plus means risk-managed momentum"
- "Amber-plus means delivery with caveats"
- "Amber-plus is temporary while definitions align"
- "Amber-plus should not be interpreted as amber"

GLENN: Is amber-plus good or bad?

MALCOLM: It's Schrödinger's progress. Alive in the press release, dead on the ground.

### 3) The fairness panel

To restore trust, the Cabinet Office convenes an independent panel: a teacher, a GP, a logistics manager, a social worker, and a small business owner.

They recommend three plain rules:
1. Use fewer labels
2. Publish real dates
3. Say what changed and why

The draft response from central comms reads: **"We welcome stakeholder sentiment and will launch a consultation on simplification pathways."**

NICOLA: They asked for plain English.

MALCOLM: We gave them corporate fog with bullet points.

---

## ACT THREE

### 1) Emergency harmonisation

Senior officials panic after contradictory map updates go live during breakfast TV.

A "single truth" protocol is introduced:
- One owner per metric
- One definition per label
- One update window per day

Then three directorates all claim ownership of "overall confidence," and the map freezes with an on-screen message:

**"SYSTEM BUSY: VALIDATING REALITY."**

GLENN: Can we manually refresh?

MALCOLM: You can't manually refresh reality, Glenn. That's how we got here.

### 2) The fallback announcement

By late afternoon, ministers scrap the heatmap presentation and issue a simpler statement:

- What has been delivered this month
- What slipped
- What will happen next
- Who is accountable

Reporters ask why this wasn't the format from day one.

A spokesperson replies: "We wanted to innovate responsibly."

MALCOLM: Translation: we built a complicated mirror, then rediscovered windows.

---

## CLIMAX

At the evening briefing, the minister returns without the giant map.

No gradients. No performance badges. No scorecards.

Just a one-page update with dates, costs, and named owners.

The questions are sharper, but the answers are clearer.

For the first time in days, nobody asks what a color means.

MALCOLM, quietly: Look at that. Honesty in plain formatting. Revolutionary stuff.

The room does not applaud, but it does stop heckling.

Which, in this building, counts as a standing ovation.

---

## TAG

Next morning, staff receive a new internal guidance note:

**"From now on, if a status needs a legend, rewrite the status."**

GLENN pins it above his desk.

MALCOLM walks past, nods once, and says:

"Congratulations. We've accidentally invented clarity. Try not to kill it in committee."

**END**
