# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 087
**Title:** The National Sign-Off Bottleneck Review
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

(CABINET OFFICE INCIDENT ROOM, 07:00. A status board reads: **"DECISIONS WAITING FOR SIGN-OFF — 1,204"**.)

Morning briefing card:

**DELIVERY RECOVERY WEEK — DAY 3**  
**Scenario:** *"The National Sign-Off Bottleneck Review"*

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Why is the urgent decision queue older than some of our interns?

**PRIVATE SECRETARY:** Because every routine approval now requires thirteen stakeholders, two visibility calls, and a ceremonial slide deck.

**OPERATIONS ADVISER:** Also, three signatures are pending from people currently in the same room.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Outstanding. We have invented participatory paralysis.

CUT TO TITLE.

## ACT ONE — THE QUEUE THAT ATE GOVERNMENT

08:14. Cross-government call with departmental directors, legal, finance, and delivery leads.

Slide title: **"IF EVERYTHING IS CRITICAL, NOTHING MOVES"**

**PM:** How many decisions are blocked today?

**DELIVERY DIRECTOR:** 1,204 waiting, 318 marked urgent, 97 marked "urgent urgent." 

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** That's not a category.

**DELIVERY DIRECTOR:** It is now.

Four failure patterns identified:

1. Approval thresholds undefined, so low-risk items escalate by default
2. Duplicated sign-off chains across policy, legal, and comms
3. No ownership clock, so approvals can idle indefinitely
4. Last-minute edits reset the process without an audit trail

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** We have built a motorway where every lane merges into one inbox.

## ACT TWO — THE DE-BOTTLENECK PLAN

10:31. Rapid redesign session with Cabinet Office process team and departmental operations heads.

Slide title: **"ROUTE BY RISK, NOT BY PANIC"**

**PROCESS LEAD:** Introduce three approval lanes: standard, elevated, and exceptional.

**LEGAL ADVISER:** One legal reviewer per item, not six parallel comments in tracked-change warfare.

**FINANCE LEAD:** Auto-approve low-value recurring spend if controls are already pre-cleared.

Three controls approved for immediate use:

- **Risk Routing Matrix:** decision path set by impact and reversibility
- **Sign-Off Timer:** 24h standard, 8h elevated, 2h exceptional
- **Change Log Lock:** edits after final review require named justification

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** If a decision needs twelve signatures, it's either historic or unclear.

## ACT THREE — FIRST DAY OF TRIAGE

12:06. New workflow goes live in five departments.

**INCIDENT COORDINATOR:** Queue split complete. Standard lane now isolated from crisis traffic.

12:42. Risk Routing Matrix enforced.

**PROGRAMME MANAGER:** 41% of "urgent" items reclassified as standard without operational risk.

13:17. Sign-Off Timer triggers.

**SERVICE OWNER:** Two bottlenecks exposed immediately: one vacant approver role, one mailbox rule sending escalations to archive.

14:03. Change Log Lock activates.

**DELIVERY LEAD:** Last-minute wording edits dropped by half once names had to be attached.

Follow-up actions agreed:

- Publish weekly median sign-off time by department
- Add deputy approvers for every elevated lane role
- Run monthly audit of reclassified urgency tags
- Sunset any approval step not linked to a legal or operational control

**PM:** We are not reducing scrutiny. We are reducing decorative delay.

## ACT FOUR — COMMS, WITH A STOPWATCH

16:38. Communications clears line: **"GOVERNMENT CUTS DECISION DELAYS WITH RISK-BASED SIGN-OFF"**

Public points agreed:

- Fast decisions and accountable decisions are not opposites
- Low-risk work should not compete with emergency response
- Named ownership improves quality and speed
- Clear timers prevent silent drift
- Transparent metrics allow challenge and correction

By 18:12 (day-one metrics):

- Total pending sign-offs down from 1,204 to 742
- Items older than 7 days down 58%
- Median standard-lane approval time down from 46h to 17h
- False-urgent tagging down 44%

**HEAD OF COMMS:** It's less dramatic than crisis language, but visibly functional.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** We didn't need a miracle. We needed a queue with rules.

## CLIMAX — THE LOBBY SCRUM

18:25. Reporters crowd the briefing room.

**REPORTER:** Minister, did bureaucracy get out of control?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** It got overconfident about how many people needed to touch the same document.

**REPORTER:** Are you cutting safeguards?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** No. We're distinguishing safeguards from sightseeing.

**REPORTER:** What changes for the public?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Routine decisions should feel routine again.
