# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 069
**Title:** The Target Dashboard Truce
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

(CABINET OFFICE, 07:00. A giant wall screen cycles through red, amber, and green boxes faster than anyone can read.)

Morning briefing card:

**DELIVERY ALIGNMENT SESSION — DAY 4**  
**Scenario:** *"The Target Dashboard Truce"*

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Why is everything flashing like we've parked policy inside an ambulance?

**DATA ADVISER:** It's the live target dashboard. Every department wanted their own color logic.

**PRIVATE SECRETARY:** Transport says amber means "on track with caveats." Housing says amber means "deeply concerning but optimistic." Treasury says amber means "not my budget line."

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Excellent. We've invented a national mood ring.

CUT TO TITLE.

## ACT ONE — THE METRIC ARGUMENT

Situation room, 08:18. Whiteboard reads: **IF EVERYTHING IS A PRIORITY, NOTHING IS READ.**

**PM:** Parliament wants one progress picture, not fourteen competing weather maps.

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** Yesterday we published three dashboards and a correction to the correction.

**HEAD OF COMMS:** Public response is clear: "Can someone explain what success actually looks like?"

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Fair question. Let's answer it before someone answers it for us.

They list recurring failures:

1. Targets measured differently across departments
2. Deadline changes posted without reasons
3. "On track" used for projects with no start date
4. Success percentages rounded into fiction
5. Footnotes doing all the honest work

**PM:** New rule: no metric without method.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** And no method that needs a priesthood to decode.

## ACT TWO — THE TRUCE PROPOSAL

Policy workshop, 10:56. Slide title: **COMMON SCORECARD FRAMEWORK (DRAFT)**.

**DATA ADVISER:** We propose three layers only: output, outcome, confidence.

**OPERATIONS DIRECTOR:** In ordinary language?

**DATA ADVISER:** What got done, what changed, and how sure we are.

Framework agreed in principle:

- **Output:** deliverables completed this period
- **Outcome:** measurable public impact to date
- **Confidence:** high / medium / low, with justification

**LEGAL ADVISER:** Confidence must include data quality notes.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Good. Uncertainty declared up front, not discovered in committee.

By 11:44 they add a mandatory field: **"What would make this fail next month?"**

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** If teams can name the risk, they can own the risk.

## ACT THREE — THE DEADLINE RECONCILIATION

Cross-government call, 13:27. Agenda: **DATES PEOPLE CAN TRUST**.

**TRANSPORT REP:** We can commit by quarter, not by week.

**HEALTH REP:** We can commit by week, but only regionally.

**EDUCATION REP:** We can commit by term dates; anything else creates chaos.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Then publish commitments at the level you can keep, not the level that sounds brave.

They settle a shared date protocol:

- **Committed date** = operationally signed off
- **Planning date** = likely window, subject to dependencies
- **Review date** = next formal update point
- **Recovery date** = published only when a committed date slips

**PM:** The public can handle nuance. They can't handle whiplash.

Ticker appears:

**"Government adopts common target definitions across core services."**

**HEAD OF COMMS:** That's refreshingly boring.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Boring is underrated when you're rebuilding trust.

## ACT FOUR — THE PUBLIC RELEASE TEST

Press office, 17:03. New dashboard goes live with a plain-language legend at the top.

Release protocol:

- Every headline metric links to method notes
- Every changed target includes reason + mitigation
- Confidence rating displayed beside each major claim
- Last updated timestamp visible on every panel
- Downloadable data published in open format

By 18:11:

- Clarification requests down 35%
- Repeat journalist queries down 29%
- Public tracker visits up 48%
- Corrections issued: one, resolved in 19 minutes

**PM:** Are we finally saying less and showing more?

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** For one afternoon, yes.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Frame that sentence. Then verify it quarterly.

## CLIMAX — THE LOBBY SCRUM

18:42. Microphones rise.

**REPORTER:** Minister, is this dashboard transparency or performance theatre?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Transparency with receipts. Theatre usually charges extra and doesn't publish CSV files.

**REPORTER:** Will you still call things "on track" when confidence is low?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** We'll call them what they are. Low confidence means extra risk, not forbidden language.

**REPORTER:** That sounds unusually direct.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** We've trialled the alternative. It was louder, not better.

## TAG

20:31. Empty situation room. The big screen is finally still.

A small note is taped to the monitor:

**"GREEN WITHOUT METHOD = GREY."**

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER (off-screen):** Keep that up. Saves time.

**END**
