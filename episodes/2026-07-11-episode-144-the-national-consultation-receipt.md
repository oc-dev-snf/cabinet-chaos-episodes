# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 144
**Title:** The National Consultation Receipt
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:02, the government discovered it had consulted the public but could not prove where it had put the public's answers.

The Prime Minister wanted reassurance.

Policy had reassurance in draft.

Communications had reassurance in landscape.

Legal had reassurance with conditional footnotes.

Digital had a folder called "final consultation responses FINAL use this one maybe."

Treasury asked whether any of the reassurance created a spending commitment.

The Cabinet Secretary said the important thing was that people felt heard.

The junior adviser asked whether feeling heard required anyone to listen.

Nobody looked directly at the junior adviser.

By 07:14, Digital proposed the National Consultation Receipt: a simple automatic acknowledgement confirming that every view submitted by a member of the public had been received, valued, categorised, and placed somewhere structurally appropriate.

The Prime Minister liked it.

It sounded respectful, measurable, and unlikely to answer back.

## ACT ONE

The first meeting was about what a receipt should actually say.

Communications wanted warmth.

Legal wanted distance.

Policy wanted language that implied careful consideration without implying a result.

Treasury wanted nothing that could be printed at scale.

Digital displayed the first draft:

YOUR VIEW HAS BEEN RECEIVED.

The Cabinet Secretary said it was accurate but chilly.

Communications added "and matters to us."

Legal changed it to "and may matter to the relevant process."

Policy suggested "your contribution will inform our thinking."

Treasury asked whether thinking had a budget line.

The Prime Minister asked for something human.

Digital changed the heading to:

THANK YOU FOR HELPING SHAPE THE NEXT STAGE OF OUR LISTENING.

Everyone agreed it sounded like progress.

Nobody could explain which stage they were in.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, the prototype receipt system was sorting public responses into categories.

Strong Support.

Constructive Concern.

Operational Detail.

Already Under Review.

Outside Scope But Spiritually Relevant.

Legal objected to "spiritually relevant."

Communications liked it because it sounded inclusive and did not bind anyone to a timetable.

Policy asked whether strong opposition should have its own category.

The room paused.

The Cabinet Secretary said strong opposition was a form of engagement.

Digital created a category called High-Intensity Participation.

Treasury asked if that reduced the number of problems.

Digital said it reduced the number of red cells.

Treasury approved.

The Prime Minister reviewed a sample public response saying the proposal was confusing, expensive, and appeared to have been designed by a committee that had never met a bus stop.

The receipt classified it as Lived Experience With Transport Adjacency.

Transport said that sounded fair.

## ACT THREE

At 12:30, Communications prepared the public line.

The government would not say it had ignored concerns.

It would say it had captured concerns.

Policy preferred "absorbed."

Legal said "absorbed" implied digestion, and digestion had outcomes.

Treasury suggested "held."

Communications said "held" sounded caring.

Legal said "held" sounded like evidence.

The junior adviser suggested "noted."

The room reacted as if someone had opened a window in a submarine.

The Cabinet Secretary said "noted" was too honest for external use and too final for internal use.

Digital presented the live dashboard.

It showed 18,462 receipts issued, 18,462 views valued, and 18,461 views successfully routed.

The Prime Minister asked about the missing one.

Digital said it had been escalated because the sender had asked a clear question.

Everyone looked worried.

Policy asked what the question was.

Digital read it aloud:

"What will change as a result of this consultation?"

The meeting was immediately moved to a higher classification.

## CLIMAX

The afternoon statement was meant to announce a new era of responsive government.

The Prime Minister stood at the lectern beside a screen showing a large green tick and the words PUBLIC VOICES RECEIVED.

Communications had written a line about listening.

Policy had written a line about shaping.

Legal had written a line about no predetermined outcome, except where a predetermined outcome had already been lawfully indicated.

Treasury had deleted three adjectives.

The Prime Minister began: "Today, we can confirm that every contribution has been heard, valued, and reflected in the next phase."

A reporter asked what the next phase was.

The Prime Minister said it was the phase after listening.

The reporter asked whether that was action.

The Cabinet Secretary, standing behind the cameras, made the smallest possible no gesture.

The Prime Minister said it was "delivery-facing consideration."

At that exact moment, the receipt dashboard refreshed on the screen.

It showed a new automatic message:

THANK YOU FOR YOUR QUESTION. YOUR REQUEST FOR A CONSEQUENCE HAS BEEN RECEIVED.

Communications tried to switch the display to a photo of a community roundtable.

Digital said the receipt system had locked the screen because the public had triggered transparency mode.

Legal asked whether transparency mode could be appealed.

The system issued another receipt:

YOUR APPEAL AGAINST TRANSPARENCY HAS BEEN RECEIVED.

For twelve seconds, the government visibly listened.

Then Treasury unplugged the extension lead on cost-control grounds.

## TAG

By evening, the National Consultation Receipt was described as a landmark in participatory assurance.

Departments were asked to show how public views had influenced their plans.

Most replied with screenshots of the green tick.

Digital proposed a second dashboard to track whether the receipts themselves had been received.

The Cabinet Secretary approved it as long as nobody consulted anyone.
