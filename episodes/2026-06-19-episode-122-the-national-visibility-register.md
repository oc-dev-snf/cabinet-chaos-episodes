# Cabinet Chaos Episode 122: The National Visibility Register

## Cold Open
At 07:00, the Cabinet Office assembled to launch the National Visibility Register, a new cross-government system designed to ensure that everyone could see what everyone else was doing, provided nobody expected the seeing to become knowing.

The Prime Minister arrived with the "Agenda Certainty" folder, now reinforced with an elastic band from Procurement and a warning label from Legal reading, "Contents may imply direction, receipt, or readiness."

On the table sat the Register itself: a glossy binder containing twelve coloured tabs, five blank dashboards, and a foreword explaining that visibility was the first step towards transparency, which was itself the first step towards something still under consultation.

Officials admired the binder's cover. It had a small window cut into the front, through which one could see a sheet of paper marked "visible." This was considered a strong start.

## The Briefing
The morning briefing opened with a slide titled "Seeing The Work Without Prematurely Interpreting It." The Minister for Coordination explained that the Register would list all major government initiatives, their current status, their accountable owners, and the reasons those owners could not yet be named.

Delivery asked whether a register of work should include work that had not begun. Policy said yes, because planned work had visibility needs. Treasury asked whether visibility created a cost pressure. Communications said visibility reduced pressure by letting people see that pressure was being visibly managed.

The Permanent Secretary proposed four official status categories: "underway," "nearly underway," "pre-underway," and "awaiting visibility." Legal asked for "underway" to be footnoted in case activity was later found to have been indicative rather than actual.

By the sixth slide, the approved line read: "The National Visibility Register provides a single, shared view of the government's active landscape, while respecting the operational sensitivities of specific horizons."

The room liked "specific horizons." It sounded precise without being near anything.

## The Complication
Trouble began when departments started submitting entries.

The Department for Implementation entered twelve programmes as "visible pending confirmation." The Department for Assurance entered the same twelve as "confirmed pending visibility." Treasury entered them as "fiscally adjacent." Communications entered them as "not for public invisibility."

The Register team tried reconciling the categories by adding a confidence column, but the confidence column required its own confidence rating. A second column was added. Then a note was added explaining that the second column should not be read as less confident than the first column unless confidence had moved.

An official suggested that the Register might be easier if it simply said what each project was, who owned it, and what happened next. This was welcomed as a bold simplification concept and assigned to a scoping annex.

The Prime Minister asked whether the public would be able to see the Register. Communications said yes, in the sense that they would be able to see that a Register existed.

## The Spin Room
By lunchtime, a draft screenshot had leaked. It showed a table where every entry was marked "amber-green," except one row marked "to be coloured responsibly."

Reporters asked whether amber-green meant good news or bad news. The spokesperson said it meant the government was taking a mature approach to visible complexity. Asked if any projects were late, they said lateness was a narrow measure and the Register preferred "sequencing realism."

Asked why several columns were blacked out, the spokesperson said the redactions demonstrated the government's commitment to visible boundaries. Asked why the visible parts were mostly headings, they said headings were an important part of the delivery ecosystem.

Advisers prepared a media plan. Morning interviews would focus on "clarity." Afternoon clips would focus on "grip." Evening panels would be offered "grip through clarity," unless audiences preferred "clarity with grip."

The reserve line was: "You cannot improve what you cannot see, and you cannot safely see everything all at once."

## Corridor Conversations
In the corridor, the Chancellor asked whether the Register could be revenue neutral. Treasury said binders were small, but visibility had a habit of attracting questions, and questions sometimes required answers.

The Minister for Delivery said the Register might help if it identified decisions that were stuck. A senior official replied that stuck decisions would be captured under "movement under review." Delivery asked whether that meant stuck. The official said it meant visible movement towards a fuller understanding of stillness.

The paper diary minister offered to maintain a handwritten backup. Digital warned that handwritten visibility risked uncontrolled legibility. He compromised by drawing a neat box around the word "progress."

Meanwhile, an aide discovered that three teams had already created their own visibility registers. One tracked priorities. One tracked risks. One tracked other registers. The third was immediately designated the interim parent register while governance caught up.

## Closing Beat
By evening, the National Visibility Register had been published as a two-page summary, a six-page explanation of why the summary was short, and a thirty-page annex listing future opportunities for fuller visibility.

The Prime Minister said the Register showed a government comfortable with being seen. The Minister for Coordination said it would bring a new level of shared situational awareness. The Minister for Delivery said he could now see several things that still needed doing, which everyone agreed was a useful visibility outcome.

In the final scene, an official placed the Register inside the "Agenda Certainty" folder.

The folder's elastic band snapped, then issued a brief statement confirming that capacity remained visible.

**Episode Notes**
- Tone: Satirical but even-handed; the joke stays on dashboards, status categories, and the way visibility can become a substitute for decisions.
- Running theme: Seeing the work becomes its own deliverable, complete with caveats, confidence ratings, and governance.
- Continuity hook: The "Agenda Certainty" folder now contains readiness, receipts, directionality, and visibility, and has begun requiring physical resilience measures.
