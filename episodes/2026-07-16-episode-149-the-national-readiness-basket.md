# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 149
**Title:** The National Readiness Basket
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the government announced that the country was ready.

Nobody had specified for what.

Communications said readiness was a mood, not a list.

Policy said the list existed but was not yet ready for the readiness announcement.

Legal said readiness should not be interpreted as preparedness, capability, delivery, or a promise to begin.

Treasury asked whether readiness required new money or could be achieved by rebadging alertness.

The Prime Minister arrived with a wicker basket containing three folders, two briefing cards, and a banana that had been included by Facilities under "visible resilience."

The Cabinet Secretary explained that the National Readiness Basket would collect every department's readiness offer into one reassuring container.

The junior adviser asked whether a basket was the strongest available metaphor for national capability.

Communications said voters liked baskets because they suggested choice, harvest, and not spreadsheets.

Policy said the basket allowed flexible prioritisation.

Legal said anything placed in the basket might become discoverable.

Treasury asked whether the basket was capital or current spending.

The Prime Minister peered inside and asked why one folder was marked READY PENDING READINESS.

The Cabinet Secretary said it was important to show momentum without prematurely moving.

## ACT ONE

By 08:30, a cross-government readiness exercise had been convened in the Cabinet Office.

Each department had been asked to submit one item to the basket.

Transport offered a map with no routes highlighted, because highlighting routes could imply commitment.

Health offered a protocol for convening the group that would determine which protocol was operational.

Education offered a laminated card saying "learning continues," which Legal liked because it did not say where.

The Home Office offered a sealed envelope labelled INTERNAL CONFIDENCE.

Nobody opened it.

Digital offered a dashboard that measured readiness through a traffic-light system.

Every light was amber.

Digital said amber meant active assurance.

Communications said amber photographed badly.

Policy suggested calling it gold.

Treasury asked whether gold had procurement implications.

The Prime Minister tried the line: "We are ready because we have prepared."

Legal said prepared was too close to preparation.

The Prime Minister tried: "We are ready because readiness has been embedded."

Communications liked embedded.

The Cabinet Secretary said embedded sounded stable.

The junior adviser asked whether anyone could point to the embedded thing.

The room agreed pointing was an implementation phase.

## ACT TWO

At 10:00, the basket became a governance issue.

Departments began arguing over which items should sit on top.

Communications wanted the banana visible because it humanised the exercise.

Policy wanted the dashboard visible because it suggested grip.

Legal wanted nothing visible because visibility invited interpretation.

Treasury wanted the empty space visible because it showed efficiency.

The Cabinet Secretary proposed a readiness hierarchy.

Tier one would contain items that were ready.

Tier two would contain items that were ready to become ready.

Tier three would contain items that were strategically unready in order to preserve optionality.

Communications asked whether tier three could be called "advanced flexibility."

Legal approved, provided flexibility did not imply movement.

Digital updated the dashboard.

The basket icon turned amber.

The Prime Minister asked why the basket itself needed a status.

Digital said every container in the system required assurance.

Treasury asked whether an assured container reduced the need for assured contents.

The junior adviser said the whole exercise was starting to look like a picnic where nobody had brought food because eating would prejudge lunch.

Communications wrote that down, then crossed out "picnic" and replaced it with "national moment."

## ACT THREE

At 12:15, the Prime Minister rehearsed the readiness statement.

Behind the lectern stood a banner reading:

READY FOR WHAT COMES NEXT.

Policy said it sounded confident.

Legal said "what comes next" implied there was a known sequence of events.

Communications changed it to:

READY FOR THE NEXT PHASE.

Legal said phase implied commencement.

Treasury asked whether phases could be funded retrospectively.

The Cabinet Secretary suggested:

READY TO SUPPORT READINESS.

Everyone paused.

Communications said it had a seriousness to it.

Policy said it was circular, but in a dependable way.

Digital said the slogan could be integrated with the basket dashboard.

The Prime Minister began: "Today, I can confirm that the government is ready."

Legal coughed.

The Prime Minister tried again: "Today, I can confirm that the government has a readiness posture."

Communications said posture sounded like something requiring physiotherapy.

The Prime Minister tried: "Today, I can confirm that the government has placed readiness at the heart of delivery."

Treasury asked whether the heart of delivery was an existing departmental location.

The junior adviser asked if the public might prefer delivery at the heart of readiness.

The Cabinet Secretary said that was exactly why junior advisers were not allowed near banners.

## CLIMAX

The press conference began at 15:00.

The Prime Minister stood beside the National Readiness Basket.

Communications had briefed that the basket symbolised calm coordination.

Policy had briefed that it represented integrated capability.

Legal had briefed that it was not a literal repository of obligations.

Treasury had briefed that no additional basket funding had been agreed.

A reporter asked what the country was now ready for.

The Prime Minister said: "We are ready for the challenges and opportunities ahead."

The reporter asked which challenges.

Communications signalled "global."

Policy signalled "emerging."

Legal signalled "not enumerated."

The Prime Minister said: "Global and emerging challenges, without enumerating them unnecessarily."

Another reporter asked what was in the basket.

The Prime Minister looked down.

Digital attempted to help by putting the dashboard on the screen behind the lectern.

It displayed:

CONTAINER: NATIONAL READINESS BASKET.

CONTENTS: UNDER REVIEW.

STATUS: READY.

DEPENDENCY: CONTENTS.

The room went still.

The Cabinet Secretary said dependencies demonstrated maturity.

Treasury asked whether empty readiness was cheaper to scale.

Communications called it a powerful visual of disciplined preparedness.

The junior adviser said it was a government holding a basket and hoping the groceries would arrive before the photograph.

The Prime Minister called it a confident platform for the next phase.

## TAG

By evening, every department had been asked to submit evidence that its readiness item was ready to be placed in the basket.

Digital added a second basket for evidence.

Legal marked it provisional.
