# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 143
**Title:** The National Priority Turnstile
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 06:58, the government discovered it had too many priorities for the priority list.

The Prime Minister had asked for the top five.

Policy had provided twelve, grouped under five headings, each heading containing a footnote explaining why it was not technically a list.

Communications said the public liked focus.

Treasury said focus was cheaper when nothing inside it was numbered.

Legal said numbering implied sequence, and sequence implied someone might ask why number four had happened before number two.

The Cabinet Secretary asked whether any of the priorities were actually urgent.

Operations said all of them were urgent in principle.

The Prime Minister asked what that meant in practice.

Operations said it meant no department wanted to be seen entering the meeting second.

By 07:11, Digital had proposed the National Priority Turnstile: a controlled entry system through which only one government priority could pass at a time, provided it had the correct authorisation, public-facing urgency, and no immediate clash with a ministerial visit.

The Prime Minister liked it.

It sounded decisive, and decisiveness with a queue was still decisiveness.

## ACT ONE

The first problem was deciding what counted as a priority.

Policy said a priority was anything included in the programme for national renewal.

Communications said a priority was anything the Prime Minister might be asked about while standing near machinery.

Treasury said a priority was anything already funded, which made the list pleasingly short.

Legal said a priority was anything described as a priority, unless that description had been made before the current strategic reset.

The junior adviser asked whether a priority could stop being a priority.

The room went quiet.

The Cabinet Secretary said priorities did not stop being priorities. They transitioned into background commitments.

Communications wrote that down and asked whether it could be softened.

Digital demonstrated the turnstile dashboard.

Each proposed priority appeared as a small rectangle with a status light: Ready, Waiting, Reframing, Temporarily Historic, or Blocked by Another Priority.

The Prime Minister selected Ready for three rectangles.

The system rejected this as "excess leadership."

Treasury asked if excess leadership carried a cost.

Digital said only reputational.

Treasury asked whether reputational costs could be deferred.

Legal said they usually were.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments were submitting their priorities to the turnstile.

Health submitted access.

Transport submitted delivery.

Education submitted opportunity.

Housing submitted supply.

Business submitted growth.

Culture submitted a commemorative season of carefully inclusive national enthusiasm.

The turnstile admitted growth first because it was alphabetically confident.

Health objected that access had alphabetical precedence.

Digital explained that growth had arrived with a ministerial foreword, which counted as momentum.

Policy said momentum should not override need.

Communications said momentum was easier to photograph.

Treasury said need was what happened when momentum had not been properly budgeted.

The Cabinet Secretary created a new classification: Priority With Managed Momentum.

Within six minutes, every department had resubmitted under it.

The turnstile jammed.

Digital diagnosed a loop in which each priority was waiting for itself to become the reason the others could wait.

The Prime Minister asked if that was bad.

The Cabinet Secretary said it was familiar.

## ACT THREE

At 12:04, the National Priority Turnstile went live internally.

The first public-facing output was supposed to be a clean statement showing that the government was focused on delivery.

Instead, the system produced a ranking:

1. Growth.

2. Access.

3. Delivery.

4. Opportunity.

5. Supply.

6. The concept of focus.

Communications said the concept of focus could not be sixth.

Policy said it could not be first either, because then everything below it would look unfocused.

Legal said if the concept of focus had appeared on an official list, it now had stakeholder implications.

Treasury asked whether the concept of focus had a departmental owner.

Nobody answered.

The Prime Minister asked to remove the numbers.

Digital removed the numbers.

The system replaced them with bands: Immediate, Near Immediate, Actively Near, Strategically Pending, and In the National Interest Later.

Communications said "later" sounded lazy.

Treasury said "later" sounded mature.

Legal said "later" sounded discoverable.

The junior adviser suggested "phased forward."

Everyone disliked it, which meant it was viable.

## CLIMAX

The afternoon press conference was meant to show the government had one clear priority.

The backdrop said DELIVERY.

The lectern note said GROWTH.

The Prime Minister's folder said FOCUS.

The autocue said OPPORTUNITY, because Education had uploaded a revision during the walk-on.

Communications whispered that the Prime Minister should say "our priority is delivery through growth."

Policy whispered that opportunity needed to be included.

Treasury whispered that supply should not be promised unless someone had counted something.

Legal whispered that focus was safest because it had no measurable endpoint.

The Prime Minister stepped up and said: "Our priority is a focused delivery of growth, opportunity, and supply, with access at the heart of the mission."

Behind the curtain, Digital watched the turnstile dashboard light up red.

The system had detected a multi-priority surge.

A warning appeared on the projector feed, replacing the word DELIVERY with:

PLEASE PRESENT ONE PRIORITY AT A TIME.

A reporter asked whether the government was unable to choose.

The Prime Minister said choosing was exactly what the government was doing.

The warning refreshed:

CHOICE NOT DETECTED.

For eight seconds, the country experienced a fully automated clarification.

Then Communications unplugged the projector.

The turnstile admitted "damage limitation" by default.

## TAG

By evening, the National Priority Turnstile had been hailed as a major step forward in sequencing ambition.

Departments were asked to submit tomorrow's single priority by close of play.

Eleven departments submitted "coordination."

Treasury submitted "restraint."

The Cabinet Secretary submitted a request for a second turnstile.
