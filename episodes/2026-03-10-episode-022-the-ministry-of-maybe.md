# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 022  
**Title:** The Ministry of Maybe  
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

**INT. CABINET BRIEFING ROOM – 07:02**

A digital sign above the lectern reads:

> **TODAY’S POSITION: CONFIDENTLY FLEXIBLE**

A junior adviser updates a slide deck titled `FINAL_POSITION_v9_USE_THIS_ONE.pptx`.

A senior adviser leans in.

**SENIOR ADVISER:**
Is this the final final, or the temporary final?

**JUNIOR ADVISER:**
Temporary final. The durable final is due at nine.

The Prime Minister enters, scanning briefing cards labelled:

- YES (subject to review)
- NO (pending options)
- MAYBE (preferred)

---

## ACT ONE

The government unveils a new decision model called **Adaptive Certainty**.

At the morning press briefing, the spokesperson explains:

- “We are not indecisive.”
- “We are timing-sensitive.”
- “A maybe today is a stronger yes tomorrow.”

Reporters exchange looks usually reserved for delayed trains and inherited spreadsheets.

Inside headquarters, departments receive guidance:

1. Avoid “definitive language” before lunchtime.
2. Replace “approved/rejected” with “directionally aligned/not yet aligned.”
3. Log every maybe in the **National Maybe Register**.

By 10:30, the register already has color coding:

- Green maybe (optimistic)
- Amber maybe (strategic)
- Red maybe (please stop asking)

---

## ACT TWO

The Treasury asks whether a major funding package is happening.

Cabinet Office replies: “Yes-adjacent.”

Health asks if emergency staffing requests are approved.

Reply: “Operationally likely.”

Transport asks whether weekend rail upgrades should proceed.

Reply: “Proceed in principle, pause in practice.”

An analyst builds a dashboard to track policy confidence:

> **CERTAINTY METER: 47% AND VIBRATING**

Every time a minister says “to be clear,” the number drops.

Meanwhile, opposition figures claim the government is “running the country on weather forecasts.”

Government backbenchers defend the strategy as “responsive realism.”

Nobody agrees on what that means, but it polls surprisingly well among people who are tired.

---

## ACT THREE

Afternoon committee meeting.

Agenda item one:

> **Should the government make fewer maybes?**

Discussion points:

- Too many maybes cause confusion.
- Too few maybes remove flexibility.
- The right number of maybes is “contextual.”

A minister proposes a cap: three maybes per policy area.

Another proposes maybe credits, tradable between departments.

The room goes silent.

Then someone asks if the credits can roll over to next quarter.

Finance writes “potentially” in the margin.

---

## CLIMAX

Evening press conference.

A reporter asks a direct question:

**REPORTER:**
Will energy bills fall this year: yes or no?

The Prime Minister steps up, calm and careful.

**PRIME MINISTER:**
People deserve honesty. The honest answer is: we’re working on outcomes, not slogans. Some measures are already in motion, others depend on markets and delivery pace. We won’t promise what we can’t guarantee.

For the first time all day, the room settles.

No spin, no verbal smoke machine.

Just an answer with limits.

Commentators call it “refreshingly adult.”

The communications team calls it “a controlled exposure to clarity.”

---

## TAG

**INT. CABINET OFFICE – 22:14**

A printer hums out tomorrow’s draft plan:

> **ADAPTIVE CERTAINTY 2.0 — CLEARER MAYBES, FASTER**

Underneath:

> “Pilot scheme: replace maybe with ‘probably’ in low-risk statements.”

A handwritten note appears at the bottom:

> “Check if ‘probably’ sounds too decisive.”

Lights out.
