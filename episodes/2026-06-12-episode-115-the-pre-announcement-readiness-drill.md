# Cabinet Chaos Episode 115: The Pre-Announcement Readiness Drill

## Cold Open
At 07:00, the Cabinet Office convened an emergency meeting to prepare for the possible announcement of an announcement that had not yet been approved for pre-announcement.

The Prime Minister arrived carrying the "Agenda Certainty" folder, now reinforced with a binder clip borrowed from the Department for Stationery Resilience. Inside was a single sheet headed: "Do not circulate until circulation has been agreed."

An official explained that the government needed to show momentum without accidentally creating a destination. The room accepted this as a sensible compromise between leadership and not finding out too much at once.

## The Briefing
The morning briefing focused on whether the public could be told that ministers were preparing to tell them something. Communications called it "visible readiness." Policy called it "sequenced transparency." Delivery asked if anyone knew what the eventual thing was.

The Minister for Assurance said the important point was not the content of the announcement, but the confidence that an announcement-shaped process existed somewhere behind the scenes. The Minister for Stakeholders proposed a listening exercise to confirm whether people preferred news before lunch, after lunch, or in the safer category of "forthcoming."

By the fourth slide, the draft line read: "The government is preparing the conditions for a timely update on the work under way to establish the basis for future action."

Legal said it was robust. Communications said it was clear enough to brief against. Delivery said it had the rare advantage of being impossible to miss.

## The Complication
Trouble began when the media grid team discovered three departments had reserved the same Tuesday for separate moments of decisive activity. Health had pencilled in a "refresh." Transport had booked a "reset." The Cabinet Office had blocked the day for a "directional milestone."

Nobody wanted to move, because moving would imply the original date had existed in a meaningful way. The National Calendar Harmonisation Unit was summoned and immediately asked for more time to understand the urgency.

The Prime Minister suggested combining all three into one cross-government announcement. This was judged ambitious until someone noticed the departments disagreed on whether the public should be reassured, energised, consulted, or gently prepared for another review.

To resolve the matter, officials created a Pre-Announcement Readiness Drill. Its purpose was to test whether the system could withstand the possibility of clarity without generating uncontrolled expectations.

## The Spin Room
By lunchtime, the official line was that no announcement had been delayed because no announcement had yet reached the status of being delayable.

Reporters asked why ministers were holding a podium rehearsal if there was nothing to announce. The spokesperson said, "Readiness is part of responsible government." Asked whether the public should expect news, the spokesperson replied, "Expectation is one of the factors we are managing responsibly."

The phrase "responsible readiness" spread quickly. Ministers used it in interviews, backbenchers used it in newsletters, and one departmental account posted a graphic reading "Preparing To Proceed" before deleting it on the grounds that proceeding had not been collectively cleared.

In the spin room, aides practised answering the question "What is happening?" with six different tones, none of which involved a noun.

## Corridor Conversations
In the corridor, the Chancellor asked whether a pre-announcement counted as spending. Finance said it depended whether hope had a unit cost. The Treasury observer wrote this down and underlined "hope" with evident concern.

Policy advisers debated the difference between "soon," "shortly," and "in due course." Communications preferred "shortly" because it sounded brisk. Legal preferred "in due course" because it came with its own fog. Delivery suggested "today," which caused the Commitment Alert System to briefly hum.

Meanwhile, the paper diary minister tried to draw a flowchart. It began with "Decision?" and immediately split into "announce," "pre-announce," "scope," "pause," and "ask whether decision is a hostile framing." The final box read: "Return to first box with confidence."

## Closing Beat
By evening, the Pre-Announcement Readiness Drill had been declared a success because every department had successfully prepared to be ready without prematurely becoming ready.

The Prime Minister praised the exercise as proof that government could move at pace while preserving the option to define pace later. The Minister for Assurance said the public would appreciate the discipline. The Minister for Delivery said the public might appreciate a delivery date. The room agreed this was useful feedback for a future feedback moment.

In the final scene, an aide updated tomorrow's agenda. Item one read: "Announce whether announcement may be announced." Item two read: "If not, celebrate readiness privately."
