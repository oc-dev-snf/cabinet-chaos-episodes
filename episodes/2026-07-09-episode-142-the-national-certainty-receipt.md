# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 142
**Title:** The National Certainty Receipt
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 06:49, the government discovered that nobody could prove it had sounded certain yesterday.

Communications said the Prime Minister had sounded clear.

Policy said clear was not the same as certain.

Legal said certainty was admissible if recorded, qualified, and not contradicted by breakfast.

Treasury asked whether certainty carried a cost.

The Cabinet Secretary asked for the transcript.

Digital provided three versions, each marked final, two marked corrected, and one marked "final-final pending tone."

The Prime Minister read the key line aloud.

"We expect to make progress shortly."

The room winced.

Shortly was an old word. It had survived four administrations, two restructures, and one official attempt to replace it with "within a meaningful horizon."

Communications said the problem was not the sentence. It was the lack of proof that the sentence had ever been meant confidently.

By 07:06, the Cabinet Secretary had proposed the National Certainty Receipt: a small, auditable confirmation issued after every statement, showing what the government believed it had definitely said, what it might have implied, and which parts were confidence-shaped rather than confidence itself.

The Prime Minister liked it.

It sounded like accountability with a barcode.

## ACT ONE

The first argument was about what counted as receipt-worthy.

Policy said only major announcements should receive receipts.

Communications said minor announcements were where uncertainty bred.

Legal said anything published could become major if a reporter used the phrase "questions are now being asked."

Treasury said receipts should be limited to statements with fiscal exposure.

The Cabinet Secretary asked how one identified fiscal exposure in advance.

Treasury said by feeling a chill.

Digital displayed the draft receipt template:

Statement Made: Yes.

Level of Certainty: Managed.

Confidence Owner: To be confirmed.

Expected Next Step: Under active consideration.

Expiry of Certainty: Close of play, subject to events.

The Prime Minister asked whether "managed" sounded strong enough.

Communications suggested "firmly managed."

Legal objected that firmly suggested pressure.

Policy suggested "balanced."

Treasury liked balanced because it implied nothing was moving.

The junior adviser asked whether a receipt for certainty was itself a promise.

The room paused.

The Cabinet Secretary said it was not a promise. It was evidence that a promise had not yet been selected.

Everyone wrote that down, then immediately asked whether it was on the record.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments had begun issuing certainty receipts.

Health issued one for a capacity statement and selected High confidence, operationally dependent.

Transport issued one for an infrastructure statement and selected High confidence, weather permitting.

Education issued one for a skills statement and selected High confidence, timetable adjacent.

Culture issued one for a museum announcement and selected High confidence, unless the plaque was the wrong size.

The system flagged all four as inconsistent uses of high.

Communications said high was a feeling.

Digital said high was a dropdown.

Legal said feelings in dropdowns were discoverable.

Treasury asked whether the dropdown could default to moderate.

Policy said moderate sounded weak.

Treasury said weak sounded affordable.

The Cabinet Secretary created five new certainty levels: Certain, Practically Certain, Publicly Certain, Internally Certain, and Certain Enough for Wednesday.

The Prime Minister asked which one applied to the afternoon statement.

Policy said Practically Certain.

Communications said Publicly Certain.

Legal said Internally Certain, privately.

Treasury asked whether Wednesday could be moved.

Digital tried to add all five levels to the receipt, but the form rejected the combination as "over-confident ambiguity."

For seven minutes, the government had no approved way to be unsure.

## ACT THREE

At 12:22, the first receipt leaked.

It was attached to a statement about a new delivery partnership and showed the certainty level as "Certain Enough for Wednesday."

Unfortunately, the leak occurred on Thursday.

Communications said the line was being taken out of temporal context.

Legal asked whether temporal context had been cleared.

Policy said the partnership itself remained valid.

Treasury asked whether the partnership had partners.

Operations said partners had been identified.

Treasury asked whether they had agreed.

Operations said they had been given an opportunity to align.

The Prime Minister asked for a correction.

Communications drafted: "The certainty receipt was issued correctly at the time."

Legal said that made the current position sound incorrect.

Policy drafted: "The government remains confident in the broader direction."

Treasury said broader direction was the most expensive kind.

The junior adviser suggested: "The receipt reflects the certainty available when certainty was receipted."

The Cabinet Secretary looked at it for a long time.

It was horrible, but structurally sound.

They approved it for holding use.

## CLIMAX

The afternoon lobby briefing was meant to reassure everyone that certainty receipts were not evidence of uncertainty.

The spokesperson stood beside a screen showing a sample receipt with every field carefully blank except the logo.

A reporter asked why the public needed a receipt for statements that were supposed to be true.

Communications advised: "Transparency."

Legal advised: "Process."

Treasury advised: "No refunds."

The spokesperson said the receipts showed the government was serious about giving people confidence in what had been said.

Another reporter asked whether that meant people should not have been confident before.

Policy advised: "Historical confidence remains valid."

Legal advised: "Do not validate history."

Communications advised: "Say this is about the future."

The spokesperson said: "This is about the future of confidence."

The receipt system immediately generated a new receipt.

Statement Made: Yes.

Level of Certainty: Aspirational.

Confidence Owner: Unassigned.

Expected Next Step: Further confidence.

Expiry of Certainty: Pending clarification.

The screen was still connected to the projector.

For a moment, the room experienced total clarity.

Then the Cabinet Secretary leaned into the control-room microphone and asked whether total clarity required a receipt.

Digital clicked yes.

The system crashed.

## TAG

By evening, the National Certainty Receipt had been described as a major step forward in accountable reassurance.

Departments were instructed to attach receipts to all statements, corrections, clarifications, non-clarifications, and denials of confusion.

Legal added a field called "Meaning At Time Of Saying."

Treasury added a field called "Confidence Already Spent."

Communications added a printable sticker reading CERTAINTY RECORDED.

The junior adviser found one stuck to the office kettle.

Nobody knew who had put it there.

Nobody wanted to issue a receipt.
