# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 066
**Title:** The Urgent Font Working Group
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

(CABINET OFFICE, 06:58. A projector displays two nearly identical briefing packs. One is in Arial. One is in Calibri. The room is tense.)

Morning briefing card:

**COMMUNICATION CLARITY SPRINT — DAY 1**  
**Scenario:** *"The Urgent Font Working Group"*

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Why are there two versions of the emergency guidance?

**PRIVATE SECRETARY:** Policy prefers Arial for authority.

**COMMS LEAD:** Digital prefers Calibri for readability.

**OPERATIONS DIRECTOR:** Frontline teams prefer whichever one arrives before lunch.

Beat.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** So national resilience currently depends on typography.

CUT TO TITLE.

## ACT ONE — THE BATTLE OF LEGIBILITY

Strategy room, 08:22. Whiteboard title: **FAST, CLEAR, CONSISTENT**.

**PM:** We need one format by noon.

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** That everyone can live with.

**DESIGN ADVISER:** And that doesn't break on older laptops.

**ACCESSIBILITY LEAD:** And that works for dyslexic readers and screen magnification.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Wonderful. A simple task with only twelve non-negotiables.

At 09:05, they map the actual problem:

1. Too many templates in circulation
2. No owner for final formatting decisions
3. Last-minute edits creating version forks
4. Guidance arriving as attachments instead of plain text summaries
5. No standard for urgency labels

**PM:** Which part is political risk?

**OPERATIONS DIRECTOR:** Delay caused by avoidable confusion.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Finally. A measurable enemy.

## ACT TWO — THE TEMPLATE SUMMIT

Ops floor, 11:17. Slide heading: **NATIONAL BRIEF FORMAT v1.0**.

**ANALYST:** We tested five templates with twelve teams.

**PM:** Results?

**ANALYST:** Fastest comprehension came from the shortest one.

**COMMS LEAD:** And the one with plain-English headings.

**ACCESSIBILITY LEAD:** Plus high contrast and bigger line spacing.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** So clarity remains undefeated. Someone inform tradition.

The group agrees a new structure for urgent notices:

**HEADER:** what happened, when, who is affected  
**ACTION BOX:** exactly what to do in the next two hours  
**DETAILS:** what is known, unknown, and next update time  
**OWNER LINE:** named official for decisions and corrections

At 12:02, first pilot issue is sent in the new format.

**REGIONAL MANAGER (call):** We understood it instantly.

**SERVICE OWNER:** Any confusion?

**REGIONAL MANAGER:** Only that this wasn't done months ago.

## ACT THREE — THE CONSULTATION ON STOPPING CONSULTATION

Cross-department meeting, 14:41. Agenda item: **"Reduce document churn"**.

**FACILITATOR:** Ground rule: no sentence can start with "just one small tweak."

Murmurs of resistance.

Findings from teams:

- 60% of edits happen after factual sign-off
- Most late edits are stylistic, not operational
- Staff trust guidance more when update times are explicit
- Conflicting document names cause avoidable rework

**PM:** What's the practical fix?

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** Single naming format and cut-off windows.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Put it on posters. Put it on mugs. Put it on people if needed.

Breaking ticker appears:

**"Government pilots plain-language emergency brief standard."**

**COMMS LEAD:** That's broadly accurate.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** In this building, "broadly accurate" is a cherished milestone.

## ACT FOUR — THE 16:30 FREEZE

Decision room, 16:29. Board reads: **ONE BRIEF, ONE VERSION, ONE OWNER**.

New protocol approved:

- Template locked at 16:30 daily
- Edits after lock require risk justification
- Each guidance note includes a unique ID
- Latest version always published to one canonical page
- Attachments banned for urgent updates unless legally required

By 18:11:

- Four parallel templates retired
- Average read time reduced by 37%
- Duplicate clarification calls cut in half
- Night teams report cleaner handover notes

**PM:** Did we simplify government?

**OPERATIONS DIRECTOR:** For one process, yes.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** That's how reform starts: small victory, no parade, fewer PDFs.

## CLIMAX — THE BRIEFING

Press room, 19:06.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER (at podium):**  
Today we introduced a single urgent brief format focused on speed, clarity, and accessibility. We did not remove challenge or scrutiny. We removed preventable ambiguity.

**REPORTER:** Is this just a branding exercise?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Branding is what you call readability when it works.

**REPORTER:** Will this solve every communication failure?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** No. But it solves one very common one: people getting five near-identical documents and none of them first.

## TAG

21:02. Quiet office. A junior policy officer updates the intranet style guide.

New line added under **Urgent Comms**:

**"If recipients need a call to understand paragraph one, rewrite paragraph one."**

A nearby printer sleeps through the hour.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER (walking past):** Remarkable. Administrative progress you can hear.

Silence. Then one calm notification: **"Update acknowledged by all regions."**
