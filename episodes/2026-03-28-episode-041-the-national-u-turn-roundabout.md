# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 041
**Title:** The National U-Turn Roundabout
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

A giant vinyl board appears in the press room:

**"DELIVERY ROUTE MAP: STRAIGHT LINES ONLY"**

MINISTER: Today we end policy zig-zags. We are now a straight-line government.

MALCOLM: Wonderful. Shame the real world has corners.

GLENN points at the map.

GLENN: Why are there seven arrows going in opposite directions?

MALCOLM: That's not contradiction, Glenn. That's optional consistency.

---

## ACT ONE

### 1) The morning certainty

At 8:00 a.m., a memo lands in every inbox:

**"NO U-TURNS. CONFIDENCE THROUGH CONTINUITY."**

By 9:10 a.m., three departments request "minor directional flexibility."

NICOLA: We've banned U-turns and invented elegant reversing.

MALCOLM: It's not reversing if you call it "forward-facing recalibration."

### 2) The terminology sprint

A cross-government language group is formed to replace the phrase "change of mind."

Approved alternatives include:
- strategic lane adjustment
- phased route correction
- evidence-responsive trajectory update

GLENN: That's all just "we got it wrong," in business-class seating.

MALCOLM: Exactly. Same destination, better legroom.

---

## ACT TWO

### 1) The stakeholder carousel

Advisers cycle through meetings with unions, councils, industry groups, and backbench committees.

Each group asks for a different policy shape. Everyone describes their request as "the stable option."

NICOLA: We have six incompatible versions of stability.

MALCOLM: Democracy, Nicola. It's chaos with minutes.

### 2) The dashboard compromise

The data team launches a tracker titled:

**"POLICY CONSISTENCY MONITOR (BETA)"**

New status labels:
- On Course
- On Course with Context
- On Course Pending Earlier Course

GLENN: "Pending Earlier Course" is a U-turn.

MALCOLM: No, it's respect for historical routing.

### 3) The rehearsal disaster

The minister rehearses the afternoon statement:

- "We have not changed direction."
- "We have refined the route."
- "The destination remains unchanged, with updated milestones."

A special adviser suggests adding one plain-English line.

MINISTER: Fine. "Some details are different because reality happened."

Room falls silent.

MALCOLM: Congratulations. You've accidentally spoken human.

---

## ACT THREE

### 1) The paper nobody expected

Late evening, an exhausted policy drafter submits a one-page note with four columns:
- What we said last month
- What changed
- Why it changed
- What happens next

No metaphors. No heroic verbs. No defensive adjectives.

GLENN: I understood every sentence. Is this allowed?

NICOLA: Only if we hide it behind a glossy foreword.

MALCOLM: Frame it as bold transparency. People forgive changes; they hate being gaslit.

### 2) The launch

Next morning, the minister presents the revised plan.

MINISTER: We listened, we updated, and we're publishing the reasons in full.

Reporters prepare for evasions and get direct answers instead.

MALCOLM (quietly): If this catches on, half the briefing industry collapses.

---

## CLIMAX

A week later, headlines are mixed, opponents still attack, and talk shows still shout.

But officials report fewer circular meetings and faster decisions because everyone can see:
- what changed
- who approved it
- when it will be reviewed again

The system remains noisy, but less performative.

MALCOLM: Turns out a U-turn is survivable. A fake straight line isn't.
