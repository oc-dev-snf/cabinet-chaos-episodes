# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 049
**Title:** The Briefing Clarity Initiative
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

(CABINET BRIEFING ROOM. Three giant binders sit on the table labelled: “SUMMARY,” “EXECUTIVE SUMMARY,” and “SUMMARY OF THE EXECUTIVE SUMMARY.”)

**CALLUM:** Why is today’s briefing taller than the intern?

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** Departments submitted updates on the cost-of-living package.

**COMMS DIRECTOR:** Broadcaster producers say viewers have “briefing fatigue.”

**POLICY DIRECTOR:** We could launch a Briefing Clarity Initiative.

**CALLUM:** Is that where briefings become clear?

**POLICY DIRECTOR:** Eventually. First we define what “clear” means across a cross-government panel.

CUT TO TITLE.

## ACT ONE

A working group presents options:

Option A: one-page decision memos with three required lines — problem, options, recommendation.
Option B: a 70-page guidance manual on concise writing.
Option C: a national campaign called “Clarity Starts With Us.”

**CALLUM:** A is useful. Why does B include an appendix on font psychology?

**POLICY DIRECTOR:** Stakeholders requested completeness.

**CALLUM:** Completeness is not the same as readability.

A budget analyst slides over a note:

**“If teams use a strict decision template and publish assumptions upfront, approval cycles drop by days.”**

The room nods politely, then debates slogan punctuation.

## ACT TWO

Trials begin in two departments.

**SERVICE DIRECTOR:** We tested one-page memos for urgent approvals.

**CALLUM:** Outcome?

**SERVICE DIRECTOR:** Average sign-off time down from nine days to four.

**DIGITAL LEAD:** We can build a dashboard that scores memo clarity from 1 to 10.

**CALLUM:** Can we skip the score and fix bad memos before they leave the room?

**DIGITAL LEAD:** Also possible.

Legal asks for guardrails.

**GOVERNANCE ADVISER:** We need confidence that short memos still capture risk.

**CALLUM:** Fine. Add one section: “What could go wrong and how we’ll spot it.”

**GOVERNANCE ADVISER:** Reasonable.

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** We should train directors to reject waffle on sight.

## ACT THREE

Comms workshop proposes campaign lines:
- “Clarity Is Delivery”
- “Less Paper, More Progress”
- “Briefly Better Government”

**COMMS DIRECTOR:** We can unveil a new logo with a very clean typeface.

**CALLUM:** Lovely. Put it on a one-page policy memo and see if anyone notices.

Callum signs the implementation plan:

1. Mandatory one-page decision memo for all fast-track submissions.
2. Standard assumptions table attached to every fiscal proposal.
3. A weekly “red-pen clinic” for unclear drafts.
4. Published turnaround metrics by department.
5. Automatic return of any memo that uses “holistic journey framework” without a plain-English translation.

**POLICY DIRECTOR:** That fifth rule is aggressive.

**CALLUM:** It’s preventative medicine.

## CLIMAX

Afternoon press statement:

**“Government is simplifying internal decision-making so urgent policies are reviewed faster and explained more clearly.”**

A reporter asks whether this is mostly an internal branding exercise.

**CALLUM:** It began life as one. Then a few civil servants did the dangerous thing and made it practical.

**REPORTER:** Is this anti-detail?

**CALLUM:** No. It’s anti-fog.

## TAG

(EVENING. BRIEFING ROOM. The giant binders are gone. Three one-page memos sit neatly on the table.)

**AIDE:** First week: average approval time down 32% in pilot teams.

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** Fewer emergency late-night clarification calls.

**COMMS DIRECTOR:** Should we still launch the campaign video?

**CALLUM:** Yes. Keep it under 30 seconds. We’re reforming, not directing a trilogy.

A new calendar invite appears:

**Subject:** “Strategic Workshop on Narrative Layering for Concision.”

Callum hovers over “Decline,” selects “Maybe,” and returns to the one-page memos.

**END**
