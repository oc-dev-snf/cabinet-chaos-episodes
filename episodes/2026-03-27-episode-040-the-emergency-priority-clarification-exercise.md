# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 040
**Title:** The Emergency Priority Clarification Exercise
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

In the Cabinet briefing room, a slide flashes:

**"EMERGENCY PRIORITY CLARIFICATION EXERCISE (NOT A REPRIORITISATION)"**

MINISTER: We are not changing priorities. We are clarifying the order in which unchanged priorities will be prioritised.

MALCOLM: So you've invented a queue for slogans.

GLENN squints at the footer.

GLENN: Why does this say "draft final pre-brief rev B"?

MALCOLM: Because clarity, Glenn, is a process nobody wants to finish.

---

## ACT ONE

### 1) The panic memo

After a rough set of morning headlines, Number Ten asks every department to send its "top three absolutely non-negotiable priorities" by 11:00 a.m.

By 10:20, the center receives 47 priorities from 12 departments.

NICOLA: We've asked for triage and got a buffet.

MALCOLM: Government by tasting menu. Tiny portions, huge bill.

### 2) The ranking framework

A taskforce unveils a scoring model called **"PRIME-RANK"**.

Each priority is graded on:
- urgency
- visibility
- implementation friction
- ministerial attachment level

GLENN: "Ministerial attachment level" is just "will someone shout if we move it," right?

MALCOLM: Exactly. Evidence-based emotional forecasting.

---

## ACT TWO

### 1) The workshop of impossible arithmetic

Officials gather for a cross-government session titled **"Doing Less, Better, More"**.

Every table is asked to cut one priority.

Nobody cuts anything. Instead, teams merge priorities into larger phrases:
- "growth-enabled resilience"
- "service-led productivity confidence"
- "delivery-integrated trust renewal"

NICOLA: We've reduced twelve things into three things that each contain twelve things.

MALCOLM: Bureaucratic compression. Like zipping a folder full of regret.

### 2) The dashboard mutiny

A live dashboard appears: **"PRIORITY CONFIDENCE BOARD"**.

Statuses are color-coded:
- Green: progressing
- Amber: progressing with caveats
- Red: progressing in principle

GLENN: Red now means progressing?

MALCOLM: Of course. Failure is bad optics. "Progressing in principle" is failure in a tie.

### 3) The briefing rehearsal

Before the afternoon lobby briefing, the minister practices lines:

- "This is disciplined focus."
- "We are sequencing for impact."
- "Nothing has been dropped; everything has been sharpened."

NICOLA: Could we say some work moved back because teams are finite?

MALCOLM: Certainly, if your career goal is podcasting.

---

## ACT THREE

### 1) The accidental document

At 7:15 p.m., an exhausted policy adviser makes a mistake: they delete every sentence longer than twelve words.

What remains is unexpectedly useful:
- six priorities
- one owner each
- next decision date
- one risk line per item
- one line on what is paused

GLENN: This is the first paper I've read without needing a translator.

NICOLA: It's almost suspiciously honest.

MALCOLM: Keep it down. Transparency spooks the horses.

### 2) The launch nobody can hate

Next morning, the minister presents the short plan as a "new era of delivery discipline."

Journalists ask whether this is a climbdown from last month's "comprehensive everything agenda."

MINISTER: It's continuity through intelligent concentration.

MALCOLM (quietly): That's a yes wearing cufflinks.

---

## CLIMAX

A month later, departments are still arguing, still grandstanding, still trying to rename ordinary project management.

But meetings now end with:
- who decides
- by when
- what stops

The noise is still there; the fog is thinner.

For once, priority-setting resembles choosing.

MALCOLM: We've discovered the radical center of government: a list that fits on one page.

---

## TAG

A private office intern updates the shared tracker tab from:

**"Strategic_Priority_Master_v12_FINAL_FINAL_REAL.xlsx"**

to:

**"Priorities - Current."**

Nobody speaks for a full five seconds.

Then someone starts a slow, respectful clap.
