# Cabinet Chaos — Episode 099: The National Password Reset Assurance Program

*Date: 2026-05-21*

## COLD OPEN
At 7:00 a.m., departments receive a banner alert reading:

**“PASSWORD CONFIDENCE IS PUBLIC CONFIDENCE.”**

Attached are four “urgent” guidance notes, each with different advice on special characters.

By 7:11, an internal poll asks whether an exclamation mark is “friendly” or “aggressive.”

## ACT ONE — THE ANNOUNCEMENT
At the daily briefing, ministers launch the National Password Reset Assurance Program.

The objective: help people regain account access quickly without compromising security or requiring a doctoral thesis in memory recall.

“Losing access to a service should be frustrating for minutes, not days,” says one official.

A journalist asks whether the new process is unified across agencies.

“Aligned in principle,” comes the reply, “with operational variation during transition.”

Another asks for plain English.

“It should work the same way most of the time, and we’ll keep fixing the parts that don’t.”

## ACT TWO — IMPLEMENTATION DAY
Teams roll out a new playbook: *Verify Smart, Recover Fast*.

It sets out four rules:

- Offer more than one secure recovery path.
- Explain lockouts clearly, including when users can try again.
- Don’t ask for information the service already holds.
- Treat accessibility as default, not exception handling.

Pilot services report a 24% reduction in support calls before lunch.

Then one unit introduces a mandatory “security proverb” field “to prove human intent.”

Support calls rise sharply. The field is retired before tea.

## ACT THREE — THE STRESS TEST
At noon, agencies run *Exercise Forgotten Friday*.

Scenario: “A user is on mobile, low battery, and has changed phone number since last year.”

One team adds recovery via verified email plus one-time postal fallback.

Another removes three legacy security questions, including “first concert attended,” after discovering answers were mainly guesses.

A compliance adviser asks if “Please try again later” qualifies as transparent communication.

The room agrees it qualifies as communication, but not transparency.

At 2:53 p.m., a test queue stalls when three systems simultaneously classify “moderate urgency” as both high and low priority.

## ACT FOUR — OVERSIGHT
In Parliament, members welcome the focus on access and safeguards.

“How will you protect people from account takeover while reducing friction?”

A minister replies, “Risk-based checks, clearer signals, and faster human escalation where needed.”

“Will you publish success and failure rates?”

“Yes — recovery completion, abandonment, repeat lockouts, and assisted-support outcomes.”

“Will there be independent review?”

“Yes. External experts will audit both usability and fraud controls each quarter.”

## CLIMAX — THE DASHBOARD DROP
At 6:20 p.m., the first public metrics snapshot appears:

- **Services Onboarded:** 143
- **Average Recovery Time Improvement:** 28%
- **Successful Same-Day Resets:** 81%

Officials call the figures “promising, provisional, and very likely to be footnoted.”

At 6:37, a correction notes that 19 onboarded services were in pilot-only mode.

An amended dashboard follows, plus a new line item labeled **“figures pending validation.”**

## TAG
At 8:06 p.m., a local contact centre changes one sentence:

From: “Authentication attempt unsuccessful.”
To: “That didn’t work — let’s try another secure way.”

Completion improves. Repeat attempts fall. Tempers cool.

At 8:41, a final all-staff message lands:

**“Tomorrow: Shared Session Timeout Standards Workshop.”**
