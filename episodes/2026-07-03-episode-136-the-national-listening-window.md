# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 136
**Title:** The National Listening Window
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the government discovered it had been listening incorrectly.

Not failing to listen. That would imply neglect, which Legal said carried a tone of avoidable knowledge. The problem was that listening had occurred in too many places, at too many times, and occasionally near people with actual complaints.

The Prime Minister asked what correct listening looked like.

Communications said correct listening was visible, contained, and unlikely to generate a transcript. Policy said it should inform decisions. Treasury said decisions should not become dependent on listening, because that created demand pressure. Digital asked whether listening needed a platform.

Everyone looked relieved. Platforms were easier than people.

Strategy proposed a National Listening Window: a daily, clearly signposted period during which the government would demonstrate active engagement with public sentiment without accidentally committing to any of it.

The Cabinet Secretary asked how long the window would stay open.

Communications suggested twenty minutes.

Policy said meaningful listening might require longer.

Treasury asked whether meaningful listening had been costed.

Legal advised that the word meaningful should be used only in documents not intended for release.

By 07:12, the window had been shortened to twelve minutes, renamed a "focused listening opportunity," and placed under review for seasonal variation.

The Prime Minister liked it. It sounded humane, managerial, and brief.

## ACT ONE

The first task was defining who could be heard.

Digital presented a prototype form with one field labelled "Your concern" and a second field labelled "Preferred category of government response." The options were "noted," "understood," "being considered," "already in train," and "outside scope."

Communications asked whether people could select "angry."

Digital said angry was not a response category.

Communications said angry was very much a response category, just not one the government wanted to offer.

Policy suggested allowing free text.

The room reacted as if someone had opened a cupboard and found a select committee inside.

Legal said free text could contain allegations, facts, names, dates, or emotionally persuasive punctuation. Treasury added that free text was expensive to read. Strategy proposed a guided listening experience in which the public selected from pre-approved feelings.

The Prime Minister asked which feelings were approved.

Communications read the list: concerned, uncertain, keen, patient, and constructively disappointed.

"What about furious?" asked a junior official.

The Cabinet Secretary said fury could be captured under "high-energy concern."

Everyone wrote that down, partly because it was useful and partly because it sounded like a gym class for democracy.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments were arguing over the schedule.

Transport wanted the window before commuter complaints became fully formed. Health wanted it after lunch, when people were less likely to phone from waiting rooms. Education requested term-time exemptions. Housing asked whether listening counted if nobody could afford to move near the venue.

Treasury proposed a single cross-government listening window at 10:15, because it was after coffee but before anyone expected an answer.

Digital warned that if every department listened at once, the system might crash.

Communications said a crash during listening could be described as unprecedented engagement.

The Prime Minister asked whether the window would be public.

Strategy said yes, but not discoverable in a way that encouraged volume.

Digital said that was not how public things worked.

Legal said it was how many public things had worked historically.

A draft ministerial line appeared on the screen: "We are opening a new channel for the country to be heard."

The Cabinet Secretary crossed out "heard" and replaced it with "engaged with."

Communications crossed out "channel" and replaced it with "structured opportunity."

Treasury crossed out "opening" and replaced it with "piloting."

The final line read: "We are piloting a structured opportunity for the country to be engaged with."

The Prime Minister said it sounded like a doorbell installed behind glass.

Strategy said that was the correct level of access.

## ACT THREE

At 12:30, the first Listening Window opened.

The homepage displayed a countdown, a neutral photograph of a community centre, and a button marked "Begin Being Heard." Legal objected to the button, so Digital changed it to "Submit Input." Communications objected that input sounded like a plug. Policy suggested "Share View." Treasury asked whether views created storage costs.

The button became "Proceed."

Within two minutes, the window had received 18,000 submissions.

Digital said this was a technical success and an administrative emergency.

Communications asked for the main themes.

Digital said the main themes were housing, waiting times, bills, local services, trust, and one person asking why the button had changed three times.

Strategy asked whether the themes could be grouped under national renewal.

Policy said grouping everything under renewal made it impossible to tell what people had actually said.

Communications said that was why renewal was such a good group.

The Prime Minister wanted one example for the briefing.

Legal advised against specific examples because they might resemble evidence.

Treasury suggested using an anonymised composite concern.

Digital generated one: "A resident in a region feels services should be better in a timeframe."

The room agreed this captured the public mood without risking contact with it.

## CLIMAX

The afternoon briefing began with the spokesperson announcing that the government had listened to thousands of voices.

A reporter asked what the voices had said.

The spokesperson said they had expressed a clear desire to be part of the conversation.

Another reporter asked whether the government would change anything as a result.

The spokesperson said the listening would feed into the next phase of engagement.

A third reporter asked whether the next phase involved action.

In the meeting room, Policy said yes. Treasury said not necessarily. Legal said action was a broad term. Communications said "next phase" should carry the weight.

The spokesperson said the next phase would build on the listening.

The reporter asked what building meant.

Strategy whispered "architecture of trust."

The spokesperson repeated it.

At that moment, the dashboard behind the podium refreshed. The National Listening Window results appeared as a pie chart. Every slice was labelled "Other."

Digital said the categories had failed to load.

Communications said maybe the chart looked inclusive.

The largest slice expanded on the screen and revealed a hover label: "Other: Please stop asking us to classify everything as other."

The room went very still.

The spokesperson turned, read the label, and nodded with public-service dignity.

"That," they said, "is precisely the kind of candid feedback this government is determined to process appropriately."

The Cabinet Secretary asked Digital whether the window could close itself.

Digital said it already had.

## TAG

By evening, the National Listening Window had been declared a successful first step in a longer listening journey.

Departments were instructed to submit weekly listening returns in three categories: views received, views acknowledged, and views whose acknowledgment required further views.

The public dashboard was amended so that future charts could not display labels without clearance from Communications, Legal, and someone emotionally prepared to read them.

The final shot showed the government homepage after midnight.

The button had changed again.

It now read: "Thank you for your patience while listening is refreshed."
