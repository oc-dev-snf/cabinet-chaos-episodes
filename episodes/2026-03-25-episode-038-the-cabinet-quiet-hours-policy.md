# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 038
**Title:** The Cabinet Quiet Hours Policy
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

A fresh sign is taped to the Cabinet Office entrance:

**"QUIET HOURS: 09:00-11:00. NO EMAILS MARKED URGENT UNLESS BUILDING IS ON FIRE."**

MINISTER: This government is leading on focus, wellbeing, and strategic calm.

MALCOLM: Nothing says calm like announcing silence through nine separate press briefings.

GLENN checks his phone.

GLENN: Why have I just had six alerts titled "quiet-hours update"?

MALCOLM: Because peace, Glenn, must be communicated at volume.

---

## ACT ONE

### 1) The productivity crusade

After a report says officials spend more time triaging messages than making decisions, the PM's office launches a **Cabinet Quiet Hours Policy**.

The aims are simple:
- fewer interruptions
- better decisions
- less performative urgency

The implementation is less simple.

Every department requests exemptions for "mission-critical correspondence," including:
- procurement
- media lines
- diary changes
- stationery shortages
- "sensitive vibes"

NICOLA: If everyone is mission-critical, no one is.

MALCOLM: Correct. We've invented a nationwide emergency for people who can't wait ninety minutes.

### 2) The comms interpretation

Comms teams ask how to handle journalists during quiet hours.

Draft guidance says:
1. Do not reply immediately
2. Reply quickly to confirm delayed reply
3. Issue holding line about thoughtful government
4. Escalate if delay becomes a story about delay

GLENN: So we pause work to explain that we're pausing work.

MALCOLM: That's modern governance. Half action, half explanation, all calendar invite.

---

## ACT TWO

### 1) The workaround economy

By day two, workarounds spread across Whitehall.

People start labeling messages creatively:
- "urgent-ish"
- "quiet-hours adjacent"
- "for awareness before panic"
- "not urgent, but emotionally immediate"

One directorate creates a channel called **"Non-Urgent Emergencies."**

NICOLA: We've turned boundaries into a word game.

MALCOLM: Bureaucracy is just Scrabble with pension contributions.

### 2) The dashboard of silence

A performance dashboard appears: **"FOCUS COMPLIANCE INDEX."**

Metrics include:
- emails sent during quiet hours
- messages reopened within five minutes
- percentage of staff marked "temporarily serene"

Departments compete to appear most disciplined.

Soon, teams schedule emails for 11:01 while continuing frantic backchannel chats on unofficial threads.

GLENN: Are we calmer, or just quieter on paper?

MALCOLM: We have achieved acoustic reform. Substance remains under review.

### 3) The staff reality check

An internal staff forum gives practical feedback:
- Keep quiet hours
- Make exemption rules short
- Stop scoring teams like a league table
- Trust managers to use judgment

A deputy secretary summarizes: "People don't hate focus time. They hate theatrical focus time."

NICOLA: That's the line. Put that in the minister's brief.

MALCOLM: Can't. It's clear, honest, and under thirty words.

---

## ACT THREE

### 1) Emergency policy simplification

After a morning where three departments simultaneously claim "critical exemption" for printer toner, lunch catering, and a typo in a tweet draft, senior officials meet to rewrite the policy.

The new version fits on one page:
- Quiet hours apply by default
- Exemptions only for legal, safety, or live operational incidents
- One named approver per exemption
- Publish weekly totals, no rankings

GLENN: That's... actually readable.

MALCOLM: Don't worry, someone will try to add an annex.

### 2) The ministerial climbdown-that-isn't

At the afternoon briefing, the minister says the policy has "evolved through active listening."

Reporters ask if that means the original version was overengineered.

MINISTER: We are refining delivery in real time.

MALCOLM (muttering): Translation: we removed eighty percent of the nonsense and called it phase two.

---

## CLIMAX

One week later, message volume during quiet hours drops sharply.

Decision notes improve.
Meetings end on time.
Fewer people type "quick one" before sending seven paragraphs.

There are still hiccups, and the odd dramatic exemption request, but the basic rule now works because everyone understands it.

At the weekly review, no one presents a heatmap.

Just a short paper:
- What improved
- What didn't
- What changes next week

For once, the room discusses work instead of wording.

MALCOLM: Astonishing. We removed complexity and discovered competence lurking underneath.

---

## TAG

A new poster appears in the corridor:

**"If everything is urgent, urgency is meaningless."**

GLENN stares at it, then quietly closes three chat windows.

MALCOLM walks by, taps the poster, and says:

"Frame it. It's the closest thing this building has to scripture."
