# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 141
**Title:** The National Urgency Buffer
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 06:58, the government discovered it had used the word "urgent" on seven unrelated announcements before breakfast.

Flood resilience was urgent.

Skills reform was urgent.

Small business support was urgent.

A consultation on public sector signage was urgent, though only because the sign announcing the consultation had already been printed.

The Prime Minister asked whether everything could be urgent at once.

Policy said technically yes.

Treasury said financially no.

Legal said semantically risky.

Communications said the real problem was not urgency itself, but uncoordinated urgency.

By 07:11, the Cabinet Secretary had convened a meeting to establish the National Urgency Buffer: a central process for holding urgent announcements until the government could decide which ones were urgent enough to remain urgent in public.

The Prime Minister liked the name.

It sounded decisive without requiring movement.

The junior adviser asked whether buffering urgency might make things less urgent.

The room treated this as a philosophical attack.

## ACT ONE

The first dispute was whether the Buffer was a queue, a filter, or a mood.

Operations said a queue would be simplest.

Policy said some urgent things could not wait in a queue because queuing would imply they were not urgent.

Communications proposed a filter.

Legal said filters created evidence that something had been kept out.

Treasury preferred a mood, because moods were cheaper and harder to audit.

The Cabinet Secretary said the public needed to know the government understood urgency.

Communications said the public also needed to know the government was not panicking.

Policy suggested "calm urgency."

Legal asked whether calm urgency was different from delay.

Communications said only if said confidently.

The Prime Minister decided there would be four urgency categories: Immediate, Near-Immediate, Strategically Immediate, and Urgent But Not Today.

Treasury asked whether Immediate implied funding.

Legal asked whether Not Today implied failure.

Communications asked whether Strategically Immediate could be shortened to "nowish."

The Cabinet Secretary wrote "nowish" on the whiteboard, then underlined it once in a way that meant everyone should forget it.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments had begun submitting announcements to the Buffer.

Transport submitted an urgent update on resilience.

Health submitted an urgent update on capacity.

Education submitted an urgent update on opportunity.

Culture submitted an urgent update on a commemorative plaque scheme that had become urgent because the launch venue had double-booked the lectern.

The Buffer immediately overloaded.

Digital said the system had not crashed. It had entered a high-priority reflection state.

Legal said high-priority reflection sounded like negligence with a screensaver.

Treasury asked whether any of the urgent items could be merged.

Communications attempted to combine resilience, capacity, and opportunity into "national preparedness."

Policy objected that preparedness implied something might happen.

Legal objected that something happening was discoverable.

The Prime Minister suggested "national readiness."

The junior adviser quietly searched the archive and found that national readiness had already been used for a different plan, a previous review, and a cancelled dashboard nobody wanted to mention.

Communications said repetition could be continuity.

Treasury said continuity could be spending.

Legal said spending could be a commitment.

The Cabinet Secretary asked for a phrase that meant readiness without implying preparation, money, or history.

After twenty-three seconds, someone from Policy offered "focus."

The room relaxed.

Focus was almost always available.

## ACT THREE

At 11:47, the Prime Minister was due to announce the first policy cleared through the National Urgency Buffer.

The selected announcement concerned a cross-government effort to improve local delivery confidence.

It had been chosen because every department could claim involvement and nobody could describe the mechanism quickly enough to create a contradiction.

The speech began with the line: "This government is focused on urgent action."

The Buffer flagged urgent action as unbuffered urgency.

Communications changed it to "focused action."

Policy said focused action sounded like a task force.

Treasury asked whether there was a task force.

Operations said there was a working group.

Legal said a working group was safer than a task force unless it had minutes.

Operations said it had minutes.

Legal said it was no longer safer.

The Prime Minister asked whether the working group could become a delivery forum.

Communications liked forum.

Treasury said forums required chairs.

The Cabinet Secretary said all groups had chairs.

Treasury said not all chairs were cost-neutral.

The junior adviser replaced the sentence with: "This government is focused."

Everyone stared at it.

It was short, vague, and almost impossible to disprove in real time.

The Prime Minister approved it.

## CLIMAX

The launch event was staged in front of a backdrop reading NATIONAL URGENCY BUFFER: FOCUSING DELIVERY NOW.

Communications had approved the words.

Legal had approved the spacing.

Treasury had approved the backdrop because it was reusable if "urgency" could later be covered with a rectangle.

The first reporter asked what the Buffer actually did.

The spokesperson said it ensured urgent priorities were treated with the seriousness they deserved.

The reporter asked whether that meant some urgent priorities were less urgent than others.

Policy advised: "All priorities remain important."

Legal advised: "Avoid ranking."

Treasury advised: "Avoid promises."

Communications advised: "Say focus."

The spokesperson said: "Our focus is focused."

There was a silence long enough for three departments to update their lines.

A second reporter asked whether the government was delaying urgent action by buffering it.

The spokesperson said the Buffer was not a delay. It was a discipline.

The Buffer flagged discipline as a possible process commitment.

Digital tried to clear the warning.

The dashboard froze and displayed: URGENCY HELD PENDING URGENCY REVIEW.

In the control room, Communications asked whether the screen was visible to cameras.

Operations said probably not.

Legal said probably was not a status.

The Prime Minister leaned toward the microphone and said: "We are acting with calm urgency."

For a moment, everyone thought it had worked.

Then the Buffer accepted "calm," rejected "urgency," and automatically scheduled a follow-up meeting for 06:58 the next morning.
