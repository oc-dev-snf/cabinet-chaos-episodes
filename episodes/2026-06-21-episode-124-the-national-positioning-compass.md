# Cabinet Chaos Episode 124: The National Positioning Compass

## Cold Open
At 07:00, the Cabinet Office discovered that every department had described the same announcement as being "at the heart" of government policy.

The Prime Minister arrived with the Agenda Certainty folder, now fitted with a small handle after Procurement ruled that continued gripping by officials had become a wear-and-tear risk. Inside was a map of the government's priorities, except the map contained no roads, borders, or scale. It was mostly arrows pointing toward a central dot labelled "The Centre."

Communications said the public liked hearing that things were central. Policy said the centre was already crowded. Treasury asked whether the centre had a cost code. Delivery asked how anyone would know when they had arrived there.

The Permanent Secretary announced the National Positioning Compass: a cross-government instrument for establishing where commitments sat in relation to strategy, visibility, readiness, directionality, and the nearest ministerial podium.

## The Briefing
The opening slide read "Spatial Confidence In Public Administration." Beneath it was a compass rose with four points: "At The Heart," "Front And Centre," "Across The Piece," and "In The Mix."

The Minister for Coordination asked whether a policy could be both at the heart and front and centre. Officials confirmed that it could, provided the anatomical and geometric implications were not tested in committee.

Health wanted "at the heart" for prevention. Education wanted "front and centre" for standards. Transport preferred "moving forward," which was ruled out as too literal. Treasury proposed "within the envelope," which everyone agreed was technically a location and emotionally a warning.

By the fourth slide, the compass had developed a fifth point called "Core To The Mission." Legal objected that compasses traditionally had four directions. Strategy replied that modern government needed flexible orientation.

## The Complication
The problem escalated when an aide opened the departmental submissions.

One department had placed its policy "at the centre of the centre." Another had described a pilot scheme as "in the engine room," although nobody could identify the engine or the room. A third had called its consultation "on the runway," prompting Aviation to ask whether takeoff had been approved.

Digital submitted "embedded across the system." The Minister for Delivery liked it until someone asked where the system ended. Treasury asked whether embedded items could be removed without write-off approval. Legal asked whether embedding created permanence. Communications suggested "lightly embedded," which sounded less alarming and more like a sofa cushion.

Then the Prime Minister noticed that "at the heart" had been used for seventeen priorities in the same weekly grid. The Cabinet Office proposed resolving this through a Heart Allocation Framework. Delivery asked whether this was a metaphor. The room agreed it was safest to treat it as both a metaphor and a governance model.

## The Spin Room
By lunchtime, reporters were asking whether the government had too many hearts.

The spokesperson took the podium with a printed diagram and the expression of someone who had been told not to point at it unless necessary.

"This is about making sure priorities are properly located," they said. "The public rightly expects clarity about what is central, what is core, what is across the piece, and what is being taken forward in a balanced way."

Asked which priority was most central, the spokesperson said the government's approach was centred on the public. Asked where the public appeared on the compass, they said the public was not a point but a guiding principle. Asked whether the compass worked, they said it was not designed for navigation.

The press team prepared three lines. For breakfast television: "We know where we are going." For radio: "This is focus." For online: "No priorities have been downgraded." Legal added a fourth: "Spatial terms are illustrative."

## Corridor Conversations
In the corridor, the Chancellor asked whether being central meant getting funding first. Treasury said centrality was an important factor in prioritisation but not as important as affordability, deliverability, or the mysterious column marked "later."

The Minister for Delivery argued that the public might prefer a simple list. A senior official said lists were useful but could create hierarchy, and hierarchy could create questions, and questions could create answers. The list was therefore renamed a "positioning sequence."

The paper diary minister suggested putting the priorities on a noticeboard and drawing a circle around the important ones. Communications liked the visual energy. Legal asked whether the circle implied exclusion. Policy proposed a dotted oval, pending equality screening.

Meanwhile, an official found an old "Delivery Dashboard" archived under "Strategic Location Products." It showed twelve initiatives in the green zone, seven in amber, and one in a box labelled "elsewhere." Nobody knew what elsewhere meant, but it sounded usefully calm.

## Closing Beat
By evening, the National Positioning Compass issued interim guidance. Departments could continue to say priorities were central, provided they clarified whether central meant operationally central, politically central, rhetorically central, or central in the sense of being nearby in a document.

The Prime Minister recorded a clip saying the government had a clear sense of direction. Communications called it reassuring. Delivery asked which direction. Treasury asked whether the direction had been costed. Legal approved the wording after adding that a sense of direction did not guarantee movement.

In the final scene, the Agenda Certainty folder was placed on the table beside the new compass.

The compass needle spun once, considered the available options, and settled on "Under Review."

**Episode Notes**
- Tone: Satirical but even-handed; the joke stays on institutional positioning language and the way metaphors become management systems.
- Running theme: The machinery becomes more certain about where priorities are said to sit than how they move.
- Continuity hook: The Agenda Certainty folder now contains visibility, directionality, receipts, readiness, approved action vocabulary, and a compass that points to review.
