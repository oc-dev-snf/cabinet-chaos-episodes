# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 026  
**Title:** The Consultation on Whether to Consult  
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

**INT. CABINET STRATEGY SUITE – 07:00**

A slide deck fills the screen:

> **PUBLIC CONSULTATION PLAN (PRE-CONSULTATION PHASE)**

Under it: seventeen sub-workstreams.

**POLICY DIRECTOR:**
Before we ask the public what they think, we should ask stakeholders how best to ask the public what they think.

**MINISTER:**
Have we asked stakeholders?

**POLICY DIRECTOR:**
Not yet. We need to consult internally on stakeholder consultation readiness.

A junior aide raises a hand.

**AIDE:**
Would it be quicker to just publish the question?

Silence. Three people type “interesting challenge” into their notes.

---

## ACT ONE

At 07:15, government promises a “straightforward national listening exercise” on transport reform.

By 07:40, four units have produced four draft consultation questions:

- “How can we improve outcomes?”
- “Which outcomes should be improved?”
- “Do you support improvement in principle?”
- “Please define transport.”

By 08:30, radio hosts ask when the public can respond.

Official line:

> “Engagement will begin following completion of engagement design.”

An interdepartmental taskforce is formed to simplify the process.

Its first output is a diagram with arrows pointing to a box labelled:

> **PHASE ZERO-PLUS: DECIDE THE DECISION PATHWAY**

---

## ACT TWO

Committee room. Cross-party hearing.

A member asks how many consultation drafts exist.

**SENIOR OFFICIAL:**
Depends whether tracked changes by mood count as distinct versions.

Another asks who owns the final question.

**SENIOR OFFICIAL:**
Collectively, through shared accountability.

**MEMBER:**
Meaning?

**SENIOR OFFICIAL:**
No single office can be blamed in isolation.

Meanwhile, the consultation webpage goes live with a timer reading:

> **PUBLIC INPUT OPENS SOON**

At noon, “soon” is updated to “very soon.”
At 14:00, it becomes “imminently soon.”
At 16:30, it reads “shortly, subject to final alignment.”

Journalists create a live tracker.

Categories include:

- “Question exists but not agreed”
- “Agreed but not signed”
- “Signed but being simplified”
- “Simplified but under legal comfort review”

By evening, all categories are active at once.

---

## ACT THREE

Emergency evening meeting.

Agenda:

1. Publish one clear question.
2. Keep legal confidence.
3. Avoid sounding like a crossword clue.

A civil servant proposes:

> “What transport changes would help you most in daily life?”

Room goes quiet.

Legal asks whether “help” implies guaranteed delivery.
Finance asks whether “most” creates ranking liabilities.
Comms asks if “daily life” excludes weekends.

A deputy minister sighs.

**DEPUTY MINISTER:**
Can we be understandable for thirty seconds without opening a constitutional incident?

A compromise is drafted:

> “What practical transport improvements would make everyday journeys better for you?”

No one loves it. Everyone can live with it.

It is immediately sent for “final final review.”

---

## CLIMAX

Night briefing, 21:48.

A reporter asks:

**REPORTER:**
Why did publishing one question take all day?

The Minister answers carefully.

**MINISTER:**
Because we treated clarity as the last step instead of the first. We can do better.

They hold up one page:

- The consultation question
- The opening date
- The closing date
- Where responses go
- When results will be published

No slogan. No maze. Just details.

For the first time, follow-up questions are about transport, not process.

## TAG

**INT. POLICY FLOOR KITCHENETTE – 22:17**

A sticky note is taped to the coffee machine:

> “If people can’t understand it, it isn’t finished.”

Below it, in different handwriting:

> “Approved for pilot clarity implementation.”

A third note appears beneath both:

> “Please consult before widening rollout.”
