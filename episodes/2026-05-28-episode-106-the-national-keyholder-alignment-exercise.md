# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 106
**Title:** The National Keyholder Alignment Exercise
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

A facilities manager unlocks a cabinet marked **EMERGENCY COMMS MATERIALS**.

Inside are:
- Six obsolete briefing templates
- Three unopened packs of branded mugs
- A laminated flowchart titled **WHO HAS THE SPARE KEY TO THE KEY CABINET**

The flowchart points to another cabinet.

Cut to opening titles.

## ACT ONE

After a minor delay in releasing a routine statement, the administration launches the **National Keyholder Alignment Exercise**.

Official aim: make sure every critical cabinet, room, and drawer has clear key ownership.

Unspoken aim: stop weekly arguments that begin with “Who signed this out?”

A central register is created with required fields:
- Asset name
- Primary keyholder
- Backup keyholder
- Backup to the backup keyholder
- Escalation path if all keyholders are in meetings

Within hours, submissions reveal four incompatible ownership models:
- Named individual responsibility
- Team-level shared custody
- Rotating duty based on calendar week
- “Historically held by Pat” (Pat retired last year)

Comms describes this as “a rich operational inheritance.”

## ACT TWO

To standardize practice, officials run a simulation called **Key Day**.

Every department receives the same scenario: retrieve a document from a locked cabinet in under four minutes.

Results vary.

One unit completes the task in ninety seconds by taping the key to a clipboard labeled **DO NOT MOVE**.

Another completes in twelve minutes after discovering the keyholder list exists in a locked drawer requiring the same missing key.

A policy team submits an incident report that reads:
“Delay was procedural, not physical. Access intent remained high throughout.”

## ACT THREE

Pressure rises when journalists ask whether any secure materials were ever inaccessible.

Ministers answer carefully: access was “temporarily sequenced.”

The exercise team proposes three reforms:
1. One plain-language key map per floor
2. Quarterly key audits with live sign-off
3. A single escalation number that routes to an actual person

A steering group objects to the word “single,” suggesting “coordinated plurality” to preserve departmental flexibility.

By late afternoon, the reform paper is retitled:
**Framework for Distributed Keyholder Coordination (Interim Draft, Confirmed Tentatively)**

## CLIMAX

At the evening briefing, the spokesperson announces success:

“We now have full visibility of key governance and clear pathways for rapid access.”

A reporter asks how many locks still have unknown backup owners.

An adviser whispers a number, then another adviser whispers a different number.

The spokesperson says, “The count is converging.”

Another reporter asks who holds the master key for the emergency cabinet.

After a pause, the spokesperson replies, “That question is currently with Facilities Gold Command.”

## TAG

At 7:42 p.m., staff receive a new document:

**NATIONAL KEYHOLDER ALIGNMENT STANDARD v0.8 (Provisional-Operational)**

- Every key must have two named custodians
- Every custodian must acknowledge custody monthly
- Every lock change must trigger a same-day register update
- If ownership is disputed, temporary ownership defaults to the person nearest the cabinet

Appendix note:

“Master key location available on request via approved request route.”

No request route is listed.

**END**
