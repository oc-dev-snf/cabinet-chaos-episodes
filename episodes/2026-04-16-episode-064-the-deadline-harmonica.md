# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 064
**Title:** The Deadline Harmonica
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

(CABINET OFFICE, 07:00. A monitor displays **"DELIVERY TEMPO: STRETCH"** in cheerful teal.)

Morning briefing card:

**SERVICE IMPROVEMENT PUSH — DAY 14**  
**Scenario:** *"The Deadline Harmonica"*

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Why is this called a harmonica?

**PROGRAMME LEAD:** Because deadlines keep being pushed out and pulled back in.

**PRIVATE SECRETARY:** We've moved the same milestone six times in opposite directions.

**OPERATIONS DIRECTOR:** The teams call it "accordion governance," but comms said that sounded artisanal.

Beat.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Superb. We now run government to the rhythm of a wheezy instrument.

CUT TO TITLE.

## ACT ONE — THE MIRACLE OF FLEXIBLE URGENCY

Delivery suite, 08:11. Wall board title: **COMMITMENT CONSOLIDATION**

**PM:** I need confidence this is landing this month.

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** We promised pace.

**FINANCE LEAD:** We also promised affordability.

**SERVICE OWNER:** We also promised not to break the live system.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Three promises, one calendar. Excellent scheduling fiction.

By 09:28, the plan has become a compromise bouquet:

1. Keep the public launch date, but relabel it "availability window"
2. Bring forward internal testing while extending external assurance
3. Ask departments for faster sign-off, but introduce a new review checkpoint
4. Publish weekly progress updates before agreeing a shared status scale
5. Declare "no surprises" while moving decisions to late-night calls

**PM:** Are we at least using one timeline?

**PROGRAMME LEAD:** Yes.

**PM:** Good.

**PROGRAMME LEAD:** Plus a contingency timeline. And a realistic timeline we don't show externally.

## ACT TWO — CALENDAR FORENSICS

Ops room, 11:03. Slide heading: **MILESTONE DRIFT ANALYSIS v7_FINAL_v2**

**ANALYST:** Milestone A slipped because policy text changed.

**LEGAL ADVISER:** Milestone B slipped because policy text did not change quickly enough.

**TECH LEAD:** Milestone C slipped because two teams interpreted the same sentence differently.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** So our delivery risk is now punctuation.

They build a reset protocol:

**RULE 1:** One owner per milestone, named and visible.

**RULE 2:** Status is binary for core deliverables: done or not done.

**RULE 3:** Date changes require reason code, impact note, and replacement date.

**PM:** Keep it plain. If a normal person can't follow it, it isn't governance.

At 12:22, regional operations joins with field feedback.

**REGIONAL LEAD:** Teams can hit dates if dependencies are frozen for five working days.

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** Five days is politically uncomfortable.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** So is launching an apology tour.

## ACT THREE — THE CROSS-GOVERNMENT TANGLE

Coordination call, 14:17. Forty-three attendees, eleven versions of "just quickly."

**FACILITATOR:** What's causing rework, not delay?

Ground truth from departments:

- Most rework comes from late changes to acceptance criteria
- Parallel approvals often duplicate the same risk checks
- Frontline teams need final wording earlier, even if branding comes later
- Escalations work when they are specific; generic "urgent" labels create noise

**PM (to room):** None of that is dramatic, all of that is useful.

News alert lands:

**"Delivery timetable revised again amid coordination strain."**

**COMMS DIRECTOR:** We can frame this as "responsible sequencing."

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Fine, but only if responsible sequencing is actually happening.

## ACT FOUR — SMALLER PROMISES, STRONGER DELIVERY

Reset meeting, 16:06. New board: **STABLE WINDOW PLAN**

Agreed operating changes:

- Lock acceptance criteria weekly; no mid-week edits without director sign-off
- Replace three layered approvals with one combined decision forum
- Publish one timeline with confidence bands instead of competing calendars
- Introduce a 48-hour dependency check before any date announcement
- Split launch into two clear phases with explicit service scope

By 18:41:

- Two high-risk milestones moved with written rationale and owner sign-off
- Duplicate review pack removed from six departments
- Public update rewritten to include what changed and why
- Out-of-hours decision calls reduced to exception-only

**PM:** Is this slower?

**SERVICE OWNER:** It's steadier.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Steady is what fast looks like after the postmortem.

## CLIMAX — THE BRIEFING

Press room, 19:07.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER (at podium):**  
Today we simplified how deadlines are set and changed. We now have one visible timeline, clear ownership, and explicit rules for date movement.

Some milestones moved. We are publishing those changes with reasons, impacts, and updated delivery windows.

This is less about theatrics and more about predictability.

**REPORTER:** Did you overpromise last week?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** We promised ambition. Today we added control.

**REPORTER:** Can you guarantee no further changes?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** No serious delivery programme can guarantee that. We can guarantee that changes are governed and explained.

## TAG

21:32. Corridor outside comms.

A fresh dashboard tile appears:

**"DATES CHANGED THIS WEEK: 2 (EXPLAINED)"**

**AIDE:** That's not exactly inspiring copy.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** It's legible copy. Inspiration can come after operations.

In the background, a project manager quietly deletes a folder named:

**FINAL_TIMELINE_REAL_FINAL_USE_THIS_ONE**

No ceremony. Just fewer surprises.
