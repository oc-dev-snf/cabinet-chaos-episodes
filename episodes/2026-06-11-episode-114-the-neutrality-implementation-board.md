# Cabinet Chaos Episode 114: The Neutrality Implementation Board

## Cold Open
At 07:00, the Cabinet Office convened an emergency meeting to discuss whether the government had become too enthusiastic about moderation.

The Prime Minister arrived with the "Agenda Certainty" folder, now held shut by three elastic bands and a paperclip labelled "temporary." The first item was a proposal for the Neutrality Implementation Board, a new cross-government body charged with making sure every public statement sounded balanced enough to survive contact with a microphone.

An official asked whether the board itself had a position. The room agreed that having no position was exactly the sort of position it should avoid appearing to have.

## The Briefing
The morning briefing opened with a draft statement that described a new policy as "bold, cautious, urgent, measured, and subject to further reflection." Communications liked the ambition. Legal liked the caveats. Delivery asked if any of the words required anyone to do anything by Friday.

The Minister for Assurance explained that the public wanted clarity without alarm, confidence without certainty, and action without the sort of action that implied previous inaction. The Minister for Stakeholders suggested adding "where appropriate" after every verb.

By the third revision, the policy no longer promised to improve services. It promised to "support the conditions in which service improvement may continue to be considered."

## The Complication
Trouble began when a junior aide noticed that the draft contained the phrase "we will." This triggered the Commitment Alert System, which had been installed after a minister once said "soon" during a radio interview and caused a measurable spike in expectation.

The Neutrality Implementation Board was assembled around a table that had been selected for being neither round nor rectangular in a divisive way. Its first task was to replace all direct language with phrasing that acknowledged complexity while leaving room for subsequent clarification.

"We will fix the backlog" became "The backlog remains an area of active focus."

"The plan starts today" became "Today forms part of the wider planning environment."

"People are frustrated" became "Public experience continues to provide valuable feedback."

## The Spin Room
By lunchtime, the official line was that the government was not watering anything down, merely ensuring the liquid had been appropriately impact assessed.

Reporters asked whether the new board would slow decisions. The spokesperson said, "It will enhance the pace at which decisions are responsibly contextualised." Asked if that meant slower, the spokesperson replied, "Speed is one metric among many."

Within minutes, ministers were praising "responsible contextualisation" on every channel. One backbencher tried to say that voters preferred plain speaking, but the media grid team advised that plainness could be interpreted as a failure to recognise nuance.

## Corridor Conversations
In the corridor, advisers debated whether "fair" sounded too judgmental. Policy suggested "balanced." Communications worried that "balanced" implied the existence of sides. Legal proposed "broadly mindful," which nobody liked but everyone found difficult to object to.

The Chancellor asked whether the Neutrality Implementation Board had a budget. Finance replied that it had not been allocated funding, only "resource-adjacent support." This was welcomed as a strong example of fiscal restraint with operational possibilities.

Meanwhile, the paper diary minister wrote "Say less, mean maybe" in the margin, then underlined "maybe" twice and referred it to the board for tone clearance.

## Closing Beat
By evening, the board had produced a style guide, an escalation tree, and a prohibited words list containing "will," "must," "now," "done," and "simple."

The Prime Minister praised the day as a success because nobody had over-promised. The Minister for Delivery said there was still the matter of under-delivering. The Minister for Assurance said that phrase risked prejudging the outcome of the delivery experience.

In the final scene, an aide pinned tomorrow's agenda to the noticeboard. Item one read: "Confirm whether neutrality has been implemented." Item two read: "If yes, avoid sounding pleased."
