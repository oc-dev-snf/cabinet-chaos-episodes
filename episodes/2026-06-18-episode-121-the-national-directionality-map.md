# Cabinet Chaos Episode 121: The National Directionality Map

## Cold Open
At 07:00, the Cabinet Office gathered to unveil the National Directionality Map, a new cross-government instrument designed to show that the country was moving forward without requiring anyone to agree which way forward was.

The Prime Minister arrived with the "Agenda Certainty" folder, now bulging slightly from yesterday's receipt for the receipt. A junior official had added a compass sticker to the cover, but Legal had covered the needle with a note saying, "Directional claims require ministerial clearance."

The Map itself was projected onto the wall. It contained no roads, borders, destinations, or scale. Instead, it displayed a large arrow labelled "responsible momentum" and a legend explaining that blank areas represented "future clarity opportunities."

This was received well. A map with too many details could become a trap. A map with almost none could become a strategy.

## The Briefing
The morning briefing opened with a slide titled "Direction Without Over-Specification." Officials explained that the Map was not intended to show where policy currently was, where it had been, or where it would end up. Its purpose was to demonstrate directional confidence.

The Minister for Coordination asked whether the arrow pointed north. Geospatial said north was a technical concept. Communications said voters preferred "ahead." Treasury asked whether "ahead" had spending implications. Legal said "ahead" was acceptable if accompanied by a caveat that no undertaking was being made about arrival.

The Permanent Secretary proposed that the arrow point slightly upward, as upward tested better than sideways. Delivery warned that upward might imply improvement. Assurance suggested "constructive incline." Everyone wrote this down.

By the fifth slide, the approved line read: "The National Directionality Map confirms that government is proceeding in a clearly signalled direction, subject to terrain, sequencing, and responsible adjustment."

The room admired the phrase "responsible adjustment." It had the rare quality of sounding like both movement and a brake.

## The Complication
Trouble began when two departments produced incompatible arrows.

One arrow pointed toward "accelerated delivery conditions." The other pointed toward "measured implementation maturity." Both claimed to represent forward motion. Neither would agree to be described as lateral.

An emergency alignment meeting was convened. Policy suggested merging the arrows into a chevron. Communications worried this might look military. Legal worried it might look like a promise. The Minister for Assurance asked whether two arrows could demonstrate plural forwardness.

Delivery asked whether the disagreement revealed that no one had settled the direction. This was considered a useful challenge and moved to the annex.

The mapping team tried rotating the slide until both arrows appeared broadly compatible. Unfortunately, this placed the title upside down. Communications said upside-down directionality could be managed, but only if introduced as a bold new way of looking at progress.

The Prime Minister asked for a version that everyone could point at without pointing against anyone else.

## The Spin Room
By lunchtime, reporters had obtained a leaked draft showing seven arrows, three circles, and a dotted line labelled "pending geography."

The spokesperson denied confusion. "The public understand that modern government requires dynamic directionality," they said. "What matters is not whether every arrow is identical, but whether every arrow has been responsibly considered within the overall map environment."

Asked where the Map led, the spokesperson said it led "towards outcomes." Asked which outcomes, they said the outcomes were "the subject of a separate outcomes pathway." Asked whether the pathway had a map, they said that was exactly the kind of joined-up question the new framework was designed to support.

Advisers prepared a media grid. Monday would be "direction." Tuesday would be "momentum." Wednesday would be "route confidence." Thursday would be held in reserve in case the public asked for a destination.

The reserve line was: "Destinations are strongest when approached carefully."

## Corridor Conversations
In the corridor, the Chancellor asked whether the Map could be fiscally neutral. Treasury said maps were normally capital-light, unless they caused people to go somewhere.

The Minister for Delivery suggested replacing the Map with a short list of decisions, owners, deadlines, and trade-offs. This produced a respectful silence. An official said that while the idea had merit, it risked turning directionality into accountability before the system had finished maturing.

The paper diary minister offered to draw the preferred route by hand. Geospatial warned that handwritten arrows might create unmanaged local specificity. He compromised by drawing a small cloud and labelling it "weather."

Meanwhile, an aide found the original commissioning note. It said: "We need to know what we are doing next."

The note was praised for its energy and filed under "foundational ambiguity."

## Closing Beat
By evening, the National Directionality Map had been successfully published with one central arrow, two supporting arrows, and a footnote explaining that all arrows should be read together unless read separately.

The Prime Minister said the Map proved government had a clear sense of travel. The Minister for Coordination said it would help departments align around momentum. The Minister for Delivery said he still wanted to know where they were going, but accepted that at least the arrows had stopped arguing.

In the final scene, an official placed the Map in the "Agenda Certainty" folder.

The folder, now visibly strained, rotated itself ninety degrees and declared the Map broadly forward.

**Episode Notes**
- Tone: Satirical but even-handed; the joke stays on vague strategic language, alignment rituals, and the bureaucratic comfort of signalling movement.
- Running theme: Direction becomes something to certify and communicate before anyone settles the destination.
- Continuity hook: The "Agenda Certainty" folder now contains a receipt loop and a direction map that can be rotated into agreement.
