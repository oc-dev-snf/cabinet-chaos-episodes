# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 138
**Title:** The National Evidence Shelf
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the government discovered it had run out of places to put evidence.

This was not because there was too much evidence. That would have been a different meeting, with more scientists and fewer people asking whether "indicative" could be made to sound warmer.

The problem was presentational.

Evidence had been arriving in reports, annexes, dashboards, letters, reviews, submissions, impact notes, pre-impact notes, and one spreadsheet called FINAL_FINAL_USE_THIS_ONE, which everyone agreed was either the latest version or a cry for help.

The Prime Minister asked where the evidence currently lived.

Policy said in the evidence base.

Communications asked whether the evidence base was visible.

Legal asked whether it was discoverable.

Treasury asked whether it had cost control.

Digital asked whether it needed a login.

The Cabinet Secretary said the public needed to feel that evidence was being treated with seriousness, but not so much seriousness that it began making demands.

By 07:08, Strategy had proposed the National Evidence Shelf: a disciplined cross-government storage concept for findings that supported action, delayed action, complicated action, or needed to be acknowledged before action continued unchanged.

The Prime Minister liked the word shelf. It sounded orderly, domestic, and high enough that nobody could reach it without assistance.

## ACT ONE

The first task was agreeing what qualified as evidence.

Health said data.

Transport said modelling.

Education said lived experience.

Treasury said anything with a number in it.

Legal said anything without a number in it might still be material.

Communications said stories counted, provided they were representative.

Policy asked who decided whether a story was representative.

Communications said that depended on whether it helped.

The Cabinet Secretary drew four categories on the whiteboard: Strong Evidence, Emerging Evidence, Contextual Evidence, and Evidence We Respect While Not Being Guided By It.

Treasury objected to Strong Evidence, on the grounds that it could create pressure to do something strong.

Legal objected to Evidence We Respect While Not Being Guided By It, on the grounds that it was accurate.

Strategy suggested "shelf-ready evidence."

Everyone liked this. It implied the evidence had passed through a process, been handled carefully, and was now safely horizontal.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments had begun submitting evidence for shelving.

Housing sent a 400-page review with eleven recommendations and a covering note saying the evidence showed "clear directionality around the need for further engagement."

Transport submitted a map, three forecasts, and a photograph of a station entrance during rain.

Health submitted a waiting list chart so large it had to be reduced to 42 percent, at which point the Cabinet Secretary said it was the first time the waiting list had visibly gone down.

Communications asked whether the shelf would be published.

Legal said a shelf could be published, but contents created complexity.

Digital offered a searchable public evidence shelf.

Treasury asked whether search results could be capped.

Digital asked whether they meant technically capped or democratically capped.

Nobody answered, which answered it.

The Prime Minister asked for a simple line.

Communications drafted: "We are putting evidence at the heart of delivery."

Policy said some evidence was not at the heart of delivery, but slightly adjacent to the lungs of delivery.

Treasury asked whether the heart had recurrent costs.

Legal advised against anatomical metaphors.

The line became: "We are ensuring evidence is properly placed."

The Cabinet Secretary said that sounded like a warning on a ladder.

Strategy said ladders polled well when associated with opportunity.

## ACT THREE

At 12:15, a dispute broke out over shelf height.

Departments wanted their evidence on the top shelf, because top-shelf evidence sounded important. Treasury wanted expensive evidence on the lower shelf, where it could be monitored. Communications wanted emotionally useful evidence at eye level. Legal wanted uncertain evidence behind a transparent door with a disclaimer.

Digital proposed metadata.

The room reacted as if a small gas leak had been detected.

Metadata meant every piece of evidence would need a status, owner, date, confidence level, relevance tag, publication risk, implementation cost, and "narrative compatibility score."

Policy said narrative compatibility was not a recognised evidence standard.

Communications said that was why it needed a score.

A junior analyst asked whether inconvenient evidence still counted.

The Cabinet Secretary said all evidence counted.

Treasury added that counting did not imply weighting.

Legal added that weighting did not imply reliance.

Communications added that reliance did not imply announcement.

The analyst wrote this down, then quietly added it to a folder called "Civic Education."

## CLIMAX

The afternoon briefing was meant to be routine.

The spokesperson announced the National Evidence Shelf as "a new framework for ensuring decisions are informed by the right evidence, in the right place, at the right time."

A reporter asked whether the evidence would change any decisions.

The spokesperson said decisions were always informed by evidence.

The reporter asked whether evidence had ever informed a decision into becoming a different decision.

In the meeting room, Strategy whispered that this was a trap.

Policy said it was a question.

Treasury said questions were often traps with punctuation.

The spokesperson said evidence formed part of a broader decision-making ecosystem.

Another reporter asked whether the shelf contained evidence that contradicted government policy.

Legal said: "Do not say contradict."

Communications said: "Say enrich."

Treasury said: "Say manage."

Policy said: "Say yes, sometimes."

Everyone looked at Policy.

Policy looked surprised to still be employed.

The spokesperson said the shelf allowed the government to manage a rich evidence environment.

Then the public dashboard went live.

It displayed a neat digital shelf with labelled folders: Supporting Evidence, Emerging Evidence, Historical Evidence, Operational Evidence, and a large locked folder titled EVIDENCE REQUIRING EMOTIONAL MATURITY.

The room froze.

Digital said it had been a placeholder.

Communications said it was now a principle.

A reporter asked what was in the locked folder.

The spokesperson looked at the screen, breathed in, and said: "That folder demonstrates our commitment to responsible sequencing."

The reporter asked responsible sequencing of what.

"Understanding," said the spokesperson.

The clip was everywhere within seven minutes.

## TAG

By evening, the National Evidence Shelf had been declared a successful first step in modernising the placement of facts.

Departments were instructed to classify all future evidence as shelf-ready, shelf-adjacent, awaiting shelf space, or temporarily unshelved for reasons of responsible sequencing.

Digital added a warning to the dashboard:

"This evidence may affect previous statements."

Communications changed it to:

"This evidence supports an evolving context."

Treasury changed it to:

"This context remains subject to affordability."

Legal changed it to:

"Context noted."

The Prime Minister approved the final line, provided nobody confused noting something with doing something.

At midnight, the shelf updated itself.

A new folder appeared at the far end.

It was labelled "Evidence That The Shelf Is Full."
