# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 065
**Title:** The Consensus Fire Drill
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

(CABINET OFFICE, 07:00. A shared inbox pings every three seconds. Subject lines: **"FINAL VIEW (MINOR)"**, **"FINAL FINAL VIEW"**, **"FINAL FINAL FINAL"**.)

Morning briefing card:

**RAPID DECISION PROTOCOL — PILOT DAY**  
**Scenario:** *"The Consensus Fire Drill"*

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Why does every urgent decision arrive with seventeen comments and a smiley face?

**PRIVATE SECRETARY:** Because everyone agrees speed matters.

**OPERATIONS DIRECTOR:** They just disagree on what "speed" means.

**POLICY ADVISER:** One team says "today." Another says "after stakeholder comfort checks." Another says "once legal has slept on it."

Beat.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** So we've invented immediate deliberation.

CUT TO TITLE.

## ACT ONE — AGREEMENT AT SCALE

Situation room, 08:14. Whiteboard title: **DECISION WITHOUT COLLISION**

**PM:** We need a decision by lunchtime.

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** With broad support.

**FINANCE LEAD:** And no fiscal surprises.

**SERVICE OWNER:** And no operational surprises.

**LEGAL ADVISER:** And no legal surprises.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Excellent. If we avoid every surprise, we can surprise ourselves with progress.

By 09:02, the room agrees on a process to agree on the process:

1. Collect all concerns in one place, not ten email chains
2. Separate "must-fix" from "nice-to-fix"
3. Timebox comments to 30 minutes
4. Escalate unresolved points to named decision-makers
5. Publish the final call with rationale in plain English

**PM:** Is everyone content?

**ROOM (various):** Broadly / with caveats / in principle.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** We'll put "with caveats" on the national crest.

## ACT TWO — THE CAVEAT ECONOMY

Ops floor, 11:11. Slide heading: **CONSENSUS HEATMAP v3_FINAL_FOR_REAL**

**ANALYST:** We received forty-two comments.

**PM:** How many were blockers?

**ANALYST:** Three.

**PM:** And the rest?

**ANALYST:** Preference, tone, punctuation, and one philosophical objection to bullet points.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Democracy is alive and deeply attached to formatting.

A new filter is introduced:

**RED:** Must change now (legal, safety, delivery-critical)  
**AMBER:** Should change soon (clarity, usability)  
**BLUE:** Can revisit later (style, internal preference)

At 12:03, friction drops immediately.

**REGIONAL LEAD (video call):** Frontline teams don't need perfection. They need stable instructions before shift start.

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** Can we hold the line on version control this afternoon?

**SERVICE OWNER:** Yes, if we stop "one last tweak" at 16:00.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Add it to the constitution.

## ACT THREE — THE MEETING ABOUT FEWER MEETINGS

Cross-government call, 14:26. Forty participants. One agenda item: **"Reduce meeting load"**.

**FACILITATOR:** Irony acknowledged. Let's continue.

Findings from departments:

- Duplicate sign-offs consume most of the delay
- Decisions slow down when ownership is shared but unclear
- Short written briefs outperform long spoken updates
- Teams trust decisions more when dissent is logged, not buried

**PM:** Good. Keep disagreement visible and finite.

News alert appears:

**"Officials adopt tighter decision windows amid pressure for pace."**

**COMMS DIRECTOR:** We can say this is a culture shift.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** Only if the culture actually shifts before tomorrow's inbox.

## ACT FOUR — THE 17:00 LOCK-IN

Decision room, 16:47. New board: **ONE CALL, ONE OWNER, ONE NOTE**

New operating rules go live:

- Decision owner must be named at meeting start
- Unresolved objections logged in a single annex
- Comments close at 17:00 unless risk threshold is met
- Post-decision edits require explicit reopen request
- Every decision note ends with: what changed, who is affected, when it starts

By 18:32:

- Two parallel approvals merged into one forum
- Seven duplicate comments collapsed into one substantive issue
- Final brief issued with a one-page summary for operations
- Next-day teams confirm rollout instructions are clear

**PM:** Are we rushing?

**SERVICE OWNER:** We're deciding.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** In this building, that's considered elite athleticism.

## CLIMAX — THE BRIEFING

Press room, 19:10.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER (at podium):**  
Today we changed how urgent decisions are made. We now distinguish critical issues from preferences, timebox feedback, and publish clear rationales.

This is not about silencing disagreement. It's about resolving it on time and in public record.

**REPORTER:** Did you cut consultation?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** No. We cut duplication.

**REPORTER:** Will this prevent future delays?

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER:** It will prevent avoidable delays. The unavoidable ones will be explained.

## TAG

21:18. Quiet corridor. A junior official posts a new template to the intranet:

**"DECISION NOTE — MAX 1 PAGE"**

Subtitle:

**"If it needs six pages, it needs one decision first."**

A nearby printer, long accustomed to panic, remains silent for a full minute.

**PETER-LIKE MINISTER (passing):** That's the sound of governance maturing.

No applause. Just fewer attachments.

**END**
