# Cabinet Chaos Episode 123: The National Action Word Review

## Cold Open
At 07:00, the Cabinet Office convened an emergency language meeting after a routine press release accidentally promised that the government would "do" something.

The Prime Minister entered carrying the "Agenda Certainty" folder in a reusable tote, because the elastic band had entered resilience leave. Inside the folder sat a marked-up statement with the word "do" circled nine times and one annotation from Legal: "Please specify whether this creates an obligation, expectation, direction, or mood."

Communications said the word had tested well with the public. Policy said it had tested poorly with departments expected to perform it. Treasury said it sounded expensive. Delivery said it sounded refreshing, which made everyone nervous.

The Permanent Secretary announced the National Action Word Review: a rapid cross-government exercise to identify verbs that sounded active while preserving a responsible distance from measurable activity.

## The Briefing
The opening slide read "Action-Oriented Language In A Managed Environment." Beneath it were three columns: "Can Say," "Can Say With Caveat," and "Can Only Say After Spending Review."

"Deliver" was placed in the third column. "Support" went in the first. "Explore" was given privileged status because it implied movement without requiring anyone to leave the building.

The Minister for Coordination asked whether "accelerate" could be used. Transport said only if no speeds were implied. Health said it preferred "improve." Education said "raise standards" remained available, provided no one asked what height. Treasury proposed "enable," which was immediately admired for sounding helpful and impossible to invoice.

By the fifth slide, officials had produced the approved sequence: "recognise, consider, support, enable, refresh, and keep under active review." Delivery asked where the action happened in that sequence. Communications said the public would hear it between "enable" and "refresh."

## The Complication
The review became urgent when departments began submitting their own preferred verbs.

The Department for Implementation requested "mobilise," but only for plans. The Department for Assurance requested "validate," but only after things had already happened. Digital asked for "iterate," which was accepted on the condition that iteration did not imply improvement. Treasury submitted "prioritise within existing envelopes," which was ruled a phrase rather than a verb but allowed to remain because nobody wanted to challenge it.

Then the leaked grid appeared. A red column labelled "Do Not Use" contained "fix," "build," "cut," "decide," and "finish." The press office insisted the list was not a ban, merely a responsible vocabulary boundary.

An adviser suggested replacing "do" with "take forward." Legal asked how far forward. Delivery asked who was taking it. Treasury asked whether forward had a fiscal profile. The phrase was approved as long as it appeared beside "where appropriate."

## The Spin Room
By lunchtime, reporters had the story: "Ministers Review Whether They Can Say They Will Do Things." The spokesperson opened with confidence and a binder.

"This is about clarity," they said. "People expect government language to be active, accurate, and suitably calibrated."

Asked whether the government would still do things, the spokesperson replied that doing remained central to the overall approach. Asked which things, they said the government was taking forward a refreshed action vocabulary to support delivery outcomes. Asked whether that meant yes, they said yes was one of several affirmative options under review.

The press team prepared three lines. For radio: "This is common sense." For television: "This is grip." For online: "No words have been banned." Legal added a fourth line: "Some words have been invited to pause."

## Corridor Conversations
In the corridor, the Chancellor asked whether verbs could be costed in advance. Treasury said strong verbs had strong consequences. "Enable" was cheap. "Guarantee" was ruinous. "Transform" varied depending on whether anyone believed it.

The Minister for Delivery argued that the public might appreciate plain language. A senior official agreed and proposed a plain-language pilot for internal audiences, subject to a readability impact assessment and stakeholder calibration.

The paper diary minister offered "sort it" as a usable phrase. Communications liked the warmth. Legal disliked the implied competence. Policy suggested "help sort it," which everyone accepted as an elegant reduction in exposure.

Meanwhile, an aide discovered the old "Action Tracker" had been renamed the "Forward Motion Register" during a previous review. Nobody could remember whether it tracked actions, motion, or the renaming of trackers. It was added to the Agenda Certainty folder for contextual alignment.

## Closing Beat
By evening, the National Action Word Review published interim guidance. Departments were encouraged to use active language, provided the activity was strategic, proportionate, subject to process, and not accidentally specific.

The Prime Minister recorded a clip saying the government would "get on with the job." Legal approved it after adding a caption: "Job definition pending." Communications called it punchy. Delivery called it progress. Treasury asked whether "get on" required travel approval.

In the final scene, an official updated the master style guide.

Under "Do," they wrote: "Use sparingly. Consider 'help make happen' where operationally safer."

The Agenda Certainty folder rustled, as if preparing to take forward a response.

**Episode Notes**
- Tone: Satirical but even-handed; the joke stays on cautious institutional language and the gap between energetic verbs and real commitments.
- Running theme: The machinery becomes more confident about describing action than taking it.
- Continuity hook: The "Agenda Certainty" folder now contains visibility, directionality, receipts, readiness, and the approved action vocabulary.
