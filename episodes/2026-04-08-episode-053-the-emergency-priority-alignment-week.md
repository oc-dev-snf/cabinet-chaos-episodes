# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 053
**Title:** The Emergency Priority Alignment Week
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

(CABINET DELIVERY SUITE. Three screens show three different banners: “TOP PRIORITY,” “IMMEDIATE PRIORITY,” and “ABSOLUTE PRIORITY.”)

**CALLUM:** Why do we have three top priorities before breakfast?

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** Overnight, each department declared its own emergency.

**COMMS DIRECTOR:** Transport wants “rapid reassurance.” Health wants “visible readiness.” Treasury wants “measured restraint with momentum.”

**SERVICE DIRECTOR:** Which appears to mean: do everything, spend nothing, and announce by noon.

**CALLUM:** Perfect. A strategic triangle of impossible.

**POLICY DIRECTOR:** We can run an Emergency Priority Alignment Week.

**CALLUM:** It’s Wednesday.

**POLICY DIRECTOR:** Then a concentrated week.

CUT TO TITLE.

## ACT ONE

Morning briefing reveals the scale of the issue:

- 147 live “urgent” workstreams across central teams.
- 63 marked “critical” with no owner.
- 29 separate dashboards measuring “delivery confidence,” none using the same definition.

**CALLUM:** We’ve turned urgency into a font choice.

**DELIVERY LEAD:** Proposal: one cross-government priority ladder — P1 to P4, with mandatory owner, deadline, and public impact note.

**CALLUM:** Good. If it has no owner, it has no priority.

**OPERATIONS ADVISER:** Departments will resist anything that demotes their flagship projects.

**CALLUM:** Then they can bring evidence, not adjectives.

Treasury weighs in from a video wall.

**BUDGET OFFICIAL:** Reclassification must not trigger automatic spending commitments.

**CALLUM:** Fine. Priority signals effort and sequencing, not a blank cheque.

## ACT TWO

A pilot “Priority Triage Room” opens at noon.

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** We’ve reviewed 40 urgent submissions.

**CALLUM:** Outcome?

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** 8 true P1s, 19 P2s, 13 politely downgraded to “important but not today.”

**COMMS DIRECTOR:** One submission was urgent because a launch backdrop had the wrong shade of blue.

**CALLUM:** Put it in P4: “aesthetic resilience.”

Tension rises when three departments claim the same policy slot.

**POLICY DIRECTOR:** Everyone wants Friday for announcements.

**CALLUM:** New rule: if your policy depends on explaining another policy, you go second.

**SERVICE DIRECTOR:** Legal asks for caveats on every ladder decision.

**CALLUM:** Add caveats where needed, but plain language only. No paragraph should require a translator and a sandwich.

By evening, teams start adapting.

**DELIVERY LEAD:** Teams are pre-ranking their own work before submission.

**CALLUM:** Excellent. Self-awareness is cheaper than crisis meetings.

## ACT THREE

Comms prepares rollout lines:

- “Clear priorities, faster decisions.”
- “Urgent means urgent — and we can prove it.”
- “Fewer collisions, more delivery.”

**COMMS DIRECTOR:** Should we call this a transformation programme?

**CALLUM:** It’s a queue with rules. Let’s not add fireworks.

Implementation package agreed:

1. Single P1–P4 priority ladder with public criteria.
2. Mandatory owner and due date for every item marked P1 or P2.
3. Weekly triage panel with rotating departmental representation.
4. Collision protocol for competing announcement windows.
5. Shared dashboard tracking age, movement, and completion of priority items.

**OPERATIONS ADVISER:** This will upset people who treat urgency as a negotiation tactic.

**CALLUM:** Then we’re finally measuring the right thing.

## CLIMAX

Press briefing, late afternoon.

**REPORTER:** Isn’t this just central control dressed up as efficiency?

**CALLUM:** It’s shared rules for shared capacity. Everyone can submit priorities. Everyone is judged against the same criteria.

**REPORTER:** Who decides what gets downgraded?

**CALLUM:** A cross-team panel with published decisions and named owners.

**REPORTER:** Are departments being blamed for overusing “urgent”?

**CALLUM:** No. The incentives rewarded noise. We’re changing the incentives, not assigning villains.

## TAG

(NIGHT. DELIVERY SUITE. Dashboard now shows: “Unowned urgent items: 63 → 4.”)

**AIDE:** Requests labelled “critical” dropped 37% in a day.

**CHIEF OF STAFF:** One team resubmitted a P4 as “P1-plus.”

**COMMS DIRECTOR:** Do we have a category for that?

**CALLUM:** Yes. It’s called “nice try.”

A fresh notification appears.

**Subject:** “Emergency request to discuss framework for defining emergency requests.”

Callum stares, creates a P3 task, assigns an owner, and goes to make tea while the system quietly does its job.

**END**
