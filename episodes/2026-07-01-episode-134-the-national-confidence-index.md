# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 134
**Title:** The National Confidence Index
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the government discovered it had misplaced confidence.

Not confidence in anything specific. That would have been measurable, and therefore dangerous. This was national confidence in the broad, atmospheric sense: the sort of confidence that could appear in a speech, survive a headline, and avoid becoming a target by lunchtime.

The Prime Minister asked where confidence normally lived.

Policy said it lived in outcomes. Communications said it lived in tone. Treasury said it lived in forecasts, provided nobody compared them with the last forecasts. Delivery said it would be easier to locate confidence if anyone had recently delivered something.

The room treated this as needlessly literal.

Strategy proposed a National Confidence Index: a simple weekly number showing whether the country felt things were moving in a direction the government could describe as forward.

Legal asked whether an index could fall.

Strategy said only if badly communicated.

Digital asked what data would feed it.

Communications said public sentiment, stakeholder warmth, ministerial steadiness, and "general upwardness."

The Cabinet Secretary wrote down "general upwardness" and underlined it twice, because once made it look accidental.

By 07:13, the government had not found confidence, but it had found a way to announce it was tracking confidence.

This was judged to be almost as good.

## ACT ONE

The first meeting focused on the scale.

Digital suggested a score from zero to one hundred. Treasury objected that numbers below fifty might exist. Communications suggested a traffic light system. Transport objected that traffic lights implied waiting. Health suggested a pulse. Legal objected that a flatline could be discoverable.

The Minister for Public Reassurance proposed a gauge with a needle that never quite reached the red.

"What happens if the public are not confident?" asked the Prime Minister.

"Then the index shows confidence is building," said Communications.

"And if it gets worse?"

"Then confidence is being actively rebuilt."

Policy admired the versatility. Delivery asked whether rebuilding confidence involved doing the thing people wanted confidence in. Treasury said that would create cost exposure.

Digital returned with a prototype. The dashboard showed five categories: "Stable," "Firm," "Strengthening," "Resilient," and "Further Resilient." There was no weak category, because weakness had been reclassified as early-stage resilience.

A junior analyst asked whether the index should include actual polling.

The room went quiet.

The Cabinet Secretary said polling could be helpful, but only after it had been properly interpreted by people who understood the value of stability.

The analyst asked whether that meant changed.

Legal said interpreted was the legally preferred form of changed.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments began competing to influence the index.

Housing claimed confidence was rising because more people had heard the word homes. Education claimed confidence was steady because parents had not yet seen the revised guidance. Transport submitted a chart showing rail reliability improving if the vertical axis began at "surprisingly possible."

Treasury wanted the index to include fiscal confidence, which it defined as the public feeling reassured by spending restraint they had not personally encountered.

Communications wanted the index to include visual confidence: ministers standing near infrastructure, folders, laboratories, maps, and anyone in a high-visibility jacket who looked calmly employed.

Digital warned that mixing all these measures would produce a composite score nobody understood.

"Excellent," said Strategy. "Composite scores are how uncertainty becomes science."

The Prime Minister asked for the current number.

Digital said the model was not ready.

Communications asked for the current direction.

Digital said the model was not ready enough to have direction.

Strategy asked for the current mood of the model.

Digital looked at the loading screen.

"Cautiously animated," they said.

The Cabinet Secretary approved "cautiously animated" as an interim finding.

## ACT THREE

At 12:30, the draft announcement was circulated.

"The government is today launching a National Confidence Index to give the public a clear, transparent account of national momentum."

Legal crossed out "transparent." Treasury crossed out "launching" because launch events had suppliers. Delivery crossed out "momentum" because the last dashboard had caused questions about movement. Communications replaced "public" with "country" because the country could not interrupt a press conference.

The revised line read: "The government is setting out a clear National Confidence Index for the country."

The Prime Minister said it sounded like a thermometer for morale.

Strategy said that was exactly the point, except without illness.

Digital reported a problem. The prototype had pulled in live news headlines, market data, service complaints, and comments from people who appeared to be awake.

The score had dropped to "Developing."

Communications asked whether Developing was above or below Further Resilient.

Digital said it was not on the approved scale.

Treasury asked whether unapproved feelings could be excluded on efficiency grounds.

Legal said feelings could not be excluded, but their relevance could be placed under review.

The Minister for Public Reassurance suggested publishing only the components that were going up.

Policy called this selective confidence.

Communications called it a communications product.

## CLIMAX

The afternoon briefing began with the spokesperson announcing that the country would now have a clearer view of its confidence.

A reporter asked whether the government was confident.

The spokesperson said the government was confident in the process for understanding confidence.

Another reporter asked what the index currently showed.

The spokesperson said it showed a nation moving through a period of strengthening resilience.

A third reporter asked for the number.

Back in the meeting room, Digital said the number had appeared.

It was 47.

Nobody spoke.

Treasury asked whether 47 could be understood as nearly 50. Communications asked whether 50 could be understood as halfway to success rather than halfway from failure. Legal asked whether saying "nearly 50" when the number was 47 created avoidable precision.

Strategy proposed rebasing the index.

Digital asked to what.

"To now," said Strategy.

On the briefing room screen, the dashboard refreshed automatically. The needle moved from "Developing" to "Rebased Confidence: Stronger Than Baseline." The baseline was a dot labelled "Five minutes ago."

A reporter noticed.

"Has confidence improved," she asked, "or have you moved the start line?"

The spokesperson paused.

"The country," they said carefully, "is benefiting from a more modern understanding of beginnings."

In the meeting room, the Cabinet Secretary closed their eyes in the manner of someone trying not to become a quote.

## TAG

By evening, the National Confidence Index had been renamed the National Confidence Framework, because frameworks could contain many numbers without being blamed for any one of them.

Departments were instructed to submit weekly confidence contributions in three categories: demonstrated confidence, anticipated confidence, and confidence not yet visible due to responsible sequencing.

Digital added a setting that prevented the dashboard from displaying any number without a ministerial adjective beside it.

The final shot showed the index after midnight.

The score was still 47.

Next to it, a green label pulsed gently:

"Firmly Emerging."

Underneath, in smaller text, the system displayed a warning:

"Confidence cannot be saved until confidence is confirmed."

Treasury approved the warning, provided it did not imply a funding commitment.
