# Cabinet Chaos Episode 117: The National Clarity Buffer

## Cold Open
At 07:00, the Cabinet Office convened an emergency meeting to decide how much clarity could be safely released before breakfast.

The Prime Minister arrived with the "Agenda Certainty" folder, now carrying a laminated tab marked "for controlled transparency only." Inside was a briefing note, a risk register, and a small envelope labelled "plain English, open only if authorised."

An official explained that the government was not withholding information, merely creating a responsible gap between knowing something and saying it in a way that could be quoted back later.

The room agreed this was prudent, provided nobody described the gap as a delay.

## The Briefing
The morning briefing opened with a slide titled "Clarity Release Options." Option A was to explain the policy clearly. Option B was to explain the process clearly. Option C was to explain that the process for explaining the policy was progressing well.

Communications favoured Option C because it sounded active without creating a measurable endpoint. Policy preferred Option B because it preserved intellectual dignity. Delivery asked whether Option A had been ruled out for technical reasons or cultural ones.

The Minister for Assurance said the public valued openness, but also valued not being surprised by openness before the handling plan was mature. Legal suggested "managed lucidity." The Permanent Secretary asked whether lucidity had been cleared by the propriety team.

By the fourth slide, the proposed line read: "We are committed to setting out further detail at the appropriate point in the detail-setting-out process."

Everyone recognised it immediately as a sentence that had survived several departments.

## The Complication
Trouble began when a junior official accidentally circulated the simple version.

It was one page long, used normal verbs, and contained a sentence beginning "This means." Within six minutes, three directors had marked it urgent. Within nine, someone had opened a new risk column called "public comprehension."

To contain the situation, officials launched the National Clarity Buffer, a temporary safeguard designed to place enough process around plain language that it could no longer move unpredictably.

The buffer had three layers: contextual framing, stakeholder calibration, and a final misting of "where appropriate." Communications said this would allow the government to remain transparent while reducing the chance of people understanding the transparency all at once.

Delivery objected that the simple version answered the main question. Assurance replied that this was exactly why it needed sequencing.

## The Spin Room
By lunchtime, the official line was that ministers were accelerating clarity through a phased communication pathway.

Reporters asked whether that meant the answer was known. The spokesperson said, "We are not going to get ahead of the answer." Asked whether the answer was behind them, the spokesperson said, "The answer is being approached in the proper way."

The phrase "phased clarity" entered the afternoon grid. Ministers used it with increasing confidence, although one accidentally said "clear phases" and had to be gently walked back by an aide carrying the correction folder.

In the spin room, advisers prepared a Q&A pack for the inevitable question "What does this mean?" The approved answer began, "It means the government is focused on meaning."

Legal described this as defensible. Communications described it as calm. Delivery described it as a cry for help in a suit.

## Corridor Conversations
In the corridor, the Chancellor asked whether the National Clarity Buffer had a cost. Finance said the buffer itself was cost-neutral, unless departments began commissioning diagrams to show why one page required twelve slides.

The paper diary minister tried to help by drawing a funnel. At the wide end was "facts." At the narrow end was "agreed public wording." Halfway down, a blockage had been labelled "confidence-building adjectives."

Policy advisers debated whether "clear" was stronger than "straightforward." Legal said "straightforward" implied a route. Communications said "clear" implied visibility. Assurance proposed "accessible," which everybody liked until Delivery asked accessible to whom.

Meanwhile, an aide found the original simple version in the printer tray. It had been annotated by hand: "Excellent, but needs more journey."

## Closing Beat
By evening, the National Clarity Buffer had successfully converted the one-page explanation into a twelve-page note explaining why explanation would follow shortly.

The Prime Minister praised the exercise as proof that the government could be open in a disciplined, proportionate, and appropriately buffered manner. The Minister for Assurance said clarity had been protected from premature exposure. The Minister for Delivery said the public might have coped with the one page, but accepted this was not a risk the system was built to take.

In the final scene, an aide returned the small envelope of plain English to the "Agenda Certainty" folder. On the front, someone had added a new instruction: "Break glass in case of interview."
