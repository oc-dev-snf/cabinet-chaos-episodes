# Cabinet Chaos Episode 120: The National Readiness Receipt

## Cold Open
At 07:00, the Cabinet Office assembled to launch the National Readiness Receipt, a new assurance mechanism designed to prove that government had been prepared before anyone asked whether it was.

The Prime Minister arrived carrying the "Agenda Certainty" folder, now fitted with a small pouch labelled "evidence of preparedness." Inside were three unsigned checklists, a barcode, and a note from Communications saying, "The receipt must confirm readiness without implying purchase."

An official explained that the receipt would not show what had been delivered. That would be too narrow. Instead, it would show that a responsible environment existed in which delivery could be recognised once it had been appropriately evidenced.

The room found this reassuring. A receipt sounded practical. A readiness receipt sounded practical without the complication of a bag.

## The Briefing
The morning briefing opened with a slide titled "Proof Before Proof." It set out the five stages of readiness confirmation: awareness, preparation, pre-assurance, receipt generation, and receipt assurance.

Policy asked whether a receipt could be issued before the thing it receipted had happened. Legal said this depended on whether the receipt was evidential, aspirational, or commemorative. Communications preferred commemorative, because it allowed ministers to sound grateful without being pinned to a date.

The Minister for Coordination said the public wanted confidence. The Minister for Assurance said confidence should be evidenced. The Permanent Secretary proposed "evidenced confidence," which was warmly welcomed until Delivery asked whether confidence could evidence anything other than itself.

By the fourth slide, the agreed line read: "We will provide a readiness receipt confirming that the relevant preparatory conditions have been appropriately acknowledged."

Everyone agreed this had the crisp feel of a sentence that might fit on official paper.

## The Complication
Trouble began when the receipt printer jammed.

For twelve minutes, the centre of government was unable to prove it had been ready to prove it was ready. Communications asked whether the jam could be described as "receipt moderation." Legal asked whether a missing receipt created a presumption of unreadiness. Delivery asked whether anyone could simply do the work while the printer was being fixed.

This was considered brave but outside the receipt pathway.

Facilities located the problem: the printer had run out of approved readiness paper. Ordinary paper was available, but Assurance warned that using it might create a two-tier evidence landscape. Finance asked whether approved readiness paper was a new pressure. Procurement said it was not new, merely previously invisible.

An emergency subgroup recommended a digital receipt. Cyber asked whether a digital receipt could be trusted. Paper Diary said a paper receipt could be trusted because it could be lost in a familiar way.

The meeting paused to respect both formats.

## The Spin Room
By lunchtime, reporters had heard that the readiness receipt was delayed because the receipt system was not ready.

The spokesperson rejected this. "The system is ready for readiness," they said. "What we are doing now is ensuring the proof of readiness meets the standard expected of a mature proof environment." Asked whether a receipt existed, the spokesperson said existence was "being validated through the appropriate channel."

Advisers prepared a Q&A pack for "Where is the receipt?" The approved answers were: "in preparation," "in circulation," and "available once the availability process has concluded."

One minister accidentally told a camera crew that the receipt was "in the till." Markets briefly wondered whether this indicated a fiscal measure. Communications clarified that the minister had been using an operational metaphor and that no till-based policy should be inferred.

The correction succeeded after the phrase "till-based policy" distracted everyone from the original question.

## Corridor Conversations
In the corridor, the Chancellor asked whether receipts could be consolidated at year end. Finance said this was possible, provided departments retained proof that the proof had been retained.

The paper diary minister offered to staple a copy of the readiness receipt into his notebook. An official said this could create a personal archive of national preparedness. He looked pleased until Legal asked whether the notebook would then become infrastructure.

Delivery suggested replacing the receipt with a short public update saying what had been done, what had not been done, and what would happen next. Communications said that was attractive but lacked a barcode. Assurance said the absence of a barcode might undermine confidence among people who liked systems.

Meanwhile, an aide found the original design note in an annex. It said: "Say we are ready if we are ready."

The annex was reclassified as conceptually useful but insufficiently receipted.

## Closing Beat
By evening, the National Readiness Receipt had been successfully issued as a provisional confirmation of pending readiness evidence.

The Prime Minister praised the framework for showing that preparation could be made visible in a disciplined, auditable, and suitably non-committal way. The Minister for Coordination said the receipt would reassure the public that readiness was no longer just a feeling. The Minister for Delivery said the actual thing still needed doing, but accepted that it now had a clearer paper trail.

In the final scene, an official placed the receipt in the "Agenda Certainty" folder.

The folder immediately requested a receipt for the receipt.

**Episode Notes**
- Tone: Satirical but even-handed; the joke stays on proof culture, assurance loops, and the institutional urge to document preparedness before acting.
- Running theme: Readiness becomes something to print, validate, and file rather than a condition for moving.
- Continuity hook: The "Agenda Certainty" folder now includes an evidence pouch that demands receipts for its own certainty machinery.
