# Cabinet Chaos — Episode 094: The National Briefing Room Seating Optimisation Plan

*Date: 2026-05-16*

## COLD OPEN
At 6:58 a.m., officials enter the main briefing room and find every chair relabelled overnight:

**SEATING NOW ALLOCATED BY POLICY INTERDEPENDENCY INDEX.**

No one knows what that means, but everyone immediately pretends they do.

By 7:06, two directors are in a polite standoff over a seat marked “Medium Urgency, High Narrative Risk.”

## ACT ONE — THE LAUNCH
At morning briefing, a senior minister unveils the National Briefing Room Seating Optimisation Plan.

“Proximity drives coordination,” they say. “Coordination drives delivery. Delivery drives confidence. Confidence drives proximity.”

A journalist asks who designed the index.

“An interdepartmental task group,” comes the reply, “supported by behavioural mapping, acoustic modeling, and one very committed intern.”

The official layout has four zones:

1. **Immediate Decisions**
2. **Near-Term Clarifications**
3. **Managed Ambiguity**
4. **Observational Support**

Zone Four is next to the faulty air conditioner.

## ACT TWO — ROLL-OUT
A guidance note explains the system:

- Seats rotate every 23 minutes to prevent “policy silos.”
- Anyone using the phrase “just circling back” moves one row backward.
- Cross-talk requires a form unless both parties are in “Aligned Dialogue Distance.”

The transport team requests an exception for a member with a knee injury.

They are told accessibility is fully supported under Annex 14B, pending spatial certification.

The treasury team brings tape measures and starts independently validating legroom variance.

## ACT THREE — THE LIVE SIMULATION
At noon, teams run a live exercise called *Operation Productive Proximity*.

Facilitator: “If your seat is blue, you own risk. If green, you own momentum. If yellow, you own stakeholder perception.”

Participant: “What if I own none of those?”

Facilitator: “Then you own escalation pathways.”

Another participant asks whether anyone owns decisions.

The facilitator pauses, checks the chart, and says, “Decisions are co-located outcomes.”

The simulation ends early when three departments occupy the same “shared accountability chair.”

## ACT FOUR — PARLIAMENTARY SCRUTINY
In Parliament, opposition members ask if rearranging chairs is now a core public service reform.

A minister responds, “It is an enabling architecture for faster clarity.”

“Has clarity improved?”

“We now know exactly where unclear points are sitting.”

“Has anything been delivered faster?”

“Movement between rows has increased significantly.”

## CLIMAX — THE METRICS MOMENT
At 5:45 p.m., the first dashboard is published:

- **Seat Rotations Completed:** 1,284
- **Cross-Department Interactions:** 642
- **Average Time to Locate Decision Owner:** 47 minutes

Officials call it “a promising baseline for furniture-led governance.”

Then facilities reports the room booking was for a wedding fair rehearsal and all labels must be removed immediately.

An emergency steering group is formed to preserve “lessons learned from dynamic seating.”

## TAG
At 8:10 p.m., one deputy quietly books a small side room with six chairs and no diagram.

They leave with three decisions, two deadlines, and one actual owner per task.

At 8:22, a fresh circular arrives:

**“Phase Two: National Table Shape Harmonisation Pilot.”**
