# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 132
**Title:** The National Alignment Meeting
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the government convened an emergency alignment meeting after discovering that several departments had expressed the same policy in different words.

This was considered dangerous. If the public heard one policy described as a plan, a package, a mission, and a practical next step, they might suspect it had moved through four meetings without becoming one thing.

The Prime Minister asked what alignment meant.

Communications said alignment was when everyone agreed to say the same sentence for different reasons. Policy said it was when different sentences meant the same thing. Legal said it was when nobody had said anything specific enough to be contradicted.

The Cabinet Secretary proposed a National Alignment Meeting: a permanent cross-government forum to align all other forums before they generated momentum.

Delivery asked whether this would create another meeting.

"No," said Strategy. "It creates the conditions for meeting coherence."

By 07:12, the alignment meeting had been adjourned so officials could align on who was meant to be in it.

## ACT ONE

The first agenda item was the agenda.

Treasury wanted spending implications moved above vision. Communications wanted vision moved above reality. Delivery wanted reality included as an annex. Legal asked whether calling it an agenda implied a commitment to order.

A junior official shared a slide titled "Alignment Principles." It contained five circles, four arrows, and a central box labelled "Shared Direction." Nobody knew what direction it was sharing, but the arrows suggested it was doing so confidently.

The Minister for Public Reassurance asked for a simpler version.

Digital produced a dashboard. Each department appeared as a coloured dot moving slowly toward a central target labelled "Narrative Fit." Health was close. Transport was drifting. Education had formed a working group with itself.

The Prime Minister wanted the dots to arrive together.

Digital said the dots were not connected to live data.

"Good," said Communications. "Then they can be accurate."

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, the alignment meeting had spawned three sub-meetings: terminology alignment, expectation alignment, and alignment alignment.

Terminology alignment debated whether "progress" sounded too complete. Expectation alignment debated whether "ambition" sounded too incomplete. Alignment alignment debated whether all three meetings should use the same font, then escalated the matter to Brand Consistency, which had been waiting outside with a lanyard.

Meanwhile, a policy note about housing had been circulated with the phrase "rapidly considered delivery pathway." Delivery objected that nothing rapid had been considered. Treasury objected that pathways sounded expensive. Legal objected that housing existed in the sentence at all.

Communications proposed replacing the line with "We are bringing partners together around outcomes."

Policy asked which partners.

"The aligned ones," said Communications.

The Cabinet Secretary wrote "partners?" on a pad, underlined it twice, and looked as if the question had become a department.

## ACT THREE

At 12:30, the Prime Minister demanded a single aligned message for the afternoon round.

The room produced six candidates.

"We are focused on delivery."

"We are focused on outcomes."

"We are delivering focus."

"We are outcome-focused on delivery."

"We are aligning delivery with the outcomes people expect."

"We are listening."

Everyone disliked "We are listening" because it was too reusable. Treasury liked it because it could be deployed across portfolios without new funding. Legal liked it because listening did not imply hearing.

The Minister for Public Reassurance suggested combining them.

The final draft read: "We are listening to people, focused on delivery, and aligning outcomes with the priorities families expect."

Policy asked whether families expected aligned outcomes.

Communications said they would once the line had landed.

Digital updated the dashboard. The dots had reached the centre, mostly because someone had changed the centre.

## CLIMAX

The afternoon briefing began with the spokesperson announcing that government was now fully aligned.

A reporter asked aligned with what.

The spokesperson said the government was aligned with the public's priorities. Another reporter asked which priorities. The spokesperson said the priorities people had been very clear about. A third asked where they had been clear.

The spokesperson checked the page.

"Across the country," they said.

Back in the meeting room, Communications called this robust. Policy called it spatial. Legal called it survivable.

Then the dashboard appeared on the internal screen with a new warning: "Alignment Exceeded."

Nobody knew what that meant until every department issued the same line at once, including the Environment department, which applied it to river quality, and the Culture department, which applied it to museum opening hours.

For nine minutes, government sounded perfectly consistent and completely context-free.

The Prime Minister asked whether that was bad.

The Cabinet Secretary said it depended whether consistency was the test.

## TAG

By evening, the National Alignment Meeting had been renewed as a standing body with a rotating chair, a fixed agenda, and a commitment to reduce duplication by merging duplicate meetings into a larger meeting about duplication.

Departments were instructed to submit all future lines with an alignment rating, a context tolerance, and a short explanation of how the sentence would remain meaningful if used by another department by mistake.

The final shot showed the dashboard after midnight.

All dots sat in the centre.

Underneath, a small message flashed:

"No distinct positions detected."

The meeting was extended by thirty minutes.
