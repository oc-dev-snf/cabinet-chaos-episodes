# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 036
**Title:** The Policy Pilot Feedback Loop
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

A banner behind the lectern reads: **"LISTENING GOVERNMENT LIVE"**.

A minister smiles into six microphones and announces a new way of making policy: "continuous public feedback at digital speed."

MALCOLM: Digital speed? Last week your website took so long to load I grew a beard waiting for the cookie banner.

GLENN checks his phone.

GLENN: Public feedback has started.

MALCOLM: Good.

GLENN: It's all the same comment.

MALCOLM: What's it say?

GLENN: "Please stop launching portals."

---

## ACT ONE

### 1) The launch

To prove government is responsive, the PM's office unveils the **Policy Pilot App**. Citizens can vote in real time on draft ideas, wording options, and implementation timing.

The options for one pilot transport plan are:
- "Deliver fast"
- "Deliver carefully"
- "Deliver affordably"
- "Deliver all three (beta)"

NICOLA: Why is there a beta option for reality?

MALCOLM: Because nothing says confidence like admitting your infrastructure has patch notes.

### 2) The dashboard room

Staff gather around a giant screen that tracks sentiment as colored arrows.

A civil servant explains the categories:
- Green: support
- Amber: uncertainty
- Red: opposition
- Purple: sarcasm (experimental)

Within minutes, purple dominates every policy area.

GLENN: Is purple bad?

MALCOLM: Purple is the nation saying, "We understand what you're doing, and we still don't trust your spreadsheet."

---

## ACT TWO

### 1) Responsive policy, minute by minute

The minister promises updates every hour based on live input.

At 10:00, the plan is "ambitious but prudent."
At 11:00, it is "prudent but ambitious."
At noon, it is "under review pending user journey alignment."

MALCOLM: You've turned governing into an auto-refreshing terms-and-conditions page.

### 2) The citizen panel stream

A moderated video panel is meant to show constructive dialogue. Participants include a nurse, a bus driver, a small business owner, a student, and a retiree.

Each gives practical, grounded advice: make timelines clear, publish costs plainly, stop changing wording every two hours.

The moderator thanks them and uploads a summary slide titled **"KEY THEME: MORE COMMUNICATIONS."**

NICOLA: They asked for clarity and delivery.

GLENN: We heard "branding consistency."

MALCOLM: That's because this building has a hearing aid that only amplifies PowerPoint.

### 3) The correction cycle

The app introduces a feature called **"Instant Course Correction."**

It triggers so often that teams receive seven contradictory notifications before lunch:
- Proceed immediately
- Pause for consultation
- Proceed with pause
- Consult while proceeding
- Reframe consultation as proceeding
- Await further confidence
- Ignore previous alerts

GLENN: Which one is current?

MALCOLM: The one that arrives after your career ends.

---

## ACT THREE

### 1) Emergency simplification

Senior officials decide to simplify by reducing all choices to two buttons:

1. "Do the thing"
2. "Do not do the thing yet"

By mistake, both buttons route to the same backend action: **"announce pilot extension."**

Press officers prepare statements for all outcomes and accidentally send all of them.

Headlines roll in:
- *"Government Extends Pilot, Ends Pilot, Then Clarifies Pilot Continues"*
- *"Citizens Asked to Give Feedback on Feedback Process"*
- *"New Consultation Opens on Whether Consultations Should Continue"*

### 2) The trust metric

A fresh dashboard is presented as proof of progress:
- **Participation:** record high
- **Comprehension:** mixed
- **Confidence:** "recoverable"
- **Number of explanatory FAQs:** 19

NICOLA: Nineteen FAQs for one pilot?

MALCOLM: If your plan needs its own library, it's not policy. It's a franchise.

---

## CLIMAX

In a final attempt to restore order, the PM's office schedules a "single source of truth" briefing.

The minister steps up, opens the app for a live demo, and asks the audience to choose one definitive path.

The poll returns a tie between:
- "Publish one stable plan and stick to it"
- "Stop polling and start delivering"

The room goes quiet.

MALCOLM, sotto voce: We've crowdsourced common sense and still managed to treat it as optional.

The briefing ends with a promise to "reflect, consolidate, and return with fewer moving parts."

---

## TAG

Next morning, the Policy Pilot App sends one calm push notification:

**"Update: We are piloting fewer pilots."**

For the first time all week, public comments are nearly unanimous:

"Honestly? That's the clearest thing you've said."

**END**
