# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 157
**Title:** The National Decision Rehearsal
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the Prime Minister announced the National Decision Rehearsal.

It was not a decision.

It was a full-scale exercise to ensure that, when a decision eventually appeared, government would recognise it safely.

Communications said the country wanted leadership.

Policy said leadership required options.

Legal said options required caveats.

Treasury said caveats required a cost code.

The Cabinet Secretary convened an urgent meeting to decide whether the rehearsal should include a simulated conclusion.

Communications warned that conclusions could be clipped for broadcast.

Policy suggested using a provisional direction of travel.

Legal said travel implied destination.

Treasury asked whether destination came with maintenance liabilities.

Digital offered to build a rehearsal dashboard showing the readiness of all departments to be nearly decisive.

The junior adviser asked whether rehearsing a decision was different from postponing it.

The Cabinet Secretary said postponement was passive, whereas rehearsal had lanyards.

The Prime Minister said the public deserved a government that could prepare decisively for the moment when decisiveness became appropriate.

Everyone agreed this sounded like leadership, provided it was not written down as a promise.

## ACT ONE

By 08:30, departments had been instructed to nominate a decision for rehearsal.

Transport selected whether to announce a timetable for reviewing timetable announcements.

Health selected whether to simplify a pathway, pending a pathway through simplification.

Education selected whether to consult on the possibility of reducing consultations about consultation fatigue.

The Home Office selected a decision marked "operationally rehearsable".

Treasury selected whether other departments could decide things without first demonstrating decision neutrality.

Communications rejected several nominations because they contained outcomes.

Policy said outcomes were traditional features of decisions.

Communications said tradition polled badly with people waiting for something to happen.

The Cabinet Secretary proposed a standard rehearsal format:

ONE ISSUE.

THREE OPTIONS.

NO PREFERRED OPTION.

ONE IMPRESSION OF PURPOSE.

Legal asked whether an impression of purpose could be judicially reviewed.

Policy said only if it developed edges.

The junior adviser asked how ministers would know when the rehearsal was over.

The Cabinet Secretary said the rehearsal would end when everyone felt more prepared to begin.

Treasury asked whether beginning could be deferred into the next quarter.

## ACT TWO

At 10:00, the Prime Minister rehearsed the public line.

"Today, we are making the tough choices."

Legal objected to making.

Policy objected to choices.

Communications objected to tough, because it suggested the previous ones had been soft.

The revised version became: "Today, we are strengthening the conditions in which choices can responsibly mature."

Digital displayed the dashboard:

DECISION READINESS: DEVELOPING.

OPTION STABILITY: BROADLY CALM.

MINISTERIAL CERTAINTY: UNDER EXERCISE.

PUBLIC ANSWER: IN FORMATION.

Communications liked public answer because it sounded close to accountability.

Legal asked whether in formation meant airborne.

Treasury asked whether anything airborne had been insured.

Meanwhile, one department accidentally selected a preferred option.

The room froze.

Policy asked whether the preference was binding.

Legal asked whether the option knew it was preferred.

Communications asked whether it could be described as a front-running scenario rather than a choice.

The Cabinet Secretary ordered the preferred option to stand with the other options until its confidence reduced.

## ACT THREE

At midday, the National Decision Rehearsal began in the Cabinet Room.

Officials placed folders around the table labelled OPTION A, OPTION B, OPTION C, and OPTION TO BE CONFIRMED.

Communications said the labels gave a strong sense of grip.

Policy said the fourth option provided resilience.

Treasury said the fourth option was cheaper if nobody opened it.

Legal said all folders should face away from the window in case a passer-by inferred direction.

The Prime Minister entered and asked which option was the one everyone secretly wanted.

The Cabinet Secretary said the point of the exercise was to ensure secret wanting remained appropriately structured.

Digital updated the dashboard:

REHEARSAL STATUS: LIVE.

DECISION ATMOSPHERE: FOCUSED.

PREFERENCE LEAKAGE: CONTAINED.

OUTCOME VISIBILITY: RESPONSIBLY LOW.

The junior adviser said the exercise appeared to be a meeting about having a meeting in which a meeting might become a decision.

Communications said that was unfair.

Policy said there would also be a readout.

Treasury asked whether the readout could be oral to avoid storage costs.

## CLIMAX

The afternoon press conference opened with the Prime Minister standing beside a lectern bearing the words READY TO DECIDE.

"Today, we have tested the machinery of decision-making," the Prime Minister said.

A reporter asked what had been decided.

Communications signalled "machinery".

Policy signalled "tested".

Legal signalled "do not identify a lever".

The Prime Minister said: "We have confirmed that the government is fully prepared to approach the point at which decisions can be taken."

Another reporter asked when that point would arrive.

Digital refreshed the dashboard.

It displayed:

POINT OF DECISION: APPROACHABLE.

CHOICE ENVIRONMENT: IMPROVED.

ANSWER PRESSURE: MANAGED.

PUBLIC CLARITY: SCHEDULED FOR REVIEW.

The reporter asked whether this meant no decision had been made.

The Cabinet Secretary explained that a rehearsal was a serious act of institutional discipline, allowing government to make sure a future decision did not take anyone by surprise.

The junior adviser said the government had practised choosing without the inconvenience of choosing.

Communications said practice showed commitment.

The Prime Minister concluded that the National Decision Rehearsal proved the government was ready for the hard work of readiness.

## TAG

By evening, departments were asked to submit lessons learned from the rehearsal.

Most requested a rehearsal of the lessons before learning them.

**END**
