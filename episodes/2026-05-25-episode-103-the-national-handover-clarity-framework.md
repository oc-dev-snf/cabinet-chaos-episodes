# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 103
**Title:** The National Handover Clarity Framework
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

Two deputy directors stand in front of the same desk at 8:59 a.m., each holding a different “final” handover note for the same project.

One says, “I was told this was complete.”

The other says, “I was told completion is a phase, not a state.”

Cut to opening titles.

## ACT ONE

A minister demands answers after three major programmes miss deadlines because nobody can confirm who owns what after staff rotations.

To restore confidence, Cabinet Office launches a cross-government fix: the **National Handover Clarity Framework**.

A taskforce is formed before lunch: **BATON** — the Bureau for Administrative Transition Ownership and Notes.

Communications drafts headlines about “seamless continuity” while policy teams are still searching shared drives titled “Final_FINAL_v7.”

## ACT TWO

BATON introduces a mandatory handover template requiring:
- A summary of decisions made
- A summary of decisions unmade
- A summary of decisions deferred pending summary
- A “confidence slider” from “Mostly Fine” to “Please Escalate Quietly”

Departments submit records at pace.

The dashboard lights up:
- Green: “Named owner confirmed”
- Amber: “Owner known socially”
- Red: “Ownership under active interpretation”

An operations lead notes that 40% of handovers list “Team” as the accountable person. A senior adviser calls this “collective resilience.”

## ACT THREE

A live pilot is staged in one building.

At 9:00 a.m., one team hands over a procurement file.

At 9:02 a.m., legal asks which version is authoritative.

At 9:04 a.m., finance asks who approved line 17.

At 9:06 a.m., everyone agrees to schedule a handover alignment meeting to discuss handover alignment meetings.

By evening, BATON reports “early operational learning” and requests a new subgroup for metadata consistency.

## CLIMAX

At a press briefing, the minister unveils BATON’s four principles:
1. Explicit ownership
2. Traceable decisions
3. Structured transition windows
4. Fewer mystery attachments

A journalist asks whether the framework has prevented any failures yet.

The minister replies, “It has prevented ambiguity about where failures are documented.”

A second journalist asks who leads BATON going forward.

After a pause, an official says, “Interim arrangements are in place pending handover.”
