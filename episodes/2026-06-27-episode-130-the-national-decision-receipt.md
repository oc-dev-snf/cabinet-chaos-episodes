# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 130
**Title:** The National Decision Receipt
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the Cabinet Office discovered that nobody could prove yesterday's decision had definitely happened.

There had been a meeting, a folder, a nod that several people described as "directionally meaningful," and a press line saying the government had acted. But when the Prime Minister asked for the actual decision, the room produced eight versions of the same sentence, none of which contained a verb willing to take responsibility.

The Permanent Secretary announced a solution: the National Decision Receipt, a short official document confirming that a decision had entered the system, passed through recognised ministerial handling, and emerged with enough shape to be mentioned in public.

Treasury liked receipts. They implied something had already been paid for. Legal liked them less. Receipts had dates.

Communications asked whether the receipt could be visual.

"The public trusts rectangles," said Strategy.

## ACT ONE

The first draft of the National Decision Receipt contained a barcode, a reference number, and a box marked "Decision Status."

Policy proposed four statuses: pending, active, implemented, and spiritually implemented. Delivery objected to spiritually implemented, because it sounded like something that had not happened. Communications said that was precisely why it tested well. Legal suggested "implemented in principle." Treasury preferred "implementation-neutral."

The Minister for Coordination asked whether a decision could receive a receipt before the decision had been made. This produced a careful silence, followed by the kind of note-taking people do when a bad idea has arrived wearing an official lanyard.

Digital offered to build a portal where citizens could download receipts for decisions affecting them. Procurement said downloads would require a download strategy. Cyber asked whether receipt numbers might reveal how few decisions existed. Strategy proposed starting the numbering at 10,000.

The Prime Minister liked the sound of 10,000 decisions.

Nobody corrected the phrase quickly enough.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, every department wanted a receipt.

Transport wanted a receipt confirming that a timetable review had been reviewed. Health wanted one confirming that a plan remained in planning. Education requested a receipt for a consultation that had not started, on the grounds that stakeholders would feel reassured by early paperwork. The Environment department asked whether a receipt could be recycled into evidence.

The National Reassurance Dashboard developed a new tab labelled "Receipted Activity." It immediately turned amber, then teal, then a colour nobody could name without consulting brand guidelines. Communications said the colour conveyed momentum. Delivery said it conveyed printer trouble.

In the corridor, the Chancellor asked whether a receipt created fiscal exposure. Treasury replied that it depended whether the receipt acknowledged a decision, a process, or a mood. The Chancellor asked for the cheapest mood.

Meanwhile, Legal found a problem: if the receipt said "decision made," someone might ask what the decision was. If it said "decision logged," someone might ask where. If it said "decision recognised," someone might ask by whom.

Policy suggested "decision-adjacent activity acknowledged."

The room relaxed.

## ACT THREE

At lunchtime, Communications prepared the public launch.

The lectern sign read "Decisions You Can See." Legal crossed out "decisions." Treasury crossed out "can." Strategy replaced the whole thing with "A Clearer Record Of Responsible Movement."

The spokesperson rehearsed the key line: "The National Decision Receipt will give people confidence that government action is being tracked with clarity, discipline, and proper administrative seriousness."

A junior official asked whether there would be an example receipt. This caused immediate panic, because an example receipt needed an example decision, and the example decision risked becoming the only decision anyone could understand.

Delivery proposed using "approve the receipt format" as the example decision. Legal said that created a circularity risk. Digital said circularity could be represented as a loading spinner. Communications said loading spinners were honest but unpopular.

The Prime Minister asked for one simple thing the receipt would prove.

The Permanent Secretary said it would prove that the government had a system for proving things.

Everyone agreed this was strong enough for broadcast.

## CLIMAX

The launch began with a receipt projected behind the spokesperson. It showed a reference number, a timestamp, and the words "Outcome: Under Confirmation."

A reporter asked whether the new system meant the government had made more decisions. The spokesperson said it meant decisions would now be more decision-visible. Another reporter asked whether the receipt was a substitute for action. The spokesperson said action remained the preferred long-term expression of action.

Asked when the first real receipt would be issued, the spokesperson explained that the receipt process itself had now been receipted, pending final receipt validation.

This was not the line Communications had written, but it had the advantage of being true in three separate systems.

Back in the Cabinet Room, the dashboard printed a test receipt. The printer jammed at "Accountability:". Procurement opened an incident log. Digital blamed legacy drivers. Treasury asked whether the jam meant accountability had been deferred.

The Minister for Delivery pulled the paper free. It tore neatly between "Decision" and "Receipt."

For a moment, nobody spoke.

Then Strategy said the tear represented healthy separation of functions.

## TAG

By evening, departments were instructed that all major announcements must include a Decision Receipt, a receipt status, and a plain-English explanation of why the plain-English explanation would follow.

The Prime Minister recorded a statement promising that the public would always know when a decision had entered the appropriate recognition environment. Communications liked "always." Legal added "where appropriate." Treasury added "subject to affordability." Digital added a QR code that led to a holding page.

In the final shot, the National Certainty Window opened just wide enough for the first Decision Receipt to slide through.

It landed face down.

The dashboard marked it "Progress: Evidenced."
