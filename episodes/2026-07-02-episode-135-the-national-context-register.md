# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 135
**Title:** The National Context Register
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the government discovered it was short of context.

Not facts. Facts were plentiful, inconvenient, and already stored in several formats nobody could open without a procurement exception. What the morning needed was context: the soft material that could be placed around a fact until it stopped looking like a headline.

The Prime Minister asked how much context was normally required.

Policy said it depended on the fact. Communications said it depended on the journalist. Treasury said context should be delivered within existing departmental allocations. Legal said context was useful, provided it did not imply anyone had known the fact before breakfast.

The Cabinet Secretary asked whether the context existed.

Strategy said it existed in principle.

Digital asked where.

Strategy gestured at the room.

By 07:08, a proposal had emerged for a National Context Register: a single cross-government inventory of background, framing, clarifications, atmospherics, and important surrounding information that could be deployed whenever reality arrived without an appointment.

The Prime Minister liked the word register. It sounded orderly. It also sounded like something that could be announced before it worked.

Communications asked whether the register would help explain why yesterday's announcement had contradicted last week's assurance.

Policy said contradiction was a strong word.

Legal said it was also an accurate word, which made it a high-risk word.

Strategy suggested "sequentially contextualised."

Everyone wrote that down.

## ACT ONE

The first problem was categorisation.

Digital proposed five context types: operational, historical, fiscal, stakeholder, and weather-related. Transport immediately requested a sixth category for things that were technically operational but felt like weather. Health asked for a clinical context category. Education asked whether "term-time complexity" counted as a context or a shield.

Treasury wanted a field labelled "cost-neutral context."

Policy asked whether any context could be cost-neutral.

Treasury said it could if it was already in a minister's head.

Communications circulated a draft interface. Each incident would have a dropdown menu titled "Primary Context." The options were "longstanding challenge," "global headwinds," "local delivery variation," "legacy friction," "implementation learning," and "unhelpful timing."

The Prime Minister asked which one applied to the morning's problem.

Communications asked which morning problem.

The room paused, because there were now three.

Digital suggested tagging them separately.

Strategy objected that separate tags created separate stories.

The Cabinet Secretary ruled that all three problems would be grouped under one broad context called "the wider picture."

Legal approved this on the basis that no one had ever successfully subpoenaed a wider picture.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments had begun uploading context.

Housing submitted "historic supply pressures," "market sensitivity," and "the calendar." Transport submitted "passenger experience volatility," "rolling stock reality," and "the calendar, but with points failure." Education submitted "classroom-facing complexity," which nobody understood but everyone admired.

The register quickly became full.

Digital explained that it was not technically full, but every field had been used to mean something different.

Communications asked whether that was a problem.

Digital said the search function now returned "ministerial confidence" for every query, including "bridge closure," "school roof," and "where are the minutes."

Strategy called this encouraging semantic alignment.

A junior official asked whether the register should include context that made the government look bad.

The Cabinet Secretary looked at the junior official with professional sadness.

"It should include the existence of that context," he said.

"But not the context itself?"

"Only where necessary."

"Who decides necessary?"

Legal said necessity was best assessed by the person least likely to be asked about it on camera.

The junior official wrote that down as governance.

## ACT THREE

At 12:30, the Prime Minister rehearsed the line for the lobby.

"The public deserve the full context."

Communications said "full" was brave.

"The public deserve proper context."

Legal said "proper" invited improper.

"The public deserve context."

Treasury asked whether deserve created an entitlement.

The final line became: "The government is making context available where appropriate."

The Prime Minister said it sounded like a museum label for a locked cupboard.

Strategy said locked cupboards were often where the most valuable context lived.

Digital entered with an update. The prototype had automatically matched a difficult statistic with the wrong context note. A graph showing service delays had been paired with "seasonal optimism," and a briefing on emergency preparedness had been tagged "low visibility celebration."

Communications asked whether the public could see the tags.

Digital said not yet.

"Then the system is working," said Communications.

Policy warned that the Opposition might demand access to the register.

Legal said they could be offered a summary of the categories.

The Prime Minister asked whether that would satisfy them.

Nobody answered, which was taken as a form of context.

## CLIMAX

The afternoon briefing began with the spokesperson announcing a new era of contextual transparency.

A reporter asked whether contextual transparency meant transparency.

The spokesperson said it meant transparency with appropriate context.

Another reporter asked to see the context.

The spokesperson said the context was being actively registered.

A third reporter asked whether the government had created a database of excuses.

In the meeting room, Communications inhaled sharply.

Legal said "excuses" was defamatory against databases.

Strategy proposed "explanatory readiness assets."

The spokesperson, receiving this in an earpiece, said the register would ensure ministers had access to explanatory readiness assets at pace.

The reporter asked whether that was yes.

Digital noticed the public dashboard had gone live.

On the screen behind the spokesperson, the National Context Register appeared with one entry visible:

**Incident:** Unspecified pressure.
**Primary Context:** Unhelpful timing.
**Secondary Context:** The wider picture.
**Public Line:** Context pending.
**Internal Note:** Do not say register.

The room froze.

The spokesperson turned, read the screen, and smiled with the calm of someone whose career had just become a case study.

"As you can see," she said, "we are being unusually open about the process by which openness is prepared."

The Cabinet Secretary closed the laptop.

The projector stayed on.

## TAG

By evening, the National Context Register had been renamed the Context Availability Programme, because programmes could be piloted indefinitely and registers sounded searchable.

Departments were instructed to submit context in three classifications: immediate context, fuller context, and context that would become clearer after the decision it was meant to explain.

Digital added a warning to the upload page:

"Context cannot be published until context has been contextualised."

The Prime Minister approved the revised briefing line:

"We understand the public want answers. That is why we are investing in the conditions in which answers can be understood."

Communications called it steady.

Treasury called it inexpensive.

Legal called it survivable.

The final shot showed the register at midnight, quietly creating a new entry for itself.

**Incident:** Context Register visible.
**Primary Context:** Implementation learning.
**Secondary Context:** Unhelpful timing.
**Public Line:** The system demonstrated transparency ahead of schedule.

Underneath, in smaller text, the system asked:

"Would you like to add more context?"

Everyone clicked yes.
