# THE THICK OF ITCH

**Episode:** 146
**Title:** The National Readout Window
**Genre:** Original political satire

---

## COLD OPEN

At 07:00, the government discovered it had held a successful meeting that nobody could describe.

The Prime Minister wanted a readout.

Policy had notes.

Communications had adjectives.

Legal had concerns about verbs.

Treasury had underlined the word "successful" and written "define."

The Cabinet Secretary said the problem was not the meeting. The problem was the uncontrolled gap between a meeting happening and the public being told what kind of happening it had been.

The junior adviser asked whether a readout was supposed to contain what was read.

The room considered this needlessly literal.

By 07:16, Communications proposed the National Readout Window: a protected daily slot in which every department could release a carefully aligned account of productive engagement before reality started asking for receipts.

The Prime Minister liked it.

It sounded transparent, timed, and impossible to check before lunch.

## ACT ONE

The first argument was over the size of the window.

Communications wanted thirty minutes.

Policy wanted enough time to mention stakeholders.

Legal wanted no time at all, ideally in writing.

Treasury asked whether windows needed cleaning.

Digital said the window could be virtual.

Facilities said the building already had windows.

The Cabinet Secretary said physical windows implied openness, which was operationally risky.

Communications drafted the core line:

TODAY'S DISCUSSIONS WERE CONSTRUCTIVE, FOCUSED, AND FORWARD-LEANING.

Policy said "constructive" suggested something had been built.

Legal changed it to "constructively framed."

Treasury asked whether "forward-leaning" created a liability if the policy later fell over.

The Prime Minister asked for something warmer.

Communications added "with families at its heart."

Nobody knew which families.

This was considered useful flexibility.

## ACT TWO

By mid-morning, departments were asked to submit items for the first readout.

Transport submitted "positive movement."

Health submitted "renewed focus."

Education submitted "a shared sense of direction."

The Home Office submitted "robust listening."

Legal asked whether listening could be robust without admitting prior softness.

The Home Office replied that the listening had always been robust but was now more visibly robust.

Treasury submitted "no additional cost at this stage."

Communications said that sounded like a hostage note from a spreadsheet.

The Cabinet Secretary created three approved readout categories:

ENGAGEMENT HELD.

PROGRESS NOTED.

NEXT STEPS ANTICIPATED.

The junior adviser asked whether "next steps anticipated" meant there were next steps.

Policy said it meant there was an anticipation environment.

Digital built a dashboard with a countdown clock to the window.

At 10:42, it accidentally went live.

For eight minutes, the public could see a draft readout that said:

MEETING OCCURRED. OUTCOME TO BE INVENTED RESPONSIBLY.

Communications called an emergency alignment huddle.

Legal called it pre-decisional candour.

Treasury asked if accidental honesty could be reused.

## ACT THREE

At noon, the Prime Minister rehearsed the statement.

"This morning, I convened a focused discussion on the issues that matter most," the Prime Minister said.

Policy nodded.

Communications nodded harder.

Legal asked which issues.

The Prime Minister said, "The important ones."

Legal wrote down "important ones" and circled it as a risk.

The Cabinet Secretary suggested "priorities facing the country."

Treasury asked whether facing a priority required travel time.

Digital refreshed the dashboard. The window now displayed a green status:

READOUT READY, SUBJECT TO MEETING CONTENT.

The Prime Minister asked if that was good.

Communications said it showed discipline.

The junior adviser said it showed the statement did not know what the meeting was about.

The Cabinet Secretary said that was precisely why it was a window and not a door.

At 12:30, a department attempted to upload a readout from a meeting scheduled for tomorrow.

Policy said this demonstrated ambition.

Legal said it demonstrated time travel.

Communications called it "proactive transparency."

Treasury asked whether tomorrow's meeting could be cancelled now that its readout was complete.

For a brief moment, the room looked genuinely tempted.

## CLIMAX

The first National Readout Window opened at 15:00.

Every department released a statement at once.

For seven seconds, the system looked coordinated.

Then the statements began contradicting each other.

Transport said delivery was accelerating.

Treasury said spending was controlled.

Policy said options remained open.

Communications said decisions had been taken.

Legal said no decisions should be inferred.

The Prime Minister's statement said the government was moving at pace while taking the time to get things right.

A reporter asked which one it was.

The Prime Minister said, "Both, in the proper sequence."

The reporter asked what the sequence was.

Communications signalled "families."

Policy signalled "delivery."

Legal signalled "not legally a sequence."

The Prime Minister said: "Families know that delivery sometimes requires a window."

Behind them, Digital's dashboard tried to reconcile the readouts and produced a single national summary:

THE GOVERNMENT HAS MET ABOUT MEETING AND WILL UPDATE THE UPDATE WHEN UPDATED.

The room fell silent.

The Cabinet Secretary said it was inelegant but not inaccurate.

Treasury asked whether it could be shorter.

Communications called it punchy.

The junior adviser said it sounded like a cry for help.

The Prime Minister approved it as a holding line.

## TAG

By evening, the National Readout Window had been hailed as a major step forward in coordinated clarity.

Departments were instructed to prepare tomorrow's readouts by yesterday.

Digital added a button marked ALIGN AGAIN.

Legal asked for the button to be renamed REVIEW WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

Treasury asked whether pressing it cost anything.

The Cabinet Secretary scheduled a meeting to discuss how the meeting about the readout had been read out.

The Prime Minister asked for a summary.

Communications said the window had closed.
