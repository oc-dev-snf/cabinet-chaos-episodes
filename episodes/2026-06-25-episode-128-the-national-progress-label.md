# Cabinet Chaos Episode 128: The National Progress Label

## Cold Open
At 07:00, the Cabinet Office announced the National Progress Label, a simple mark that could be attached to any government activity to show that progress was associated with it.

The Prime Minister arrived with the Agenda Certainty folder, the readiness receipt, and yesterday's dashboard printout, which had been laminated overnight to preserve confidence. Communications said the public wanted to know what was moving. Delivery said some things were moving. Treasury asked whether movement implied momentum, because momentum had historically led to costs.

The Permanent Secretary explained that the label would not claim completion. It would merely indicate that the item was within the recognised vicinity of progress.

Everyone found "vicinity" helpful. It sounded close without being legally adjacent.

## The Briefing
The opening slide read "Progress, Clearly Indicated." Beneath it were three proposed labels: "Progress Under Way," "Progress In View," and "Progress Being Prepared."

Communications liked "Under Way" because it sounded active. Legal worried that under way suggested a direction. Policy preferred "In View," which allowed for distance. Delivery asked whether "Being Prepared" meant the thing was happening or the preparation was happening. Strategy said the distinction would matter only to specialists, stakeholders, journalists, and the public.

The Minister for Coordination asked whether departments could apply the label themselves. The room went quiet. Self-certification sounded efficient, which made Treasury suspicious, and optimistic, which made Legal sit forward.

Digital proposed a central portal where departments could request a label, track the label request, appeal a label deferral, and download a badge confirming that the label request was progressing. Procurement said the portal could begin with a discovery phase into whether labels should be rectangular.

The emergency affordability bell rang once when someone said "badge ecosystem."

## The Complication
The trouble began when Health asked to label a consultation as "Progress Under Way" before it had opened, on the grounds that the opening process had begun.

Education wanted to label a review as "Progress In View" because the review had located the issue. Transport wanted "Progress Being Prepared" for a plan to improve timetable certainty, although the timetable for the timetable certainty plan remained uncertain. Digital asked whether the dashboard itself qualified for a label. Communications said it absolutely did, because public confidence improved when visible things certified one another.

By mid-morning, the label scheme had produced a label hierarchy. Gold meant progress was happening. Silver meant progress was intended. Blue meant progress had been discussed in a room with minutes. Grey meant the matter had not gone backwards in a formally recorded way.

Treasury liked grey.

Delivery objected that a progress label without measurable change risked becoming decorative governance. Policy said decorative governance had a role when people needed to see the shape of activity before the substance was ready. Legal asked whether the substance had been consulted. Communications suggested not using the word substance until the label family had settled.

## The Spin Room
At lunchtime, the spokesperson stood beside a podium bearing a temporary sticker reading "Progress Label: Pending Final Artwork."

"This government is making progress easier to identify," they said. "People will be able to see at a glance where work is advancing."

A reporter asked whether the label showed actual progress. The spokesperson said it showed that progress was being treated seriously. Asked whether any failed policy could receive a label, they said every label would go through appropriate assurance. Asked what assurance meant, they said it meant the public could be assured that assurance had occurred.

Another reporter asked whether the label was just a sticker. Communications had prepared for this.

"It is not just a sticker," the spokesperson said. "It is a structured visual commitment to transparent momentum."

Within minutes, the phrase was online as a quiz: "Sticker Or Structured Visual Commitment?" The Cabinet Office scored four out of ten and asked for a retake.

## Corridor Conversations
In the corridor, the Chancellor asked whether the National Progress Label could be applied to savings. Treasury said yes, provided the savings were in view and not yet dependent on the expensive part of implementation.

The Minister for Delivery carried a draft list of things that had actually improved. Communications admired the list but said it was visually untidy. Strategy suggested translating it into a progress narrative. The minister asked whether the narrative could include numbers. Policy said numbers were powerful but should be introduced slowly, like strong coffee in a nervous meeting.

The paper diary minister proposed a stamp. A real stamp, with ink. Digital said a stamp would not integrate with the portal. The minister replied that a stamp had excellent uptime. Procurement said ink supply would need a framework.

Meanwhile, an official discovered that one department had applied the blue label to a meeting about whether to request the grey label. Legal said this was technically coherent. Delivery said it was spiritually bleak. Communications said spiritually bleak could be repositioned as "candid about complexity."

## Closing Beat
By evening, the National Progress Label entered controlled rollout. Departments were invited to submit no more than three candidates each, unless they had a strong case for four, or a weak case with senior sponsorship.

The Prime Minister recorded a statement saying the label would help people see where progress was being made. Communications liked "see." Treasury liked that seeing was cheaper than doing. Legal approved the statement after adding that the presence of a progress label did not guarantee speed, direction, completion, satisfaction, transformation, improvement, or progress as ordinarily understood.

In the final scene, the National Reassurance Dashboard refreshed and displayed a new indicator: "Progress Label Adoption."

It was grey.

Treasury called it a good start.

- Tone: Satirical but even-handed; the joke stays on process theatre, certification habits, and the comfort of visible labels.
- Running theme: Progress becomes something to tag, approve, and display before anyone agrees what changed.
- Continuity hook: The Agenda Certainty folder, readiness receipt, National Reassurance Dashboard, and emergency affordability bell now feed into the National Progress Label.
