# Cabinet Chaos — Episode 095: The National Stationery Readiness Framework

*Date: 2026-05-17*

## COLD OPEN
At 7:01 a.m., a red banner appears on every office printer:

**STATIONERY STATUS: AMBER (PAPER CONFIDENCE BELOW TARGET).**

No one remembers approving a paper confidence target, but everyone agrees it sounds prudent.

By 7:09, three teams are arguing over whether sticky notes count as “temporary governance instruments.”

## ACT ONE — THE LAUNCH
At the morning podium, a senior official announces the National Stationery Readiness Framework.

“Public service delivery depends on writing things down,” they say. “Writing things down depends on stationery. Therefore stationery is now strategic infrastructure.”

A reporter asks when this became urgent.

“Since we discovered nineteen different pen standards across government,” comes the reply.

The framework introduces four readiness levels:

1. **Green — Operational Scribble Capacity**
2. **Yellow — Managed Annotation Pressure**
3. **Amber — Escalating Margin Risk**
4. **Red — Uncontained Administrative Smudging**

Level Red triggers mandatory pencil-sharing protocols.

## ACT TWO — ROLL-OUT
Implementation guidance is circulated in a 43-page document titled *Ink, Intent, and Institutional Continuity*.

Key rules include:

- Blue pens for decisions, black pens for context, purple pens for “trajectory.”
- Any meeting exceeding 25 minutes must appoint a Certified Page Turner.
- Highlighters require pre-clearance if used near legal text.

One department requests emergency marker supplies.

They are told emergency markers are available only through the Reserve Visibility Stockpile, held off-site pending shelf-labelling harmonisation.

Another department pilots “digital-first handwriting” by writing notes on tablets and printing them for filing.

## ACT THREE — THE LIVE EXERCISE
At noon, officials run *Exercise Steady Hand*.

Facilitator: “You have seven minutes to produce a one-page action plan using only approved stationery tiers.”

Participant: “Can we borrow a stapler?”

Facilitator: “Only if your risk owner signs Form S-12.”

A second participant asks what happens if the risk owner’s pen runs out.

Facilitator: “That is a resilience scenario. Please log it as a near-miss.”

The exercise pauses when two teams submit identical plans because both copied the sample template header and forgot to add content.

## ACT FOUR — PARLIAMENTARY SCRUTINY
In Parliament, members ask whether the government is “governing by office supply.”

A minister replies, “This is about preparedness, consistency, and legible delivery.”

“Have outcomes improved?”

“Illegible signatures are down 38%.”

“Have services improved?”

“We now have clearer labels on filing trays.”

## CLIMAX — THE METRICS MOMENT
At 6:02 p.m., the first dashboard goes live:

- **Pens Audited:** 12,404
- **Staples Accounted For:** 1,902,118
- **Average Time to Find a Working Hole Punch:** 34 minutes

Officials call it “an encouraging first signal of material readiness.”

Then facilities sends a correction: the main count included items from a school supply donation drive in the same warehouse.

An urgent cross-team review is launched to separate “public stationery assets” from “community generosity inventory.”

## TAG
At 8:27 p.m., one quietly practical team meets with plain notebooks, one shared checklist, and no colour-coded legend.

They leave with four decisions, two owners, and a delivery date.

At 8:41, a new memo lands:

**“Phase Two: National Binder Spine Alignment Programme.”**
